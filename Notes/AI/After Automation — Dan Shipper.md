# After Automation

Dan Shipper's argument: AI progress creates more work for humans, not less. The more we automate, the more expert human judgment becomes necessary.

## The Paradox

Every has automated everything they can (Codex, Claude Code, customer service, writing). They're a team of ~30. They haven't fired anyone. There's more human work than ever.

> We don't write code by hand anymore. If you @-mention someone in our Slack, it's a toss-up whether you're talking to a human or an agent.

AI responds to 95% of Shipper's work emails. He's at inbox zero. He still reviews every one.

## Two Modes of Working with Agents

**Agent employees** — async delegation. Tag them in Slack, they go off and produce output. Two flavors:
- *Coworker agents* (Claudie for consulting, Andy for editorial, Viktor for org-wide research)
- *Embedded agents* (Fin in customer service: handled 65% of support conversations, closed 40% without humans)

**Human-agent collaboration** — shared operating systems (Codex, Claude Code, Cowork). Not handoff. You and multiple agents working in the same workspace on complex, original work.

> Kieran calls this the human "sandwich" — we're the bread on either end of the AI's work.

## The Cycle

See [[Slop and Sameness]] for the full breakdown. Four steps:

1. **AI commoditizes human expertise.** Models trained on the visible residue of completed tasks. Rare skills become cheap.
2. **Cheap competence gets adopted everywhere.** Operations people issue PRs. Marketers make thumbnails. OpenClaw: 44,469 PRs as of May 2026.
3. **Abundance creates sameness.** Everyone uses the same models. Output ranges from "decent start" to "plain slop."
4. **Sameness creates demand for difference.** What doesn't fit the pattern becomes rare and valuable. Demand for difference is demand for human experts.

> Once a situation has been reduced to text, once it has become corpus, it is a corpse. Humans are alive to a specific moment, customer, codebase, or conversation in a way the training corpus isn't yet.

## Zeno's Paradox of AI

See [[Zeno's Paradox of AI]] for the full analysis. The gap between humans and AI keeps regenerating because [[Frames vs Framers|the frame is not the framer]].

Every benchmark measures work inside a frame we supplied. Models hill-climb that frame. When they saturate it, we shift the frame. Scores reset. The cycle repeats.

Shipper's Senior Engineer benchmark: GPT-5.5 scores 62/100 on a codebase rewrite. Human senior engineers score 80s-90s. Models will hit 90s within a year. Then the frame shifts to "can you decide *whether* to rewrite?"

> The score tells us how well the model operates inside a frame we supplied; it does not tell us that the model has become us. That is the category error underneath the panic.

## Agents Without Agency

See [[Agents Without Agency]] for the full argument. Two definitions getting mixed up:
- **Agency** = ability to act independently, for oneself
- **Agent** = something that acts on behalf of another

AI is purely the second. The toddler comparison: toddlers can't write code or pass exams, but they have ends. They want things. They invent games. They're not waiting for a prompt.

> The toddler is alive inside a field of desire, attention, frustration, delight, fear, imitation, and play.

## The Rabbi Story (Coda)

From Martin Buber's *The Way of Man*: A man so stupid he can't find his clothes. He writes down where everything is. Next morning he follows the list perfectly — cap on head, pants on legs — then asks in horror: "Where in the world am I?"

You can automate every task and still lose the thing that makes you *you*.

## Key Takeaways

- More automation → more expert work, not less
- AI commoditizes yesterday's competence, creating demand for today's judgment
- [[Frames vs Framers|The frame is not the framer]] — benchmarks measure frames, not us
- [[Agents Without Agency|Agents have autonomy but not agency]] — they're means to human ends
- The structurally safe position: use AI to address today's problems as you see them
- [[The Allocation Economy]] was the first-order prediction; this essay is the second-order consequence
