# The Allocation Economy

Dan Shipper's 2023 thesis: working with AI tools would eventually look like the work of human managers. Written when basic ChatGPT prompts were still considered futuristic.

## Core Idea

Knowledge work shifts from *doing* to *allocating*. Instead of writing code, drafting emails, or building spreadsheets yourself, you direct AI agents to do it. Your job becomes deciding what needs doing, pointing agents at the right problem, and judging whether the output is good.

This predicted what actually happened at Every by 2026: managers committing code like ICs, engineers talking directly to customers, AI handling 95% of emails.

## Why It Matters

The allocation framing reframes "AI taking jobs" as "jobs changing shape." The skills that matter shift:
- **Execution** → **Judgment** (is this output good?)
- **Craft** → **Direction** (what should we build?)
- **Specialization** → **Coordination** (how do these pieces fit together?)

## Connection to [[After Automation — Dan Shipper|After Automation]]

Shipper revisits and extends this in 2026. The allocation economy was the first-order prediction. The second-order insight: allocation itself creates more work, because every agent output needs human review, and cheap competence floods the system with work that needs expert judgment.
