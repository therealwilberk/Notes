# How to Philosophize with a Hammer

## Nietzsche's Warning, Peterson's Mirror, and the Question Nobody Wants to Answer

Nietzsche said of his own books: "I write in a single sentence what it takes other men a book to write." Then he said, "What other men can't even write in a book." Which is pretty good, eh. A hell of an egotistical statement right off the bat, and instantly he tops it with an even more egotistical statement. Except it wasn't egotistical. Because it happened to be true.

That's the thing about Nietzsche. He sounds like he's boasting until you sit down with *Beyond Good and Evil* and realize you've earmarked both sides of every page. Not because you're thorough. Because there's that much packed into it. Whole fields of endeavor live inside a single paragraph. Psychoanalysis. Moral philosophy. Political prophecy. All of it compressed into these aphorisms that hit like hammer blows.

And that's what it means to philosophize with a hammer. Not to destroy for the sake of destruction. To go down into the foundations, into the things you take for granted, the axiomatic presuppositions that orient you in the world, that make you free of anxiety, that unite you with other people, that give you direction and purpose. And to tap on them. To find the weak spots. And if the whole structure comes crumbling down, well. That's part of the process.

---

We have this intuition of depth. All of us know there's a difference between something that's shallow and something that's deep. And if something inside you that's shallow moves, it doesn't move much of you. But if something deep is moved, it moves a lot of you at the same time. Nietzsche philosophized in the depths. And that's why reading him is daunting psychologically. Not because he's difficult to understand, although he can be. Because he's dangerous. He goes after the things that stabilize you. And he does it with a kind of joyful ruthlessness that's almost unbearable.

It was Nietzsche who pronounced that God was dead. That we have killed him. That we'll never find enough water to wash away the blood. And people read that as a triumph. The great atheist, celebrating the death of the old tyranny. But that's not what it was. It wasn't a triumphalist statement. It was a warning. A prophecy. And it came true.

Nietzsche knew that the death of God would bring two things. First, a universal nihilism: a rootlessness, a hopelessness that would pervade Western culture and result in an intense, pervasive demoralization. And second, the rise of totalitarian ideologies that would rush in to fill the vacuum. He was particularly concerned about anti-Semitism, which was already stirring. But more importantly, he prophesied that huge swaths of the Western world would become enticed by the blandishments of something like a utopian communism. And that the consequence would be the death of tens of millions of people.

That's a multi-dimensional prophecy. And all of those things transpired. Every single one. In the 20th century. The century of the camps and the gulags and the killing fields. All of it foreseeable to a man who was half-blind, sick to his stomach, and writing in bursts because his body wouldn't let him do more.

---

Here's what people miss about Nietzsche. He wasn't just tearing things down. He was asking the hardest question a human being can ask: once the old foundations are gone, what do you build on? And he proposed something. The overman. The *Übermensch*. A new kind of person who would be capable of creating his own values. Who could stand in the rubble of the old order and forge meaning from nothing.

And that sounds magnificent. Heroic, even. Until you start to think about it seriously. Because the psychoanalysts, Freud first among them, showed that it's not obvious at all that you're the master of your own house. For Freud, you were essentially a collection of loose biological inclinations. Id-driven impulses. Lust, aggression, the impulse towards death. Your primordial gods, instantiated at a biological level. And you're their plaything. Their toy. It isn't obvious that you can be the master of your own values in a manner that would allow you to create them from scratch.

And even if you could. Even if you somehow managed it. What about the person sitting next to you? What if they create their own values too? And what if those values have nothing in common with yours? Why would you assume they'd be compatible? And if they're not compatible, then you're not in a state of creative freedom. You're in a Hobbesian war of all against all. The state of brutal nature. As soon as you want something different from what I want, we are going to be at each other's throats.

So the problem with "create your own values" isn't just that it's difficult. It's that it might be incoherent. And Jung understood that. He spent his entire career trying to answer the question Nietzsche posed: on what basis should we erect our morality? Is there a structure, a pattern, a form that values must take in order to be viable? Not just for an individual, but for a society. Across time. And that's the question of what constitutes a playable game versus a non-playable game. One that can iterate. Sustain itself. Fortify itself across generations. Maybe those games have a set form. A pattern they have to occupy. And maybe that pattern is something closer to what the old religions were pointing at than any of us would like to admit.

---

Nietzsche wasn't a systematic philosopher. He wrote aphoristically. Poetically. Using narrative and image and metaphor. And that alienated him from the academic community of his time, because he didn't write in a dry and academic manner. He had a prophetic and poetic temperament. He's more like a character in a Dostoevsky novel than your typical philosopher. And that's not a coincidence. Dostoevsky and Nietzsche were both living on the edge of what would be. They could see the deep undercurrents. They could feel which way the zeitgeist was shifting. And I think it's because they were dreamers. Both of them. Intensely poetic and imaginative people. And the dream tends to inhabit the edge of the knowable.

There are people now who are living a hundred years in the future. Establishing what the world will be far down the road. And there are people now who live hundreds of years in the past. The bulk of us are somewhere ensconced in the present. But a few are out there on the edge. And Dostoevsky and Nietzsche were certainly on the edge in the mid-1800s. They had some sense of what was going to unfold over the next two hundred years. And they could see it with a kind of unerring accuracy.

Jung believed that. That some people live in different times at the same time. And maybe it's the dreamers who can do that. Because the dream gains in richness what it loses in precision. And there are geniuses who can stand on the border between the dream and the word, and translate the image into language. Nietzsche was definitely one of those figures.

---

And here's what's worth sitting with. Nietzsche was extraordinarily ill for most of his life. Half-blind. Could hardly write. Sick to his stomach constantly. Suffering from some illness that eventually manifested as psychosis and then a series of strokes that killed him at fifty-six. And one of the villages he lived in, in Switzerland, he was regarded as "the saint." That was his nickname. Because he was such a pleasant person. So good to everybody he met. Despite struggling with these deep questions. Despite being so ill he could barely function.

And that's worth thinking about. Because we have this sense that if terrible things happen to you, you'll turn out to be a terrible person. And that the fact is justified by your unfair suffering. You can understand that impulse. If you meet someone who's been terribly hurt and isn't behaving properly, you say, well, maybe if I was in that situation, I'd behave the same way. But the problem is that you do come across people, and maybe you'll be one of them, to whom terrible things have happened and will happen, because that will be the case for all of us, who are still nonetheless doing everything they can to make the world better rather than worse.

Nietzsche was one of those people. And I say that with all due care, because it's certainly also the case that his philosophical hammerings did destabilize the entire structure of Western civilization. You could think of him as destructive. But I've always thought of him, especially in regard to Christianity, as the kind of critic you really want to have around. Because if you're sure of yourself but your foundations are unsettled and in need of repair, it might be your best friend who comes along and taps on the weakest spot so you know where they are. And that might be initially very destructive. It might even speed the process of your crumbling. But if the consequence is that you can rebuild something out of the ruins that's even stronger, then that's the process of creative destruction.

And if you subject something to a radical criticism and it remains standing, that's an indication of its integrity. Its utility. Maybe the Judeo-Christian ethos can withstand the Nietzschean criticism. Combine it with the Freudian criticism, with the implicit criticism of Darwin, with the Enlightenment. Maybe there's more down in the foundations than we imagined. And maybe we can thank Nietzsche for laying the groundwork. Because without the questions he generated so trenchantly and so devastatingly, the quality of the answers would have been much lesser.

---

The world Nietzsche lived in was dynamite. Literally. The late 1800s were the height of the Industrial Revolution. The utter transformation of everything. In 1865, the typical person in the Western world lived on less than a dollar fifty a day in today's terms. Just barely clinging to the edge of hand-to-mouth sustainability. The entire world was poor in a way we can hardly imagine. And that was starting to transform with incredible rapidity.

Everything was happening at once. Darwin published *The Origin of Species* and provided the first plausible mechanism for life generating itself. Semmelweis proposed that surgeons should wash their hands and was hounded into insanity and death for his trouble. Edison invented the light bulb. The machine gun was invented, making one man into an army. The telephone connected people across oceans. The Impressionists exhibited their paintings and caused riots in Paris because they were teaching people to see the world in a completely new way. And you have no idea how much the way you see the world is a consequence of you mimicking great artists across time. They've literally taught you to understand what constitutes beauty. To see miracles where other people just see the predictable and the familiar.

And in the middle of all this, the Communist Manifesto was published. 1848. And that's interesting because Nietzsche prophesied that the ideas embedded in that document would prove extraordinarily attractive to people motivated by envy, lost in the nihilistic chaos that follows the collapse of traditional morality. Because you have to orient yourself with some form of systematic thought. The world's too complex otherwise. You have to reduce the multiplicity of existence to something approximating a systematic perspective, or you drown. And if one system of value collapses, another is going to beckon.

You're always in the situation of the Israelites in the desert. In the Exodus story. They leave the tyranny of Egypt, and where do they end up? Not in the promised land. In the desert. And in the desert, you're enticed into the worship of idols. Because you need some centralizing tendency to guide you and unite you. If you lose one, there's a terrible unconscious longing for another. And you see that in the Communist Manifesto. A new attempt to develop a system of axiomatic presuppositions. Almost entirely derived, at least in principle, from a rational approach. The communists insisted they had scientific materialism at the basis of their system. Which is a radical overstatement. It's not that easy to derive an ethic from science. And it isn't obvious that communism was driven by love of the common man. It was driven, to a tremendous degree, by an unacknowledged and omnipresent envious resentment. And the proof is in the pudding. The spirit of envious resentment, taken to its logical conclusion, results in the deaths of many, many people. Because it's murderous to the core. And that's exactly what happened every time the communist presuppositions were realized in any given state.

The radical leftist types say, "Well, that's not true communism." But Solzhenitsyn laid out, in the early seventies, an unassailable argument: the murderousness of the communist regimes was a necessary consequence of the a priori axioms. From each according to his ability, to each according to his need. Sounds extraordinarily compassionate. Generous, even. But has inside it an ethos that, allowed to unfold in its natural environment, produces something indistinguishable from the genocidal murderousness of Stalin and Mao. Could all that be embedded in a phrase that innocuous? Perhaps not all of it in that one phrase. But in the cloud of ideas that surrounds it, certainly. The proof is that when you planted that seed in various places, the same tree grew and bore the same evil fruits.

---

Nietzsche only lived fifty-six years. At twenty-five, he was offered a full professorship at the University of Basel. He didn't even have a PhD. They gave him an honorary one as a precondition. It was unheard of. His brilliance was recognized by the academic community at a very young age. And that's remarkable because he was a romantic spirit. Not a well-behaved person. Controversial. But so brilliant that despite the controversy, he was hot intellectual property. Those are relatively rare.

In 1879, he resigned. In 1880, he collapsed. Paralysis. Vascular dementia. Emergent psychosis. People have speculated endlessly about what was wrong with him. Syphilis. Mercury poisoning. Something hereditary. His father died at thirty-six of something that looked like a brain illness. Other people have speculated that his own philosophy drove him to the edge of madness. I don't believe that. I've read his works in sequence, and I don't see evidence of an emerging pathology caused by his thought. Instead, there's a fundamental optimism about Nietzschean thought, which is quite remarkable. <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">He believed we weren't motivated by the will to survive, but by the will to express power.</mark> To expand ourselves in life. And he tried to live in accordance with that ethos. And I think very successfully.

Jung published fourteen hundred pages of seminar notes on the first third of *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*. That's about forty pages. Fourteen hundred pages of exposition on a third of what's a very small volume. And so there's a tremendous amount to unpack in Nietzsche. It's a mustard seed issue. Christ compared the kingdom of God to a mustard seed, this tiny little grain of possibility that unfolds into a remarkable structure. Poetic language, image-laden language, has that proclivity. To contain much more than you'd expect given the brevity of the statement. Because the meaning isn't just in the words. It's in the relationship between the words. Between the phrases and the sentences. And then the poetic relationship of the sentences to the corpus of thought they refer to. It can pack a tremendous amount of information into a very small space.

And that's what Nietzsche does. He packs whole civilizations into a paragraph. Whole psychologies into a sentence. And if you sit with it long enough, if you let the hammer fall, you might find that what's left standing is something worth believing in after all.

## Takeaways

**1. The death of God is not a celebration.** Nietzsche's most famous pronouncement was a warning, not a victory lap. He foresaw nihilism, totalitarianism, and the death of millions. All of it came true.

**2. You cannot simply "create your own values."** Freud showed you're not the master of your own house. Jung showed that even if you could, your values and mine might have nothing in common, and we'd be at war. The question isn't whether to have a moral structure. It's what structure can actually sustain itself across time.

**3. The best critic is the one who respects what he's hammering.** Nietzsche attacked Christianity not because he hated it, but because he took it seriously enough to test its foundations. If something survives radical criticism, that's proof of its integrity. Not proof that the critic was wrong.

**4. Ideas have consequences that unfold across centuries.** The Communist Manifesto sounds compassionate. Its real-world implementations were genocidal. The same seed, planted in different soils, grew the same tree and bore the same evil fruits. Be careful what you worship in the desert.

**5. Suffering doesn't excuse you from virtue.** Nietzsche was half-blind, constantly ill, and dying by inches. His nickname was "the saint." Because he chose to be good to people anyway. The worst things that happen to you don't give you permission to become a worse person. They're the test of whether you can become a better one.
