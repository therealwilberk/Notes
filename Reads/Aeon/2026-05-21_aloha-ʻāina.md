# Aloha ʻāina

---

What do you think of when you think of Hawai‘i? Do you picture an island paradise ideal for beach holidays and teeming with tropical cocktails? The poet, activist and scholar Jamaica Heolimeleikalani Osorio wants to transform the story in your head. The Kānaka Maoli (‘true Hawaiian’) artist’s spoken-word performances are both forceful expressions and history lessons, often exploring the chasm between how Hawai‘i has been sold to the world and the far more complex realities of its history and culture. This includes the painful and enduring legacy of colonisation since Indigenous Hawaiians first made contact with Europeans in 1778.

In their portrait of Osorio, the California-based filmmakers Adam Loften and Emmanuel Vaughan-Lee chronicle her history of activism, rooted in the poetry she began performing as a teenager and continuing today in the fight against the planned construction of the Thirty Meter Telescope (TMT) on Mauna Kea, Hawai‘i’s most sacred mountain. At the core of both the film and Osorio’s philosophy is the Indigenous Hawaiian concept of ‘aloha ʻāina’, which teaches that the connections between people are inseparable from the land they share.
