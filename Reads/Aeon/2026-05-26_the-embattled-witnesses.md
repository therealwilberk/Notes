# The embattled witnesses

**Source:** Aeon  
**Author:** Alvina Hoffmann  
**Date:** 2026-05-26  
**Link:** https://aeon.co/essays/a-fragile-crown-jewel-the-uns-human-rights-experts

---

On 9 July 2025, the government of the United States imposed sanctions on Francesca Albanese, the UN special rapporteur on Palestine. Earlier in the spring, Albanese had written confidential letters to US companies, warning that she would name them in her forthcoming UN [report](https://www.un.org/unispal/document/a-hrc-59-23-from-economy-of-occupation-to-economy-of-genocide-report-special-rapporteur-francesca-albanese-palestine-2025/) for contributing to gross violations of human rights in Israel’s war in Gaza. The US administration framed these letters as a campaign of political and economic warfare. The sanctions against Albanese, an Italian-born legal scholar and human rights expert, are part of a broader executive order that sanctioned judges and prosecutors of the International Criminal Court. The US secretary of state Marco Rubio [cited](https://www.state.gov/releases/office-of-the-spokesperson/2025/07/sanctioning-lawfare-that-targets-u-s-and-israeli-persons) as the reason her direct engagement with the International Criminal Court ‘in efforts to investigate, arrest, detain, or prosecute nationals of the United States or Israel’. Albanese and the sanctioned ICC staff are now on the US Treasury Department’s Specially Designated Nationals list, alongside suspected terrorists, drug traffickers and arms dealers.

This is the first time the US has sanctioned a UN official, and the first time such a punishment was imposed on a UN special rapporteur for exercising their mandate. As Volker Türk, the UN high commissioner on human rights, stated: ‘UN special rapporteurs … address – by their nature – sensitive and often divisive issues, that are of international concern.’ The sanctioned individuals are banned from entering the territory of the US, have their assets frozen, and are unable to access basic financial services, such as using a credit card in their name or doing online shopping. The travel sanctions also extend to family members. On 14 May 2026, a US federal judge blocked these sanctions, arguing that the Trump administration had violated her free speech rights. Albanese welcomed the decision but said the battle was not over.

The experience of previous UN special rapporteurs on Palestine has shown that this is ‘a demanding, controversial, and sometimes embattled assignment’, as the foreword to the collected [edition](https://www.plutobooks.com/product/a-moon-will-rise-from-the-darkness/) of Albanese’s reports puts it. The American Richard Falk, a former special rapporteur on Palestine (2008-14) and a professor emeritus of international law at Princeton, was detained during his first trip in 2008, despite UN assurances to assuage his reluctance to travel to Israel from his home in California. Albanese assumed the mandate in 2022, but circumstances changed dramatically after Hamas’s attack on Israel on 7 October 2023 and Israel’s [genocidal war](https://www.icj-cij.org/case/192) against Gaza. Her mandate was renewed for another, final, three-year period in 2025.

But the Palestine mandate is not the only target. With a total of 46 thematic and 13 country mandates – part of what is known in the UN as special procedures, comprising special rapporteurs, independent experts and working groups – other rapporteurs have come under attack for their human rights work too. For example, in early 2018, Victoria Tauli-Corpuz, a member of the Indigenous Kankanaey Igorot people and at the time a UN special rapporteur on the rights of Indigenous peoples, was accused of membership in a terrorist organisation by her own government of the Philippines, then led by Rodrigo Duterte. She was forced to leave the country due to concerns for her safety. The accusation followed public comments she and other special rapporteurs made on the militarisation, attacks and killings of several Indigenous groups from the Philippine island of Mindanao after it was placed under martial law in May 2017.

Much earlier, two International Court of Justice cases in 1989 and 1999 dealt with the independence of UN special rapporteurs. The cases involved two experts and their home countries of Romania and Malaysia, which sought to restrict their ability to exercise their mandates. Both cases clarified that special rapporteurs held privileges and immunities as stipulated in the 1946 UN [convention](https://treaties.un.org/pages/ViewDetails.aspx?src=TREATY&mtdsg_no=III-1&chapter=3&clang=_en), which protected them from legal proceedings against words spoken in their official capacity during the course of their mission.

Such harsh pushback and attacks against special rapporteurs often heighten public awareness of these independent experts. But who are these people who come to assume such high-profile roles as independent human rights experts at the UN?

Special rapporteurs are part of a global body of human rights experts who have built a reputation in their particular thematic field, recognised expertise and often networks with other experts, civil society or UN officials. However, what counts as human rights expertise is made up of a selection of professional profiles, resources, skills and education. This means that there can be quite a variety within this collective. For example, over time, the body of experts has been shaped by four broad types of professional profiles, growing from lawyers and state representatives to human rights academics and professionals in human rights advocacy to experts with subject-specific knowledge. Some experts have more knowledge of the UN system than others, acquired through professional or previous expert appointments in the UN or their academic knowledge. Taken together, they bring a number of skills to their appointments, such as legal knowledge, experience in litigation, engaging a variety of audiences, connecting and amplifying the voices of victims of human rights violations, and at times managerial skills and diplomatic etiquette.

Take, for example, the mandate on the human rights situation in the Occupied Palestinian Territory. This position was created in 1993, and Albanese is the eighth mandate-holder and the first woman in this mandate. The first mandate-holder, René Felber, was a professional politician from Switzerland, followed by Hannu Halinen of Finland and Giorgio Giacomelli of Italy, who were both professional diplomats. This mirrors the more traditional pool of experts from high politics and state diplomacy appointed to special rapporteur positions in its earlier days, in the late 1960s and ’70s. Their appointments were followed by John Dugard of South Africa (2001-08) and Falk of the US, both international law academics with a number of high-profile appointments in international organisations and NGOs. Dugard, for example, was a member of the International Law Commission of the UN and a judge ad hoc at the International Court of Justice. Falk was a chair of the board of the Nuclear Age Peace Foundation and an advisory board member of the World Federalist Institute, among other positions. Their appointments were followed by Makarim Wibisono of Indonesia (2014-16), another career diplomat. Wibisono, initially chosen to mend relations with Israel, resigned from this position after only two years as, in the words of Albanese’s book, ‘his expectations of cooperation and assured entry’ to the Occupied Palestinian Territory were not met. The appointment of the Canadian law professor Michael Lynk (2016-22) followed, to the opposition of the Canadian government.

A coalition of special rapporteurs managed to avert what they saw as an attack on their independence

In their foreword to Albanese’s book, _A Moon Will Rise from the Darkness_ (2025), Falk, Dugard and Lynk take stock of the evolution of the mandate. They draw a sharp contrast between the first three special rapporteurs, and their diplomatic demeanours, with their own appointments. The career diplomats were able to access Israel, and wrote diplomatic reports that had little public impact in the immediate aftermath of the Oslo Accords (the interim agreements of 1993 and 1995 between Israel and the Palestine Liberation Organisation). Dugard’s appointment changed this diplomatic orientation, given his international reputation and experience in other special UN assignments and as a human rights lawyer opposing apartheid in South Africa. From this appointment onwards, the mandate-holders brought their human rights law expertise in the areas of apartheid and occupation to bear on their reports on the human rights situation in the Occupied Territory. Falk, Dugard and Lynk all emphasise how Albanese’s reports are a continuation of their own tone and recommendations. Albanese herself worked for the UN Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights and the Relief and Works Agency for Palestine Refugees, before appointments as an affiliate scholar at Georgetown University, and providing research and legal assistance to the think tank Arab Renaissance for Democracy and Development.

The evolution of appointments in this mandate showcases the great variety of professional profiles and skill sets that special rapporteurs bring. Other factors that shape the body are their educational background. Many but not necessarily all have expertise in law or human rights law. The decision to pursue a law degree is often shaped by the broader societal context and family backgrounds: many will have witnessed struggles for human rights up close, during moments of transition from authoritarian regimes to democracies, or been influenced by a parent or another family member to take up the study of law.

Before 2006, special rapporteurs were appointed directly by the chair of the UN Commission on Human Rights, based on recommendations, their global reputation or on personal networks. In 2006, the Commission on Human Rights was dismantled and replaced by today’s UN Human Rights Council. During this reform process, states attempted to exert more control over independent human rights experts, introducing a code of conduct. A much more constraining draft was initially presented, but a coalition of special rapporteurs managed to avert what they saw as an attack on their independence. However, the biggest change during this reform process was the appointment structure. Since 2006, candidates can apply for a vacant position, often with support from states or civil society, which lobby behind the scenes. An advisory committee of state ambassadors on the Human Rights Council will shortlist and interview candidates, then submit their ranking to the president of the Human Rights Council, who, in almost all cases, appoints the first chosen candidate.

While many special rapporteurs are already known and recognised in their professional fields, being named an independent human rights expert by the UN brings their names to a high-profile global stage. They soon learn that, at its core, this is a peculiar position whose potential needs to be carefully negotiated: while special rapporteurs receive their mandates from the UN (including privileges and immunities like UN officials), as independent experts, they act in their personal capacity rather than representing the UN. Many special rapporteurs have described this as an in-between space that requires them to adjust their human rights work to be fully able to leverage the potential of such an ambiguously defined position. The learning curve over a period of three years – though usually six, if the mandate is renewed – is steep.

Once appointed, special rapporteurs have four broad tasks: they undertake country visits to investigate human rights situations around the world; they send direct communications to state parties on individual cases or concerns of a broader nature; they make contributions to the development of international human rights standards; and they engage in advocacy, raise public awareness and provide technical advice on human rights issues.

To be able to fulfil these tasks, they need to persuade state representatives to give them access to their territories while maintaining the integrity of the special rapporteur office. Many rapporteurs, especially if they worked previously in academia or professional advocacy, must quickly learn the peculiarities of diplomatic etiquette in the UN. This is akin to what the legal scholar Mikael Rask Madsen has [called](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/abs/human-rights-in-the-twentieth-century/legal-diplomacy-law-politics-and-the-genesis-of-postwar-european-human-rights/28869C53649201456ABF4484F963FA40) legal diplomacy, a practice of the subtle interplay between law and politics. This skill is learnt on the job: from diplomatic protocol when engaging with ambassadors and repackaging messages for different audiences without compromising their independence or position, to negotiating access to states or sites of detention.

Some have chosen to act as individuals while others harness the institutional resources offered to them

For example, in June 2023, the then-special rapporteur on counterterrorism and human rights, the Irish legal scholar Fionnuala Ní Aoláin, made the first-ever official [visit](https://www.ohchr.org/en/press-releases/2023/06/expert-welcomes-historic-visit-united-states-and-guantanamo-detention) by a UN expert to the Guantánamo Bay detention facility during a technical visit to the US. Diplomatic immunity gives special rapporteurs, in theory, licence to conduct their work without fear of reprisals or attacks on their person. However, the diplomatic field can also limit what can be said or if access is granted to state territories and other sites. This is why many choose to rely on their pre-existing networks in their work for the mandate, which requires an enormous investment of time in addition to their paid jobs in fields such as academia, NGOs or law.

However, state visits can come at a cost if they compromise the special rapporteur’s perceived independence. For example, the special rapporteur on unilateral coercive measures, Alena Douhan of Belarus, has been criticised by various actors for conducting state visits without meeting with civil society representatives and human rights defenders. For example, in 2022, Douhan was the first UN special rapporteur to visit Iran since 2005, after the country had previously denied access to 14 other UN human rights experts. Her visit was [criticised](https://www.rferl.org/a/iran-un-rapporteur-propaganda/31848567.html) by Iranian human rights defenders as playing into the hands of the regime. Douhan’s previous visits to countries, such as Venezuela in 2021, followed similar patterns.

While practising legal diplomacy, special rapporteurs must assert their independence by carefully balancing relations with member states, civil society representatives, and the UN Secretariat. They are part of what the scholars Tatiana Carayannis and Thomas G Weiss have [called](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-third-united-nations-9780198855859?cc=gb&lang=en&) the ‘Third’ United Nations: a knowledge ecology that consists of non-state actors, scholars, consultants, think tanks, NGOs, the private sector and the media, which make important contributions to ideas, standard-setting and decision-making. They interact with the ‘first’ UN, the member states, and the ‘second’ UN, the staff members of the Secretariat. And so, to maintain their independence in this web of relations, some have chosen to act as individuals while others harness the institutional resources offered to them by building relations and acting with a collective of special rapporteurs. For example, a number of special rapporteurs regularly issue joint communications on pressing global human rights issues, for [example](https://spcommreports.ohchr.org/TMResultsBase/DownLoadPublicCommunicationFile?gId=29360), an address to the government of Kenya in the context of its violent crackdowns of protests in June, July and August 2024.

What explains the distinct position of UN special rapporteurs, appointed by the UN but acting in their personal capacity to investigate human rights violations in states? A look at the historical development of this position offers some clues.

The key principles of the UN Charter, signed by all members in 1945, are simultaneously anchored in a commitment to human rights beyond state borders and the domestic jurisdiction principle, which protects state sovereignty from external interference. At the time, leading voices in international law, diplomacy and philosophy argued about the ambiguous relationship between universal human rights and domestic jurisdiction. This relationship was imagined, if not sought to be reconciled, in quite different and at times conflicting ways.

These two faces of the Charter were the imprints of various kinds of actors. NGOs and small states introduced the language of human rights into the Charter against the preferences of the great powers after the Second World War, which ended up retaining veto rights in the Security Council. This early period left important legacies for the first two decades of the UN human rights system.

From the beginning, the figure of the expert was contested. States grappled with the question of what role, if any, experts should play in the UN Commission on Human Rights, the precursor of today’s Human Rights Council, since the late 1940s. Countries like the Soviet Union favoured governmental representatives, while countries like Lebanon, the United Kingdom and Belgium argued in favour of independent experts who had, in their view, a more universal representativity, as they would represent people all over the world, and not merely their own countries. Experts could also raise questions deemed ‘embarrassing’ for governments. But it was only in 1967, after a long hiatus from its 1948 Universal Declaration of Human Rights, that the UN became authorised to appoint experts to investigate human rights violations.

UN delegates in the 1990s became ‘bound by a relatively tight corset of state directives’

John Peters Humphrey, the first director of the UN Division of Human Rights, described this long hiatus of the UN human rights machinery as self-inflicted, the result of an institutional moment that could have been avoided. The Indian member of the Commission on Human Rights, Hansa Mehta, suggested that a list of communications about human rights violations, with the names of the authors redacted, be circulated. Then, she wanted to discuss allegations made by NGOs that held consultative status with the UN’s Economic and Social Council. Communications by individuals were made available upon request. While at first this procedure was agreed, the Filipino diplomat General Romulo became worried about possible reprisals against its authors, and so a committee was tasked with assessing the matter. It was this committee that found that the commission lacked any powers to study communications and hence could not take any action. ‘Such was the genesis of a much-criticised doctrine that prevailed in the United Nations until 1967,’ Humphrey wrote in his memoir about his years at the UN.

Born of this moment in 1967 were various ‘ad hoc working groups’, investigating apartheid in South Africa, Israeli practices in the Occupied Palestinian Territory and the human rights situation in Chile, the precursors of today’s special rapporteurs. The Austrian international law academic and politician Felix Ermacora – a delegate to the UN and a member of the Council of Europe’s human rights commission – was appointed to all these ad hoc working groups, and later became the first special rapporteur on Afghanistan (1984-95). As one of the first, and longest-serving, independent human rights expert at the UN, his UN career spanned the earliest attempts to conduct fact-finding on human rights violations in the 1960s to the mid-1990s, when independent human rights experts became firmly institutionalised as special rapporteurs.

According to his own writings, Ermacora developed a reputation among other state representatives at the European Commission of Human Rights for being outspoken and frank. This, and coming from the small neutral state of Austria, might have been reasons for his frequent appointments as an independent expert to examine human rights violations. He exercised these mandates with ‘relative freedom’ compared with delegates in the 1990s, when they became ‘bound by a relatively tight corset of state directives’. In his generation, he wrote, international human rights developments were largely driven by professional diplomats.

This would soon change with the [ascent](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev.lawsocsci.2.032406.145708) of international human rights NGOs such as Amnesty International. Theo van Boven of the Netherlands, the widely respected director of the Human Rights Division between 1977 and 1982, came from the world of NGOs and enjoyed a lot of support from civil society. Most of his tenure coincided with the US presidency of Jimmy Carter, a period during which the US enshrined a commitment to human rights in its foreign policy. Van Boven initiated pioneering innovations in the human rights programme of the UN. He reformed the Human Rights Division and moved the UN’s work of human rights from promotion towards protection through a set of new mechanisms that are still in place, including the special procedures system as we know it today. Despite this apparent success, his contract was not renewed in 1982 after he gave a poignant speech on the right to life, calling out by name those states that practised summary and arbitrary executions. That year, van Boven decided to shift the focus of the human rights programme towards the right to life. While his position was seen as untenable, this successfully established the UN special rapporteur on extrajudicial killings.

As this brief history showcases, the special procedures system has its roots in ad hoc working groups, courageous human rights leadership and a supportive geopolitical moment. Today, the UN has been facing ‘a race to bankruptcy’, with financial pressures caused by a number of member states that failed to pay their legally binding contributions in full and on time. This comes at a time when conflicts are rising around the globe, and more, not less, assistance is needed. The UN’s human rights system, however, has been chronically underfunded for decades. One such important pillar has long been propped up by unpaid experts, whom Kofi Annan [called](https://news.un.org/en/story/2006/11/201202) the ‘crown jewel’ of the Human Rights Council. How much longer?
