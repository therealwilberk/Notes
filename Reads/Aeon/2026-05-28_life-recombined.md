# Life recombined

Recombinant DNA – or DNA created by artificially splicing the genes of more than one organism – has transformed modern medicine, helping to launch the biotechnology industry and opening up paths to countless new treatments. However, as this short documentary from the Science Communication Lab chronicles, it was a revolution born in controversy. Early experimentation prompted alarm both within the scientific community and among the broader public, with fears about what tinkering with the building blocks of life might unleash drawing comparisons to the advent of the nuclear bomb.

The film explores the early days of modern biotechnology through archival footage and interviews with some of its central figures. These include the late US biochemist Paul Berg, who received the 1980 Nobel Prize in Chemistry and is widely credited with creating the first recombinant DNA molecule, and his former graduate student, the US biochemist Janet Mertz, whose vital yet less celebrated work made the breakthrough possible. By revisiting this revolutionary moment in science – and perhaps even in the history of our species – the film reveals how scientific discovery is shaped by social and political forces, often born of diffuse and collective efforts, even if only a few names end up in the history books.
