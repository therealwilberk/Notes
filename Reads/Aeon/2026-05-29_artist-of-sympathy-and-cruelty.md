# Artist of sympathy and cruelty

**Source:** Aeon  
**Author:** Dorian Bandy  
**Date:** 2026-05-29  
**Link:** https://aeon.co/essays/why-mozarts-operas-are-tests-of-moral-character

---

Midway through the second act of Mozart’s 1790 opera _Così fan tutte_ , a young woman named Fiordiligi stands alone on stage, about to betray a commitment she once held at the very core of her identity. Her fiancé Guglielmo has, she believes, been called away to war and, although she promises to remain faithful in his absence, a charming stranger has begun to solicit her affections. Fiordiligi resists these advances for much of the opera, but by the middle of Act II she can no longer ignore the growing attraction she has begun to feel. The aria she sings is at once a pre-emptive confession of guilt and a plea for mercy:

> In pity’s name, my darling, forgive  
> The error of my loving soul.  
> Amid these shadows and these groves,  
> Oh God, it will always remain hidden.  
> My courage, my constancy  
> Will drive away this wicked desire;  
> It will lose the memory  
> That shames and horrifies me.  
> To whom did my vain, ungrateful heart  
> Fail in its faithlessness?  
> Your trust, beloved,  
> Deserved a better reward.

Mozart sets this text to music of almost unbearable tenderness. The aria is graceful and resigned, and the whole scene is among the most beautiful in the opera. Nowhere up to this point has Fiordiligi sounded so sincere, so defeated, so richly deserving of our sympathy and compassion.

But beneath the music’s ravishing surface runs an undercurrent of cruel irony. Woven into the orchestral accompaniment are two solo horns that intrude on Fiordiligi’s melody when it repeats, punctuating her line with ornate interjections. Audiences in the 18th century would have recognised these horn calls as musical symbols of cuckoldry, a widely understood pun on the image of the ‘horned husband’ who suffers sexual betrayal by his inconstant wife. The horns’ presence here shatters Fiordiligi’s most heartfelt declaration of shame.

For centuries, audiences have struggled with this and other scenes from _Così_ , an opera whose plot is laden with moral confusion, in which musical beauty is a weapon in the composer’s hands. Perhaps Mozart is simply depicting a character who is both anguished and, in a sense, guilty. But perhaps he is also slyly mocking her with a painful sign that the plot will soon push her further and further towards infidelity. Indeed, he may even be mocking _our_ sympathies by smashing the sincerity of the moment and reminding us listeners that, in the end, these characters we come to love are mere puppets in the hands of the omnipotent artist, destined to play out whatever heartbreaking narrative has been set for them.

The tension we feel as we witness Fiordiligi’s struggles – that tug between sympathy and judgment, tenderness and unease – is a general feature of Mozart’s operas, something he seems to positively revel in conjuring. Across a series of operatic masterpieces composed from 1781 until his death in 1791, Mozart pushed the medium as far as he could, drawing listeners repeatedly into troubled and morally ambiguous viewpoints. Opera was, in his hands, a narrative engine for producing sympathy – not sympathy in its modern-day sense but in a more rigorous sense widely explored at the time, not least by the great Scottish philosopher [Adam Smith](https://aeon.co/essays/we-should-look-closely-at-what-adam-smith-actually-believed).

Smith’s first book, the _Theory of Moral Sentiments_ (published in 1759, when Mozart was three years old), frames sympathy as that imaginative faculty whereby we learn to place ourselves in other people’s positions, model their dilemmas, weigh their actions against our own imagined responses, and thereby come to evaluate their behaviour. ‘We enter as it were into [another’s] body,’ Smith writes, ‘and become in some measure the same person with him.’ He even posits a similar mechanism for our own self-understanding in the form of the image of an ‘impartial spectator’, essentially a stand-in for the conscience, whom we imagine observing and adjudicating our behaviour just as we do for others. This emphasis on imaginative flexibility was itself a departure from an older philosophical tradition that treated moral judgment as a matter of rational deduction and the application of timeless principles, as if ethics were a branch of geometry. For Smith, moral life was messier and more provisional than such strictures allowed; the question of how we come to understand other minds was among the central intellectual challenges of the mid- and late-18th century, one that cut across disciplinary lines from philosophy to economics to the arts.

Mozart’s operatic genius was to treat his listeners like sympathetic spectators. He does not tell us what to think about the characters on stage. Instead, he forces us to feel with them and leaves us to reckon with the visceral sense of ethical confusion such an experience reveals. The result is a series of artworks whose musical structures themselves enact a philosophical agenda.

This is not how opera usually worked, either before Mozart’s time or for much of the century following his death. Since its birth at the close of the 16th century, opera had dealt primarily with scenarios drawn from mythology or ancient history. The dominant voice in shaping these stories was the Italian poet Pietro Metastasio, whose libretti were set to music by virtually every major composer of the 18th century. Metastasio’s plots revolve around rulers faced with tests of character, often in the form of betrayals and conspiracies, and they invariably end with virtue rewarded, vice punished, and moral order restored. Composers setting these texts served as moral chaperones, using music to guide audiences to the proper conclusions. This applied both to the outlook of operas as a whole and to composers’ handling of individual arias, where the role of the music was largely to buoy up the emotions and ideas described in the text. By Mozart’s lifetime, such conventions were so firmly entrenched that even familiar mythological stories were reshaped to conform to them. Christoph Willibald Gluck’s _Orfeo ed Euridice_ (1762), for instance, contrives a happy ending in which the gods intervene on behalf of the titular lovers, resurrecting Euridice as a reward for Orpheus’ noble constancy.

Mozart, too, grew up composing operas in this tradition, and he knew its conventions intimately. He often crafted music that would project the essence of each character’s psychological world. In a letter written to his father in September 1781, Mozart describes the musical mechanics of a rage aria he was composing for the character Osmin, the comic villain in his German opera _Die Entführung aus dem Serail_ :

> As Osmin’s rage grows and grows – just when you think the aria is over – the allegro assai – in a completely different meter and different key – is bound to make a tremendous effect … I’ve not chosen a key foreign to F (the key of the aria) but one related to it, but not the one closest to it, D minor, but the more remote A minor …

In Mozart’s account of composing this aria, no musical effect is off limits. He plays with tempo, key and rhythm, planning like a skilled strategist who calibrates his move against his listeners’ expectations.

But as Mozart’s narrative technique ripened, he fell back on convention less and less. Beginning with _Le nozze di Figaro_ in 1786 and continuing through _Don Giovanni_ and _Così fan tutte_ , composed in the following years, Mozart started to explore possibilities that made his manipulations of operatic viewpoint particularly visceral. Good authors can do much with words alone, just as good composers can do much with music alone. But Mozart began to explore ways that a hybrid artform like opera could use music to comment ironically on the words, multiplying the expressive possibilities and opening space for the contradictions and ethical ambiguities that would become central to his work. By using music to tell a different story from what an operatic text might seem to imply, Mozart could place his characters and listeners alike in ethically ambiguous positions, suspending them without a clear path to resolution.

The music tells us what the character does not realise: Zerlina has long since fallen under Giovanni’s spell

Consider, for example, the celebrated seduction duet ‘Là ci darem la mano’ (‘There we will join hands’) from _Don Giovanni_ (1787). Giovanni – the Italianised Don Juan – is trying to win over the peasant girl Zerlina immediately after her marriage to Masetto. In the dialogue leading up to the duet, Giovanni promises riches and marriage. Zerlina’s knowing reply, however, is that noblemen often deceive young girls. When the duet proper begins, Giovanni continues his urging, and Zerlina continues to profess indecision:

> _Giovanni:_

> There we will join hands,  
> There you will say ‘yes’ to me.  
> Look – it’s not far.  
> Let’s leave from here, my beloved.

> _Zerlina:_

> I want to and I don’t want to:  
> My heart trembles a little.  
> It’s true, I would be happy,  
> But he may be playing tricks on me after all.

Giovanni opens the duet with a suave tune – indeed, a tune so irresistible that it took on a life of its own outside the opera as later composers like Beethoven and Chopin paid homage to it. But Zerlina’s response is where the complexities set in. She claims to be undecided, yet she adopts Giovanni’s melody. The music tells us something the character herself does not realise: that beneath the professed ambivalence, she has long since fallen under Giovanni’s spell and made up her mind to go with him. The words suggest a woman who is wise to male treachery, while Mozart’s music paints a foregone conclusion as-yet unacknowledged even by Zerlina herself, for whom the consciously constructed self-narrative lags behind the momentum of desire.

Not only does this reading lend Zerlina’s character psychological depth. It also complicates the listener’s moral experience. We know Giovanni to be a predator, yet the music does not depict a guilty aggressor and his innocent victim. Rather, we hear two characters engaging in an increasingly tender exchange. As the duet continues and the opening tune repeats, Mozart even reworks it so the two would-be lovers share the melody, Zerlina now eagerly completing Giovanni’s phrases even as her ambivalent text remains unchanged. The orchestration, too, adjusts. Where the vocal lines were, at the start, delivered by each singer against an unobtrusive background of quietly pulsing strings, we now hear them expanded with sensual wind writing.

The plot views Giovanni as a villain: in the Act II Finale, he is dragged to Hell. But for the duration of the duet, we are brought within the erotically charged world of these two characters and invited to feel Zerlina’s attraction from the inside. The music highlights Zerlina’s excitement and then spins this premise into an irresistible duet, drawing us gently out of the moral perspective we inhabited at the start of the scene. It may be impossible to identify the precise moment of change – yet by the close of the duet, when the fling is interrupted by one of Giovanni’s righteously outraged exes, we just might feel a tinge of disappointment, against our own better judgment.

Return now to Fiordiligi, whose predicament is far more wrenching than Zerlina’s. There is a further layer of irony permeating the scene I described at the start. Fiordiligi’s charming stranger is seducing her under the falsest of pretences, for he is in fact her fiancé’s best friend, disguised as part of a cruel bet to prove that women cannot be trusted. Fiordiligi and her sister are engaged to two soldiers, and these men, goaded on by a cynical philosopher named Don Alfonso, agree to stage a phoney departure to battle, disguise themselves as Albanians, and return to seduce each other’s partners. Shortly before Fiordiligi sings her pained aria, we watch as her fiancé Guglielmo seduces her sister Dorabella. Fiordiligi is not privy to this, but we viewers are.

The horn calls thus take on further significance. In addition to taunting Fiordiligi in her self-inflicted guilt, they remind us that she, too, is a victim of deceit and betrayal, that the ‘faithful’ lover she so ardently addresses in her aria is even now dallying with her sister. Although it is fashionable to pin the blame for this cruel plot on the poet Lorenzo da Ponte, who provided Mozart with the text of this opera (as well as the texts for _Le nozze_ _di Figaro_ and _Don Giovanni_), it is Mozart’s music that so pointedly implicates us in the mechanism of mutual destruction set in motion by the men’s vanity and hubris. The aria’s music simultaneously involves us and repels us, forcing us to experience its strange allure alongside the basest moral wreckage.

If the ‘Là ci darem’ duet showed us how the manipulation of viewpoint can lead to an audience’s complicity in a relatively simple case, _Così_ puts a far more complex situation on display, distributing sympathy in every direction at once. We feel for Fiordiligi in her anguish and know how strong the pull of temptation must be if it can shake so entrenched a belief in fidelity. We recognise that the men who engineered her predicament are themselves betraying the trust they claim to be testing. And they, too, are in a sense betrayed, both by their partners and, as the plot unfolds, by each other, as each succeeds in winning over his friend’s betrothed. That the new matches seem musically and textually so much more aligned than the original pairs only adds to the confusion. We cannot resolve these contradictions into a verdict; the music refuses to let us stand outside the scene and judge. We experience Fiordiligi’s pain as well as the entire kaleidoscopic clutter of other perspectives – and that is precisely the point.

Mozart never read Adam Smith, so there is no direct causal relationship to establish. But both were responding to a concern that became central to intellectual and artistic life during the second half of the 18th century: the problem of knowing other minds and the limits of understanding human decision-making. Smith’s model of sympathy and spectatorship is compelling; but as he himself argues, sensory inputs can be misleading: ‘our senses … never did, and never can, carry us beyond our own person, and it is by the imagination only that we can form any conception of [other people’s] sensations.’

This is not to suggest that Mozart’s music simply plays out the sympathetic process Smith describes, as though the value of his art lay in its facile demonstration of a philosophical outlook. But it is striking that both men arrived almost simultaneously at a shared insight: understanding other people is not a matter of simply observing them and running moral arithmetic, but of imaginatively and uncertainly entering their situation and attempting to feel the forces that act upon them. For Smith, this idea would form the basis for his most famous work, _The Wealth of Nations_ (1776), where those ‘moral sentiments’ driven by sympathy and imagination are now reframed as what would today be called economic incentives, the whole synthesis a virtuosic meditation on the problem of interpersonal coordination on the scale of society writ large.

For Mozart, these ideas led to a heightened interest in the depiction of characters’ psyches, specifically in the act of making difficult choices. Fiordiligi’s romantic dilemma and Zerlina’s decision to dally with Giovanni exemplify this point, and both operas track how these characters’ behaviour changes as more information becomes available to them – when the women in _Così_ learn of the deception they have suffered, and when Zerlina discovers Giovanni’s treachery and resists his further advances. Mozart’s purpose in crafting scenes that so carefully draw us into these characters’ inner worlds is to make our understanding of their motivations all the more visceral. He is less interested in the question of whether a character is blameless than he is in making us feel the forces pulling at them. That he generates sympathy not only with virtuous characters but with morally compromised and villainous figures is no sign of relativism, for these operas are not indifferent to questions of right and wrong. It is, rather, his way of acknowledging just how tangled human motivation can be.

Even a villain must be given music that invites listeners inside that character’s perspective

Mozart’s capacity to enter into his characters’ minds is part of what made him so powerful a dramatist. In that same letter from 1781, in which he describes the careful construction of a rage aria, he goes on to explain that:

> passions, whether violent or not, must never be expressed to the point of exciting disgust, and music, even in the most terrible situations, must never offend the ear but must please the listener, or in other words, must never cease to be music.

Even a villain, even a character in the grip of murderous rage, must be given music that invites listeners inside that character’s perspective. Mozart’s point is not that villainy deserves aesthetic reward; he simply considers the very purpose of music as projecting a psyche so thoroughly that listeners can follow the opera as a play of decisions and deliberations.

He took the same approach with performers. Sending his soon-to-be sister-in-law Aloysia Weber an aria he had composed for her for the role of Andromeda, he advised: ‘put yourself seriously into Andromeda’s state and situation! – … imagine that you really are that person yourself.’ These letters make Mozart sound like a method actor, and as a composer he seems to have followed his own advice. His music suggests a mind that could slip in and out of other identities the way most people change clothes, modelling characters’ interiority so completely that the distinction between creator and creation often seems to dissolve.

Unlike the many operatic composers who view their characters from an authoritative perspective, Mozart projects each character’s viewpoint rather than taking an overarching, judgmental view. The Count in _Le nozze di Figaro_ , for all his predatory designs on Susanna, sings music that is only occasionally laced with menace, but which more often is balanced by vulnerability. When we witness his fumbling attempt to flirt with Susanna at the start of Act III, we are asked, for a moment, to share his feelings of frustration and wounded pride: we feel, with him, what it is like to be thwarted. Don Giovanni, too, though far more evil than the Count, is censured not by Mozart but by the otherworldly forces whose intervention is needed to contain him. While Don Giovanni is alive, his music crackles with such vitality that, after his death, we are left feeling at once a sense of relief and justice – and perhaps a tinge of loss. The closing scene feels empty without him, as if Mozart has quietly conscripted us as Giovanni’s accomplices. In all these cases, Mozart gives full voice to their desires. He asks us to do the moral work ourselves, having first ensured that we cannot consider such charged matters from a safe distance. Whether the horn calls mock Fiordiligi or mourn for her, Mozart is still daring us to decide.
