# A philosophy of home

**Source:** Aeon  
**Author:** Sandrine Bergès  
**Date:** 2026-06-12  
**Link:** https://aeon.co/essays/what-ancient-philosophy-really-thought-about-domestic-life

---

Political philosophy – a discipline we trace back to Plato and Aristotle – is reasoning about how we live together in political units. It is about states, government, laws, institutions and citizenship. But it doesn’t have much to say about homes, families, marriage or parenting. The discipline that studies how we live together in family units, as opposed to political ones, used to be called economics, from the Greek _oikonomika_ , or science of the home. This was before the name became exclusively attached to how we make and spend money, when what went on inside the home still mattered to philosophers. It no longer does. This may be because, until very recently, philosophers were almost exclusively male – even today in the UK, only a [quarter](https://bpa.ac.uk/2021/11/15/women-in-philosophy-uk-2021-report/) of those at the top of the profession are women.

![Ancient clay sculpture of two figures around a pot on a fire, one seated, wearing a head wrap with a hand raised to face.](https://images.aeonmedia.co/user_image_upload/4960/full-woman-cooking-watched-by-a-girl-cr9155-d1.jpg?width=3840&quality=75&format=auto)

Women philosophers, of course, have always written about the home. I write about it in my new [book](https://academic.oup.com/book/61894), _No Place like Home_ (2026). But women philosophers have long been excluded from the canon: until very [recently](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/disappearing-ink-9780190086633?cc=gb&lang=en&), they were not read, they were not studied, they were not taught. This is changing. But, until it does, we can still ask why the home was not a topic of philosophical interest.

My answer is that things needn’t have been that way. Philosophers took from the past what they wanted, and helped the rest disappear. The story of how the home as a domestic space dropped out of philosophical thinking is a story of reception. And I will try and reconstruct a small part of that story here.

Although it’s not clear when thinking about the home as a sphere separate from political life actually started, we can find evidence of it in Aristotle’s political philosophy, which was developed in Athens in the 4th century BCE. Aristotle postulated a natural division between men and the state, on the one hand, and women and the home on the other: a public and a private sphere. He believed human beings could associate in two distinct ways, with a sphere corresponding to each mode of association: the household for the private sphere versus the city-state for the public sphere. The household he saw as a natural necessity for survival, and for bringing up children, and this is where women belonged, both for their protection, and because they were best suited for obeying and not for taking part in public life. Adult men, who did not need protecting, and whose abilities went beyond simple domestic organisation, belonged to the city-state and public service.

However much we’d like to blame Aristotle for millennia of sexist prejudice, he did not write in a vacuum. Nor was he the only one in Athens who believed that men and women needed to live separate lives, in separate spheres. Pericles, the great general of the Peloponnesian war, addressed women thus in his funerary oration: ‘Your glory is great if you do not fall beneath the natural condition of your sex, and if you have as little fame among men as is possible, whether for virtue or by way of reproach.’ Women’s presence should not be felt outside the home – they should neither be seen, nor heard.

Aristotle’s philosophy would have been better had he learnt to cook

Even before that, ancient Greek society was arranged in such a way that it was impossible for a woman to leave her home by herself, let alone to join in political debates in the city. A respectable Athenian woman could leave her home only to take part in specific religious festivals, veiled and chaperoned. When men from outside the family were invited into the home, they were kept away from the women’s quarters, the _gynaecea_. Some women did work outside the home as labourers or entertainers, but they were, as working people often are, invisible. They were not considered part of the city, of the political sphere. And they were not respectable.

Gynaeceum scene depicted on an epinetron, a thigh guard worn by women during wool-working. Courtesy the [Louvre](https://collections.louvre.fr/en/ark:/53355/cl010274766), Paris

Aristotle did not, then, create the situation in which women were expected to stay at home and men to go out into the world; rather, his thought reflected the _status quo_. But he was one of several influential men who endorsed this situation, perceiving it to be a fine arrangement, one that was conducive to general happiness. And among these men, he is the one whose writings enjoyed the longest and greatest influence.

One way to measure Aristotle’s influence is to observe how it became integrated into philosophical culture, but also how it was resisted. Aristotle was read by people thinking about gender roles in Western Europe from the 13th century onwards, and much earlier in other parts of the world such as Constantinople – where he influenced the thought of the royal historian Anna Komnene, and much of the Islamic world. Such was his intellectual authority that women philosophers who came across his pronouncements felt unable to fight them. There were exceptions though. In 14th- to 15th-century France, Christine de Pizan cites Aristotle as one of the authors who underestimated the contributions of women to the progress of humanity. In 17th-century Venice, Lucrezia Marinella engaged with Aristotle’s sexism, first attempting to refute it in her _Nobility and Excellence of Women_(1600), and later, subverting his description of the spheres to create her own account of domesticity in _Exhortations to Women and to Others if They Please_ (1645). In 17th-century Mexico, the philosopher Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz took Aristotle to task for his exclusion of women and domesticity, arguing in her essay ‘Answer by the Poet’ (1691) that Aristotle’s philosophy would have been better had he learnt to cook.

Aristotle’s influence was, and still is, powerful. But while many philosophers of antiquity agreed with him that there were two spheres shaped to accommodate the various needs of humanity, the discourse was a lot more diverse than you might think if you’d read only Aristotle’s _Politics_. Unfortunately, through accidents of history, other texts that might have encouraged a different take on the home and domesticity did not survive as completely, or their provenance was not as clear as that of Aristotle’s _Politics_. But had these texts survived and been transmitted, how might the story have gone? Would women still have been confined to the home, and condemned to political insignificance?

To diversify our reading of ancient Greek philosophy of the home, we don’t even need to go (much) further than Aristotle. Until very recently, Aristotle was believed to have authored a companion [book](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691173450/aristotles-politics?srsltid=AfmBOor3BsPgZ4nwJsj5boQPzG2r-PoHSkzfCHigCGAkrd0PY74aF15u) to his _Politics –_ the _Economics_ or _Oeconomica_ (from _oikos_ , the home or household), a treatise on domesticity that has a lot more to say about the organisation of the home and gender roles than the _Politics_ does. But the authenticity of the _Economics_ as a work of Aristotle’s has been seriously challenged – for a variety of reasons. Book I, the discussion of private and public spheres, which describes the household and its members, is seen as a mishmash of combined elements of Aristotle’s _Politics_ and Xenophon’s _Economics_. There is evidence suggesting that Book II, which looks at different types of historical economic practices, was written in the early 3rd century BCE, after Aristotle’s death, and Book III, which discusses the relations between husband and wife, exists only in Latin, which makes it impossible to date.

The origins of the _Economics_ may be dubious, but this didn’t stop it being attributed to Aristotle for centuries and read with a certain reverence well into the 20th century. Then it disappeared from the list of easily available ancient texts– the last editions were published in 1876, translated by John Gillies, then in 1920, by Edward Seymour Forster. In 2011, Marcello Valente published an Italian translation and commentary of the text. While Valente does not say that Aristotle wrote the _Economics_ , he shows it’s unlikely that any other of the putative authors that had been put forward did either. Its authorship remains a mystery, but it is making a comeback in ancient philosophical scholarship.

Households come together to protect their wellbeing, not to achieve the ultimate goal of belonging to a political unit

Reading the _Economics_ brings something somewhat surprising into focus _:_ for ancient Greek philosophers, the home mattered. If the author was a student of Aristotle’s, then the home was something to be discussed at the Lyceum, where Aristotle and others trained aristocratic young men to become active thinkers. But also, what the _Economics_ has to say about the place of the home in the pursuit of human happiness is strikingly different from what we read in the _Politics_. It is in the _Politics_ that Aristotle argues that human beings must leave the household and go into the city-state if they are to engage their full capacities. The state, he says, is _prior_ to the household, by which he means it is more essential to our full development. Man, Aristotle explains, is a political animal, and this aspect of his nature cannot be nurtured within the household. Wives, children, slaves: these are not the proper subject matter for political debate. They do not possess the full rational abilities required to discuss justice authoritatively. So a free man needs to leave his home and meet up with others like him, if he is to flourish. The city state is the sphere where he can realise his full capabilities:

> We have already said, in the first part of this treatise, when discussing household management and the rule of a master, that man is by nature a political animal. And therefore, men, even when they do not require one another’s help, desire to live together; not but that they are also brought together by their common interests in so far as they each attain to any measure of wellbeing.

Book I of the _Economics_ tells a different story. It suggests that the home is constitutive of human development, rather than simply necessary for human survival:

> Now a city is an aggregate made up of households and land and property, self-sufficient with regard to a good life. This is clear from the fact that, if men cannot attain this end, the community is dissolved. Further, it is for this end that they associate together; and that for the sake of which any particular thing exists and has come into being is its substance. It is evident, therefore, that economics is _prior_ in origin to politics; for its function is _prior_ , since a household is part of a city. We must therefore examine economics and see what its function is. (My italics)

‘Aggregate’ here, means ‘a collection of many units’. Each household seeks the good life, and each achieves its goal by joining with many others and pooling their resources to form a city. If the arrangement doesn’t work, they separate again, and each household goes on its own way or goes to seek another group. The home, on this reading of the _Economics_ , comes first, and households come together or apart to protect their own wellbeing, not to achieve the ultimate human goal of belonging to a political unit. When she read this, Marinella observed that well-run homes were like well-run cities, and concluded that that, because a city was simply an agglomeration of homes, it was women who were responsible for the city being well run. The division of human life into two spheres did not rob women of their political role – it just meant that it was remote, and more discreet or hidden.

The _Economics_ was far from the only text of antiquity to tackle the question of home. First, there was Xenophon’s book of the same name, written slightly before Aristotle’s, and slightly less interesting. And later, Stoic writers took up the topic of the home with some enthusiasm, writing about marriage, the family and the education of women. The most notable among them were perhaps Musonius Rufus, a 1st-century CE Roman philosopher, and Hierocles the Stoic, a 2nd-century CE Greek philosopher. Then there is the whole corpus of texts by a group of philosophers known as the Neo-Pythagoreans, some signed by women, and many of them discussing the home, women’s virtues and marital relations. The home, it turns out, was a topic of considerable philosophical interest in antiquity.

Many of the fragments we have from Neo-Pythagorean writers who sign their texts with women’s names concern the ethics and politics of domestic arrangements: what to do if your husband cheats on you? How to deal with household slaves? Would a woman’s virtue enable her, in principle, to rule a state? These texts are commonly rejected by studies of ancient philosophers because they are hard to date, and quite likely written by writers using pseudonyms. But they are philosophical texts written in antiquity, and they are about the home, and I suspect that, if the home was regarded as a topic worthy of philosophical interests, they would be read today. The Neo-Pythagorean authors are the subject of several recent excellent and accessible books – eg, _Pythagorean_ _Women_ (2013) by Sarah Pomeroy, _Pythagorean Women Philosophers_ (2020) by Dorota Dutsch, _Pythagorean_ _Women_ (2022) by Caterina Pellò – so I would urge readers to consult these, and will now turn to Musonius and Hierocles.

Musonius Rufus had his 15 minutes of fame some 25 years ago when Martha Nussbaum noticed that he recommended women be educated as well as men. But while education does matter to gender relations, so does the home, and Musonius, it turns out, has a lot to say about that, as well as about education. First, he says that marriage is ‘community of life with a view to the procreation of children’. Then he goes further and explores what married life should be like, and what its legal and moral implications are. The marital ‘community’, he argues, must be material and emotional: a husband and wife must regard ‘all things in common between them, and nothing peculiar or private to one or the other, not even their own bodies.’ As well as sharing their material possessions, they must share affection: there should be ‘above all perfect companionship and mutual love of husband and wife’. If these conditions fail to obtain, ‘the union is doomed to disaster and though they live together, yet their common interests fare badly’.

Hierocles argues that marriage, rather than political association, constitutes the first human community

The home comes with duties for both husbands and wives, and this is where education comes in: men and women need to be virtuous in order to fulfil their duties, and the same virtues are required for both politics and household management. The main difference Musonius recognises between a man’s and a woman’s role is related to physical strength: men, because they are stronger, are better suited to working outdoors, whereas women, due to their weakness, are better living protected indoors. This only makes sense if we think of the husband as engaged in actual physical labour, rather than simply political activities, as Aristotle’s men were. Mopping the floor requires more physical strength than raising one’s arm to vote for a motion does.

Hierocles the Stoic is famous among ancient philosophers for his account of Stoic _oikeiôsis_ – moral development – as a progression through concentric circles starting from the desire for self-preservation to cosmopolitanism. Stoic moral development consists in first making oneself ‘at home’ in one’s body, then we reach out to those in the circles closest to us: parents and siblings, then to those farther from us – our other relatives and neighbours, our countrymen and women – and finally to the whole world. The concentric circles are important because they offer a clear and vivid explanation of what the Stoics mean when they declare that they owe moral allegiance to the entire world. So we often find Hierocles’ fragment cited in work on Stoic ethics or politics. But placing it outside of its context obscures another topic that mattered to Hierocles and should matter to us. The passage about the concentric circles comes from a series of fragments on how to treat one’s relatives, which is itself part of a work titled ‘On Appropriate Acts’. In these fragments, Hierocles argues that marriage, rather than political association, constitutes the first human community. A husband and wife, he says, need to work together, not simply alongside each other. They must supplement, rather than complement, each other. Although they have distinct tasks attributed to them by function of their gender – a husband works in the fields, the market place and in city business, while a wife spins wool, makes bread and carries out other domestic tasks – they also need to be able to help each other in their respective roles, and take over from each other whenever needed:

> And in truth, in festivals she [the wife] can take care of sacrifices and rites along with us; during her husband’s trips abroad she can maintain the house in order and not altogether without a manager; she can take charge of the slaves; she can be an assistant during illnesses – it would be a long story to go through everything one by one.

So while he accepts the traditional Greek view that outdoors jobs are better suited to men, Hierocles also argues that a woman should be taught to take care of her husband’s business while he is sick or travelling, and that a husband should be willing to take on domestic jobs. Indeed, he says, some are better suited to men’s physical strength:

> For of other domestic tasks should not one believe that most, indeed, are suitable to husbands rather than to wives? For some are very tiring and require bodily strength, such as grinding, kneading flour, splitting wood, drawing water, moving furniture, shaking out bedding, and all that is similar to these things.

Hierocles goes so far as to say that a man may enjoy the textile arts occasionally – provided he is secure in his masculinity:

> I myself would not advise any men who did not exhibit complete confidence in their own masculinity and restraint to touch such a thing. If, however, through a life of this kind he should have rendered himself free of every absurd suspicion, what will prevent a husband from sharing in these things too with his wife?

The spheres, for Hierocles – and to some extent for Musonius too – seem a lot more porous than they do for Aristotle. Society dictates that men and women observe their boundaries, so they do. But they can function well only if both husband and wife can do the work (or some of the work) in both spheres. Marriage is a partnership that requires working together, and while the Stoics agree with Aristotle that the role of the man is to acquire and the woman to preserve, they also need to be able to exchange roles when needed. Would a wife let fruit rot on a tree because her husband is travelling and unable to pick it? Would a husband let fruit go bad because the wife is ill and cannot make the jam? Of course, many households would have servants, or slaves, but being able to take over from one’s partner can save a lot of waste and trouble.

What can we learn from looking at these little-known, or, in the case of Aristotle’s _Economics_ , discredited texts? What all those texts and authors have in common is that they treat the home as something that matters, philosophically and for human flourishing. The home is not simply the domain of women’s work, and it is not impervious to politics or to the work that goes on outside the home. Strictly speaking, none of these texts is a feminist work. They still presuppose that women should mostly stay at home and men should mostly be in charge at home and outside. But they at least encourage readers to think about the home, and what goes on there.

The _Economics_ , decidedly, is not a feminist text. But it is more explicit than the _Politics_ as to the nature of the division of labour that is expected within a marriage. It brings the home to the forefront of philosophical debates. It forces us to take it seriously as a topic of thought, and to think of women as agents of human development, rather than simply support workers who feed their men so they may go about the important business of discussing justice.

Given the importance of ancient culture and philosophy in shaping our culture and philosophy, excluding those texts that discuss the home and women’s participation is a way of reinforcing the exclusion of women from the political and the philosophical arena. Reading Aristotle’s _Economics_ may have given us a chance to argue back, especially if we are prompted to reflect on the nature of marriage and family by looking at other philosophers such as Hierocles and Musonius Rufus.
