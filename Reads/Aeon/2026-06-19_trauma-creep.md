# Trauma creep

**Source:** Aeon  
**Author:** Lily Dunn  
**Date:** 2026-06-19  
**Link:** https://aeon.co/essays/not-everything-is-trauma-language-needs-to-mean-something

---

Last autumn, my mother was in a near-fatal car crash, when a massive electric BMW veered across the centre line and hit her head-on. After getting the call, I rushed to the trauma hospital and waited for the helicopter that was bringing her in. In a small, cube-shaped room I sat stunned for what felt like hours, staring at the door’s hinges. When the nurse brought me a cup of tea and handed me my mum’s mobile phone and her bag, the strap cut in half, fear suddenly caught up with me.

After I was led into the resuscitation area, time condensed, and I entered a vortex of panic watching doctors and nurses trying to stabilise my mother. She was hyperventilating and half her face was covered with gauze; her head was in a neck brace. I had no idea how bad the injuries were. Holding her hand, I became fixated by the blood on her teeth, with a distant thought that she might have internal bleeding. But she appeared to hear me as I calmly repeated that she must try to slow down her breathing.

There is a lot I don’t remember about the hours following, but I do remember my brother’s protective presence. He took over because I couldn’t bear to see them stitch together our mother’s split face. I remember his arm around me; the nurses passing me our mother’s jewellery; staring at the clog-like shoes moving about on the floor. I had forgotten where I’d parked my car, and once I started shivering, I could not stop.

It was useful when everyone around us used the word trauma. We were in a trauma hospital; her body had sustained multiple traumas, with breakages to eight ribs, her breastbone, lumbar spine, pelvis and shoulder as well as a trauma impact to her head. The language of the doctors and nurses was validating. After 10 days in the intensive care unit, our mother spent three weeks in the trauma ward with women who had broken bones from falling. According to many of the nurses, it was a miracle she was alive.

But as part of the Baby Boomer generation, my mother didn’t have ‘trauma’ in her lexicon. At times, my brother and I were astounded by how relentlessly positive she was. Despite not being able to move or sit up, let alone walk, she smiled and chatted away, making friends with all the staff. She’d say: ‘I have a lot of be grateful for. Imagine if we were in Gaza!’

It didn’t occur to me and my brother that we might be suffering from secondary post-traumatic stress disorder as we pushed through the crisis and tried to fix everything, until we both hit a wall and started to cry randomly, exhausted and overwhelmed. And yet I felt confronted by the younger generation’s easy use of this concept. I am a university lecturer and had to transfer my workshops online. I worked from my mother’s bedside, feeding her homemade smoothies from a straw while receiving emails from students saying they were not able to come to class because of the ‘trauma’ of their anxiety, or a bad day for their mental health. Increasingly, I was irritated.

I became abruptly aware of how my generation, Gen X, is caught at the centre of two extremes, with a parent who takes pride in her resilience and Gen Z students so reactive to potential difficulty as to consider everyday challenges triggering. One day I shouted: ‘I will no longer tolerate someone claiming it’s traumatic to break their acrylic nail!’ Then I felt guilty. How could I continue to mentor and teach younger generations with acceptance and empathy? To not slip into the propensity of our culture to see trauma in terms of hierarchy: to say _my mother’s trauma is far worse than yours_. Was my mother in denial, or does Gen Z need to get real by spending a month in a trauma ward?

The word trauma derives from the Greek word for ‘wound’, defined in pathological terms as early as 1684 in relation to a wound from an external cause. Despite the term expanding in the late 19th century to include psychic injury caused by emotional shock, its original definition stuck for most of the next century. But in a [paper](https://www.jstor.org/stable/27215635) published in 2020, the psychology researchers Nick Haslam and Melanie J McGrath note that ‘the broadest sense of trauma has become a great deal broader since then.’ The concept, in short, is creeping.

As a writer and teacher of memoir, I’m often grasping at ways to find form and story from traumatic memory, and so this rising and expanded use of the term is central to my interest. In the early 20th century, [Sigmund Freud’s](https://aeon.co/essays/every-school-of-psychology-has-its-own-theory-of-the-unconscious) theory of hysteria as a manifestation of repressed sexual trauma from early childhood helped to shift our understanding. A few decades earlier, the French psychologist Pierre Janet had pioneered the idea of dissociation in the context of ‘severely upsetting events [that] could generate pathological reactions not by physically damaging the body but by injuring the psyche,’ as Haslam and McGrath put it. By the late 1970s, the word trauma came to be used more colloquially in newspaper reports, to refer to damage or hurt rather than life-altering harm (the ‘trauma’ of strike action, for instance, in _The Daily Telegraph_ in 1981).

The dilution of harm-related terms reduces them to cliché and, at worst, renders them meaningless

Haslam and McGrath note that harm-related moral language shows a striking uptick from the 1980s onwards, reflecting ‘a growing cultural concern with harm and vulnerability’. Much of this concern followed the first inclusion of post-traumatic stress disorder in the third edition of the _Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders_ (_DSM-III_ , 1980), and it led to the steepest increase in the designation of trauma, understood as a psychic wound, appearing in texts throughout the 1990s. Still, the emphasis was on the severity of external events, like rape, assault and car accidents, whereas later revisions of the _DSM_ foregrounded the person’s subjective experience of distress in response to the trauma as opposed to its ‘objective’ severity. Trauma shifted from what is done to a person to how they respond to it.

Today, a simple scroll through Instagram turns up memes and viral videos with the word ‘trauma’ emblazoned in flashing letters. The concept has entered the mainstream lexicon of suffering only to have its meaning bleached from overuse. Haslam and McGrath call this semantic dilation ‘concept creep’. The UK mental health charity Mind offers a list of potential causes of trauma: you might feel ‘frightened; under threat; humiliated; rejected; abandoned; invalidated, for example [if] your feelings or views have been dismissed or denied’. You might then feel ‘unsafe; unsupported; trapped; ashamed; powerless’, as many of my undergraduate students have demonstrated. The definition of trauma has changed so significantly that, now, physicians differentiate between the vernacular use of ‘trauma’ with a small t and the official capital-T ‘Trauma’ of psychiatry. Other examples of harm-related semantic broadening include the terms abuse, addiction, bullying and narcissism.

It could be argued that a [broader understanding](https://psyche.co/ideas/a-broad-definition-of-trauma-is-useful-an-open-ended-one-isnt) of trauma extends open arms to more social groups – like movements around #MeToo, Black Lives Matter, and the neurodivergent community – which in turn leads to progressive social change, igniting our empathy for those who were previously denied it. But equally, the dilution of harm-related terms reduces them to cliché and, at worst, renders them meaningless. The vernacular is as homogenised as supermarket milk.

It also plays into a victim narrative. Returning to Mind’s bullet-point list of ‘trauma triggers’, I picture a person who is overpowered by their emotional response and lacking in strength or agency to protect themselves. Has the younger generation become so sensitive to external forces that they see serious harm in what past generations would have considered simple hurt, or, more cynically perhaps, is this an ideology that is driven by market forces? After all, where there is disease there must be a cure, and internet gurus, pharmaceuticals and trauma doctors step into the breach.

The literary memoir is guilty of abetting this cultural movement. Sometime in the 1980s and ’90s, the ‘biography of the somebody’ (usually male and successful) moved over to give way to the ‘memoir of the nobody’ (predominantly female or working class, damaged and victimised), much of which was termed ‘misery memoir’. Two early examples are Andrea Ashworth’s _Once in a House on Fire_(1998) and Kathryn Harrison’s _The Kiss_(1997). Writers were emboldened by a booming market to write their own true-life stories. What through shame or discretion would have previously been disguised as a novel (with strong autobiographical leanings – such as Antonia White’s _Frost in May_ (1933) or Sylvia Plath’s _The Bell Jar_ (1963) – now took shape as memoir.

![Cover of the book 'Once in a House on Fire' by Andrea Ashworth featuring an image of a girl's legs wearing one roller skate; a band aid on her knee.](https://images.aeonmedia.co/user_image_upload/4979/once-in-a-house-on-fire-.jpg?width=3840&quality=75&format=auto)

At the same time, we saw the beginnings of a popular self-help movement spearheaded by the ‘recovery movement’ and Alcoholics Anonymous’s much-publicised 12-step programme. And we witnessed the birth of the ‘media confessional’ on TV, with the likes of _The Oprah Winfrey Show_ and _Ricki Lake_ in the US, and _Richard & Judy_ in the UK, featuring guests sharing intimate personal stories from the sofa. Stories of unhappy families and of cheating and being cheated on became voyeuristic entertainment. In _The Limits of Autobiography_(2001), the feminist scholar Leigh Gilmore writes: ‘the age of memoir and the age of trauma have coincided.’ Suddenly, trauma had become a commodity in popular and literary culture, and the more shocking, the better.

The broadening of terms like addiction meant that everyone was suddenly recovering from something

In many ways, the memoir boom was positive. In _Memoir: An Introduction_ (2012), the disability studies scholar G Thomas Couser writes:

> if we think of life narratives … as collectively comprising a significant part of the historical record, we can see … ‘misery memoirs’ as revisionist, filling out and correcting earlier, partial accounts … The feel-good memoirs of middle-class family life published during the Depression and the immediate aftermath of World War II were never fully representative of actuality.

My grandmother was of that generation and a nurse during the war. She was the most resilient character I’ve known, but there were wounds and horrors she never spoke about. But my mother’s generation was the ‘feminist generation’ – in valuing women’s experiences, they helped forge the way for other underrepresented voices: people of colour; gay, queer, trans, neurodivergent and disabled people; and survivors of sexual and nonsexual violence.

Part of the appeal was that these voices belonged to the underdog, which played into the trope of victimhood. The US social critic Wendy Kaminer [questions](https://freshairarchive.org/segments/wendy-kaminer-dissects-self-help-culture) the recovery movement’s submissive ideology, which disempowers people by making ‘minor inconveniences into crises, in the same way that it makes every parental mistake into a form of abuse.’ It promotes the idea that we aren’t capable of dealing with life’s ordinary difficulties by ourselves. The broadening of terms like addiction, meanwhile, meant that everyone was suddenly recovering from something. More bait for the advertisers to step in and sell you a cure. Also, more material for the victim-narrative memoir. As a mentor of memoir, I too am guilty of pushing my students for the more extreme narrative, not merely because this is what publishers want, but because it is usually where the story’s tension lies.

It’s true that, of all literary forms, memoir best reflects the post-Freudian Western view that our unhappiness should be blamed on our parents. But what if the pendulum of parental blame and victim mentality has swung too far, giving rise to what the psychologist and family specialist Joshua Coleman calls an epidemic of detachment, in the [form of estrangement](https://aeon.co/essays/modern-culture-blames-parents-for-forces-beyond-their-control) between adult children and their parents – as we’ve seen recently with the fallout between the media personality Brooklyn Beckham and his family? According to Coleman, ‘therapy culture’ – with its tendency to blame ‘narcissistic’ parents and its labelling of childhood as ‘traumatic’ – coupled with the ‘psychobabble’ of the internet’s echo chamber only exacerbates the problem. In late-stage capitalism, everything is a potential marketing device that works, in cases like these, against empathy and understanding. Where being harmed has become a mark of tribal belonging or identity, garnering social approval and ‘likes’, the message is sobering: I am dysfunctional; you are dysfunctional; let’s show each other our scars. To which I want to caution that words are powerful and they must be used with care.

The integrative therapist Nova Nolan says the pendulum is important – it might swing to extremes, provoking discussion, but it then settles in the centre. As a therapist, Nolan understands that ‘the use of the word trauma is a signpost for the story beneath.’ She asks her clients: what is the feeling behind the word? Do you feel sad, angry, is your heart beating fast? And, in fact, I do something similar when mentoring writers, always reading between the lines to get at what is not said. What is it you _really_ want to say? Good therapy, like good writing, depends on attention and reflection, on noticing not only what is said, but also what is communicated unconsciously.

At the start of 2022, my teenage daughter got a secondary infection following COVID-19 and didn’t emerge from her bed for three months, which turned into her struggling with fatigue and mental illness for three years. As a family, we were thrown into turmoil. She was clearly weak and tired, unable to cope with full days at school, but, over time, it became difficult to separate the physical from the psychological, and to even know what had come first. Doctors, psychiatrists and counsellors queried us about past trauma, and I began to wonder at its definition. It was tough for her to live through her [parent’s separation](https://aeon.co/essays/a-stepfamily-can-rebuild-over-fault-lines-of-failure-and-loss), then to have to travel by plane to visit her dad in Amsterdam, taking responsibility for her younger brother (with assisted help), when she was just 10, and he eight. But was this traumatic?

And what about the pandemic? COVID-19 is commonly considered to have been a ‘mass’ or ‘collective’ trauma, a global ‘wound’ that affected the way we view the world and our security within it. But what about the impact on the developing teenage mind? Shut out of school, subject to enforced isolation, social media provided children a way of staying connected, even as it exposed them to pernicious and damaging ideas. My daughter was 15 when her illness struck. At her lowest point, she cried over how frightened she was of getting better. Her world had reduced to her bedroom, and she had fallen so far behind in school and with friends that the distance she had to jump simply felt impossible.

According to a [survey](https://digital.nhs.uk/data-and-information/publications/statistical/mental-health-of-children-and-young-people-in-england/2022-follow-up-to-the-2017-survey) from 2022, the number of children in England aged seven to 16 years affected by a mental health condition rose from one in nine to one in six during the pandemic. And a [study](https://acamh.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jcpp.13586) from the same year of London adolescents suggests those most affected either had pre-existing mental health problems, special educational needs, or were female. After being on the Bristol Child and Adolescent Mental Health Services unit’s waiting list for more than a year, we tried to get a private therapist for young people, and failed because they were all fully booked, mostly with teenage girls suffering from eating disorders, self-harm and/or suicidal ideation.

Memoir explodes this silence by finding unique symbols and language to express very specific experiences

The undergraduates I teach are likewise ‘Covid kids’ who came into their teenage years when the pandemic struck. Are they suffering a form of complex PTSD? Like trauma, the designation has become so overused that it’s easy to dismiss it as a catch-all phrase for the distress that many young people feel in their efforts to simply get by. Just as my daughter had, my students struggle with what is expected of young people in a world driven by money, technology and social media: the relentless pace of consumer culture; a competitive job market and inflated cost of living; scrolling and overstimulation; the isolation of feeling like you don’t fit in. The chiropractor Nina Hollingsworth tells me that she has seen a dramatic rise in young people using a walking stick because of the pain of fibromyalgia – a condition that many experts believe is a manifestation of PTSD, an over-protective pain response for the nervous system.

The psychologist Bessel van der Kolk’s bestselling book _The Body Keeps the Score_ (2014) mainlined the idea that untreated trauma lives in the body as physical symptoms and can progress to severe and chronic illness if untreated. I came across Van Der Kolk’s book reading Lucia Osborne-Crowley’s memoir _I Choose Elena_ (2019), about her profound physical response to a rape she had denied since her teens. With Van Der Kolk’s emphasis on the [lasting impacts](https://aeon.co/essays/how-bad-experiences-in-childhood-lead-to-adult-illness) of post-traumatic stress on the immune system, the nervous system, the muscular system and the brain, he puts the body back in the picture, helping us come full circle to return to trauma’s original definition, but with more understanding of its lasting bodily legacies.

Towards the end of _I Choose Elena_ , Osborne-Crowley writes:

> There are so many predators in the world, but silence itself may be chief among them. It is deadly. When it comes to recovery, silence is the knife edge between illness and health. Between dignity and indignity. For some, between life and death.

Memoir explodes this silence not by entering into vernacular labelling, or creating Instagram memes, but by finding unique symbols and language to express very specific experiences with depth and reflection. This can have powerful effects, particularly when speaking up incriminates others: witness #MeToo, which has done so much to advance our acceptance and understanding of the manipulative behaviours of predators and the complexity of a trauma victim’s response.

Kathryn Harrison was met with scorn when her memoir _The Kiss_(1997), about father-daughter incest, was published. She was accused of lying, of being an attention seeker, a traitor to her family. However, today, her book is considered to be a fine description of abusive power and a victim’s dissociative response to trauma: ‘I fall into what feels like a stupor,’ Harrison writes, ‘my essence distilled into a safe, impermeable core far within my body.’ Today’s readers are also not so shocked by her memoir’s content that they can’t appreciate its artfulness. They might even wonder whether creating an aesthetic from such trauma might not be transformative, even healing.

It’s through critical distance and creating an aesthetic that we finally untether ourselves and become free

When I wrote my own memoir, _Sins of My Father_(2022), my understanding of my past experience as abusive and potentially traumatic was a [slow dawning](https://aeon.co/essays/lost-innocence-the-children-whose-parents-joined-an-ashram) that came as a result of the writing itself, of sitting with my experience and researching it (and hearing other people’s similar stories), and then, after publication, having it reflected back in readers’ responses. My father had abandoned our family to join a ‘sex cult’ when I was six and, when I was still a minor, I was groomed and sexually abused by one of his friends. Before writing the book, my past had had a hold on me; embedded in my psyche, it affected the way I’d moved through the world, but writing helped me find image and symbol for my experiences and gave me full control over my story.

The best memoir goes beyond simple testimony to create a ‘third space’ where memory, imagination and meaning are transmuted into art. Personal experience is only the starting point. When [writing creatively](https://psyche.co/guides/to-start-to-heal-from-trauma-in-your-life-write-about-it), language discovers its own rules and conventions to give shape to the abstractions of emotion. As the author Jeanette Winterson puts it in _Art Objects_ (1995): ‘The bad writer believes that sincerity of feeling will be enough, and pins her faith on the power of experience. The true writer knows that feeling must give way to form.’ Only then can we name what we have experienced and find meaning in what we have named, but not as a sticking point. We might be drawn to writing to find meaning and identity, just as some of us are drawn to therapy, but it’s through critical distance and creating an aesthetic that we finally untether ourselves and become free.

As my daughter got better, I encouraged her not to hang on to the label of chronic fatigue because I believed it trapped her in a state of powerlessness, tied to something greater than her. Much as in the practice of [cognitive behavioural therapy](https://psyche.co/heal/cognitive-and-behavioural-therapies), it felt important to acknowledge the struggle and all she had lost, but then look forward and put into place ways of aiding her recovery. As Haslam and McGrath explore in their hypothesis of concept creep: ‘People who believe they harbour a deep and defining psychic wound are less likely to have hopes for future recovery, less likely to recognise and exercise their agency, and more likely to embrace a victim identity.’ I did not want this to be my daughter’s narrative.

My mother is of the generation whose grandfathers fought in the First World War, when soldiers who deserted or refused to fight because they could not cope were executed by their own side. There was not yet an understanding of what is obviously recognisable to us now as post-traumatic stress. These were men who had watched their friends die of horrific injuries, who were sent insane with grief and the fear that they would be next – yet whose letters home largely hid it all, continuing the silence. There was simply no language for what they felt: mental collapse was considered [a moral weakness](https://aeon.co/essays/why-it-s-brave-and-prudent-to-think-like-a-coward) that deserved punishment. Even when ‘shell shock’ was first recognised in print in 1915, it was understood as the brain’s hidden physical damage, rather than an emotional and psychological response to threat.

Perhaps this generational, stiff-upper-lipped inheritance is why my mother first resisted acknowledging the psychological after-effects of what had happened to her in the car crash. She got through her time in hospital by projecting outwards rather than by dwelling on her misfortune. Yes, there were times in the night when she felt desperate, fearful that she would not make it out of the hospital alive, but still she wouldn’t give in to it, and pushed the fearful thoughts away, knowing she would wake up to a new day, when the nurses started their shifts and she could focus on recalling all their names and their families. Traumatic response to a situation depends on the circumstances of the rupture and on the quality of [acknowledgement and care](https://psyche.co/ideas/from-wars-to-pandemics-people-in-crisis-need-to-feel-connected). My mother was lucky to have a large family rally around her, and brilliant doctors and nurses, but I couldn’t help noticing how stoic she was and how much that [stoicism helped](https://psyche.co/ideas/the-stoics-were-right-emotional-control-is-good-for-the-soul) her recovery.

My mother now acknowledges the psychological effects of her accident

But stoicism and resilience can be problematic too. My generation are perhaps more reactive because of the inattention of our Boomer parents and are also more enmeshed with our children as a result. At the same time, after hearing of the epidemic of estrangement, I can understand how a young adult – the same generation as my children – might be driven to feel they can gain independence only by breaking with parents who constantly check up on them via text, or follow their live locations on ‘family sharing’ apps. The trauma therapist Jeremy Fox has commented that Gen Z generally lack life experience and have not learned to build resilience from exposure, and that this lack of exposure can itself lead to anxiety and depression.

The car accident destroyed my mother’s life as she knew it. She has not been able to return to her beautiful home, and now her house is on the market. And she no longer lives with her husband, who has been forced into care prematurely. They lost their beloved dog and their local friends. But she is living with me and my family now, and continues to make a remarkable recovery. Within weeks of my mother’s accident, my daughter shed her old skin and moved to London to start university, overcoming her attachment to chronic fatigue with the help of her new love for photography and creative direction. We lost one household member and gained another.

With the help of a psychologist, my mother now acknowledges the psychological effects of her accident that manifest in a reflex response to loud noises or sudden movement. The other day, I saw her jump out of her seat in response to my son suddenly shouting up at me from her right-hand side, the same side as the crash. ‘I’ve got post-traumatic stress disorder!’ she told him. I stopped what I was doing and smiled.
