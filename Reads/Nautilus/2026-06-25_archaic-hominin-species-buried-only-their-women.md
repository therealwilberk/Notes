# Archaic Hominin Species Buried Only Their Women

**Source:** Nautilus  
**Author:** Jake Currie  
**Date:** 2026-06-25  
**Link:** https://nautil.us/archaic-hominin-species-buried-only-their-women-1282257/

---

In an eyebrow-raising Facebook post in 2013, paleontologist Lee Berger made [an interesting overture](https://nautil.us/the-man-who-used-facebook-to-find-an-extinct-human-species-235692). He needed a team of people with paleontology and caving experience, with one other stipulation: They had to be small. Berger was assembling a team to descend into the Dinaledi Chamber of the Rising Star Cave system in South Africa, a journey that required squeezing through claustrophobic spaces (some only as large as seven inches). Inside were the bones of several members of a roughly 300,000-year-old hominin species previously unknown to science. 

**Read more: “[The Beloved Mesolithic Girl](https://nautil.us/the-beloved-mesolithic-girl-238374)”**

Six women, dubbed “underground astronauts” in a nod to the risky subterranean mission, were selected to excavate the site. The bones they recovered belonged to the _Homo naledi_ , which had a curious mix of modern and archaic features. _H. naledi_ walked upright and had human-like hands and faces, but also smaller skulls like _Australopithecus_. Strangely, they seemed to lack any sexual dimorphism, something that puzzled the researchers for more than a decade. Now, a new analysis of their remains [published](https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674\(26\)00644-6) in _Cell_ has solved the mystery—just like the underground astronauts who recovered their bones, they were all female.

![](https://lede-admin.nautil.us/wp-content/uploads/sites/70/2026/06/Currie_BREAKER_b46019.png?w=710)**TIGHT SQUEEZE:** This illustration highlights just how narrow the entrance to the Dinaledi Chamber really is. _Credit: Paul H. G. M. Dirks et al._

An international team of researchers from a variety of disciplines made the startling discovery using a [cutting-edge method](https://nautil.us/ancient-teeth-hint-at-homo-erectus-denisovan-interbreeding-1280849) to analyze the proteins contained within _H. naledi_ teeth. By carefully extracting samples using acid, they were able to determine the sequence of amino acids in peptide fragments of amelogenin, a protein involved in enamel formation. Amelogenin comes in two different isoforms: amelogenin-X from the X chromosome and amelogenin-Y from the Y chromosome. Normally, male teeth show a mix of 90 percent amelogenin-X and 10 percent amelogenin-Y, but there was no amelogenin-Y present in any of the _H. naledi_ teeth. 

But why only females? 

Researchers believe the bodies, which include both adults and children, were placed in the Dinaledi Chamber _because_ they were female, as part of a ritualized burial practice. If so, the implications are staggering. _H.naledi_ could be the first non-human (or Neanderthal) species known to lay their dead to rest, a rite that hints at a larger culture, now lost to time. ![](https://assets.nautil.us/sites/3/nautilus/nautilus-favicon-14.png?fm=png)
