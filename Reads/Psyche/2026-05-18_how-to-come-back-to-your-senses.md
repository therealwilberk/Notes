# How to come back to your senses

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Tereza Violet Stehlíková  
**Date:** 2026-05-18  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/guides/how-to-come-back-to-your-senses

Do you ever have the feeling, after spending hours on a computer – engaged in meetings, replying to endless emails, or watching video after video – that you are oddly absent from your body? Perhaps you feel disconnected from your immediate surroundings, having been sucked into a virtual dimension for the whole afternoon. Maybe you forgot to drink water, and now you’re very thirsty and developing a headache. Or you suddenly realise how stiff your body has become, and now you feel all the tensions running through you. Time has passed, you’ve ticked off tasks or taken in a bunch of information, and yet there is a lingering sense that you haven’t really been present.

Most of us now spend a large part of our lives in digital environments. What used to involve a diversity of gestures and tools (dialling a phone, writing a letter, taking a photograph, searching in a book) has been compressed into taps on a single device. It is efficient, yes. But this streamlining comes with a cost: it reduces the richness of our everyday sensory life. More and more of our actions happen in a kind of placeless elsewhere, and we are rarely fully present in the here and now.

It was during the COVID-19 pandemic lockdowns that this drift out of the body became painfully clear to me. Here I was, teaching through my laptop, speaking for hours each week to black squares on a screen, hoping there was still someone listening. The nourishing in-person encounters that had anchored my days were gone. In this atmosphere, I began to consider what life would be like if it proceeded toward ever greater physical disconnection. I pictured a woman who lives almost entirely through her screens, doing all of her work and socialising online. She hasn’t touched another person for a long time. I began to ask: how does she feel in her body? What happens to her sense of reality when nearly all of her experiences are mediated by a screen?

To explore what prolonged sensory deprivation might do to someone, I spoke with researchers and fellow artists about it. Again and again, a theme emerged. People underestimate how deeply involved all of our senses are – not only vision and hearing, but touch, taste, smell, balance and movement – in making us feel real and connected to others, and how the different spaces we move through daily help us to know ourselves better.

To my relief, I’ve found that we do not have to stay exiled from our bodies. It is possible, even within a screen-saturated life, to find a way back home.

In this Guide, I’ll share a series of simple exercises I’ve developed over many years of exploring sensory perception in my artistic and pedagogical practice. I turned to these exercises myself during the COVID-19 lockdowns as a way of feeling more grounded and alive in an increasingly virtual world. I have since used them in my teaching too, where they have prompted very positive feedback. They are designed to help you restore some of the small delights of multisensory aliveness – and to feel a little more real and present in your own life.

### Open up a sensory toolbox

This exercise can be done at any time, but might be especially useful before, during or after a day spent on a computer. It is meant to help you reconnect with your sensing body. The duration is up to you, but I recommend not rushing this, to give yourself the benefit of focus.

Before you start, prepare your own sensory toolbox. The components listed here are just suggestions, and you can adapt them according to your own needs and what’s available:

  * a small bowl of uncooked rice (can also be dry lentils, couscous, etc)
  * a bowl of water
  * a fragrant herb or herbs – fresh or dry (coriander, lavender, etc)
  * a slice of a lemon or lime
  * a glass of peppermint tea (or any other herbal tea you like, warm or cold)



Once you have your toolbox assembled, allow yourself to experience the sensations of each component, one at a time:

  * Dip your hand into the bowl of rice, feeling its texture, consistency and temperature. Pick up a handful and let it slowly fall between your fingers. Take your time just enjoying this tactile experience.
  * Slowly submerge your fingertips into the water. Notice how it feels, including the contrast between the submerged part of your fingers and the part that is out.
  * Pick up the herb and take in a deep breath. Pay attention to the scent. What does it remind you of? You may have a few different herbs, so you can compare these different olfactory sensations.
  * Lift the sliced lemon to your nose. Pay attention to the smell: how does it feel? Sharp? Round? Refreshing? Now lick the lemon and think about the taste. It is sour, but what else? Does it make your mouth curl? How does your tongue feel?
  * Finally, take a slow sip of the tea. Can you feel it as it passes through your body?



Note: closing your eyes during this exercise can help you focus more on each sensory experience.

This small ritual gently reawakens your senses and reminds you that you are not just a pair of eyes at a screen or the swipe of a finger on glass.

### Practise tactile attention

It’s easy to fool yourself into thinking you can know something simply by looking at it. But looking does not always mean seeing. This exercise is designed to make you appreciate what the fingers perceive, and what we can miss when we don’t take time to pay attention.

Find an object for your tactile exploration. It can be anything. You can do this exercise with an object that you choose yourself, but if you want to make it even more interesting, ask a friend or family member to choose the object for you, without showing it to you first. This enhances the exercise because the more ambiguous the object is to you, the more intriguing the experience can be. I’ve found great success in using semi-broken children’s toys, where the identity of the object cannot be easily guessed. Another option is to ask somebody to combine a few objects together (a kind of readymade), so that there are different textures and elements.

Close your eyes (or use a scarf as a blindfold) and explore the object through touch only. As you do this, consider the reciprocity of touch: as you touch, the object is touching you back. What do you learn about the object this way? Make sure to give yourself enough time for this – I suggest at least three minutes. You can set a timer on your phone.

If you’d like, you can even try to draw the object from memory before seeing it. You will be surprised and perhaps even delighted by how your touch ‘sees’.

Touch without sight transforms an object into an unfamiliar landscape. It trains your tactile attention to notice subtle differences in texture, shape and temperature, while also revealing hidden facets of everyday reality.

### Have a tactile conversation

If you want to take your tactile exploration further, here’s another exercise you can try. This one requires the participation of a friend or family member. You’ll also need some plasticine or clay, and ideally a scarf each to blindfold yourselves.

Sit facing each other, within reach. Each person takes a lump of clay and then puts on their blindfold. Begin to explore the material. Shape it, guided by your desire. It is a deeply pleasurable process. Ideally, you should not speak while you do this.

After about five minutes, you can swap the pieces. Now you have somebody else’s object and are able to feel the tactile imprints in the material. Respond to this shape by altering it, according to your own wish. Then swap again.

After a few more swaps, you can join the materials together and collaborate through touch, co-creating. There is no specific aim in mind – only the process, the tactile dialogue. In the end, you can place the object on the floor, take off your blindfolds and see your joint creation.

Immersing yourself in this kind of activity brings you back to felt presence – and highlights how communication happens on many levels, beyond words.

### Explore the space around you

Small changes in how your body is occupying space – in your position, posture or point of view – can reveal the influence this has on your thoughts and feelings.

Here are some easy tasks to get you exploring:

  * As you are reading this, notice your position right now.
  * Get up and stand in the middle of a room. How does it feel? Exposed? Freeing in some way?
  * Go and stand next to a wall, with your back to it. How does that feel? Do you feel safer, perhaps?
  * Lie down in the middle of the floor. How does this transform your point of view? You’ll certainly feel less formal, and different kinds of thoughts might come to you compared with when you stand up. Different bodily positions can also unlock different emotional states.
  * Now try a minor act of subversion: go to your desk or a table, climb under it, and sit there for a while. How does it make you feel? Safe? Strange? Like a child?



This exercise – and the ones that follow – help to recalibrate your attention and show how even ordinary environments can become intriguing adventures when you move through them differently.

### Go for focused walks

Sometimes, when you’re feeling stuck in your own head, the best solution is to get up and take a walk. Moving through different spaces is enlivening because each environment affects you differently and, in turn, you might learn something about yourself – for example, whether a narrow passage makes you feel confined or held, or an open view makes you feel smaller or freer. For years, I have explored how a simple walk can be reframed to get the whole body, with all of its senses, more consciously involved. What follows are a few variations on this type of exercise.

#### Sensory walk

Plan a simple walk where you pay attention to one sense specifically, carefully noticing what you touch, or smell, or hear, or see.  
  
You can start with touch. But remember, touch includes not only the active touch of your hands or feet on surfaces, but also passive touch: the feeling of wind, the sense of temperature, humidity. It also involves proprioception, your sense of where the different parts of your body are in space (such as knowing where your feet are landing without looking), and kinaesthesia, your sense of movement (such as noticing the rhythm of your stride, or the shift of weight as you walk uphill).

The next time, you might focus on smell. If it is dry outside, you can bring a water bottle or a spray bottle, because moisture or humidity can enhance how smells are perceived. You might need to crouch down or come closer to surfaces to give your nose a chance. If you think you can’t smell anything, don’t give up. With practice, you can train yourself to notice smells that you might otherwise miss.

Note: if you walk with a friend, you could each focus on a different sense, take notes, and discuss your findings later. If you want to be even more ambitious, you can make a map charting your impressions.

#### Infra-ordinary walk

This approach borrows a little from psychogeography (which describes the effect of environments on emotions and behaviour) and also from the writer Georges Perec’s idea of the _infra-ordinary._ This term refers to the habitual, unnoticed aspects of everyday life, the things so familiar that they become invisible.

The infra-ordinary walk consists of a simple decision to deviate from the habitual, most efficient route. Where you’d normally take the first left, take the next one. How does it feel to arrive at your office or home from a different direction, or after a little meander? Sometimes the tiniest shift out of the routine can inspire new ways of sensing, while opening a feeling of wonder at the everyday.

To help, you can also introduce an attention-framing technique: use something like a cardboard tube, or your hand, to frame a portion of the view, or a piece of coloured acetate to bring a filter to your reality – a little like an Instagram filter, only in real time. Using this technique when taking a walk can help you focus on overlooked details, the beauty of the infra-ordinary.

#### Blindfolded walk

For this, you’ll again need to pair up with a trusted partner. Ask your partner to choose a route for you this time, to ensure you’ll be surprised by what you experience. They should consider safety, of course (especially traffic, etc), but also nuance: I like walks that have differing soundscapes, textures underfoot or even air temperatures (as in interior/exterior spaces).

Tie a scarf over your eyes to prevent peeking. Ask your partner to lead you, ideally keeping an arm over your back. They should allow for a slow pace, incorporating a few exploratory pauses, to let you explore unguided, while supervising you.

This exercise may produce both a sense of re-enlivenment of everyday reality as well as a deepening of your emotional bond with the person who guides you. Based on my experience with doing this in varied contexts, many people cherish the moments when they can let go of being ‘in charge’, trusting their guide instead.
