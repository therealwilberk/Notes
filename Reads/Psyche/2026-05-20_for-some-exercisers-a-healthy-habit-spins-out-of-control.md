# For some exercisers, a healthy habit spins out of control

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Ashrene Rathilal  
**Date:** 2026-05-20  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/ideas/for-some-exercisers-a-healthy-habit-spins-out-of-control

---

Picture a woman staggering out of a 5 am spin class, legs trembling, face drawn. A man who keeps adding a few extra kilometres to his run because it doesn’t feel like enough. Or a teen who restarts their gym routine if their fitness watch glitches, because otherwise ‘it doesn’t count’. Would you be surprised if each person collects a stream of admiring comments from friends or the people they meet while working out? ‘You’re so disciplined.’ ‘I wish I had your motivation.’ Many of us know and applaud people like them.

But for some highly dedicated exercisers, beneath all the praise and the appearance of being in control, there are troubling signs. Private feelings of exhaustion. A rigid set of demands. A wave of guilt for thinking of skipping tomorrow’s session.

This is the paradox that exercise and sport psychologists are increasingly asked to navigate. Exercise is widely recognised as a form of stress relief and self-care, and people who commit themselves to a routine are admired for it. How does it turn into something damaging?

The unhealthy pattern commonly described as exercise addiction (or exercise dependence) can begin unremarkably enough. At first, a person might decide to run just a little more or lift a little heavier. The exercise routine steadies them emotionally. Someone with anxiety, for example, might start adding an extra 20 minutes to their training sessions and feel immediate relief. The routine becomes the anchor that holds the day in place. But over time, the anchor grows heavier. The workouts start to be seen as the only reliable way to manage emotions, stress and frustrations. Tension builds, training provides relief. The routine hardens.

Typically, someone who has developed an exercise addiction plans their life around their workouts. They might wake before dawn to train because the idea of missing a session feels intolerable. Taking a rest day, usually a welcome pause, breeds irritation or guilt. The run or bike ride starts to feel like an obligation that must be met to regain a sense of equilibrium. A major motivation to exercise becomes avoiding withdrawal symptoms – such as irritability, disturbed sleeping patterns, or anxiety – that one experiences without it.

Eventually, compulsive exercise can have adverse effects on a person’s physical, psychological and social wellbeing. The runner who keeps adding ‘just a few more kilometres’ may find himself treating minor injuries and fractures that never quite heal. The teen who doubles up on gym sessions could struggle with persistent fatigue. Other compulsive exercisers might frequently decline social invitations that interfere with their routine, frustrating friends and family. The exercise routine can reshape and shrink the rest of someone’s life.

In a world that often feels unstable, an exercise routine can feel like one of the aspects of life over which someone has control

While exercise addiction is not yet recognised as an official category of addictive disorder in the _Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders_ (_DSM_), there is ongoing research (including [work](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S221126692500088X) by my colleagues and me) aimed at better understanding what it is and who is at risk. These studies point to a number of background factors that might make it more likely that a frequent exerciser falls into this pattern. Compared with others, people at risk of exercise addiction seem to be more likely to have symptoms of at least one mental [disorder](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352853220301292), such as an eating disorder. Individuals who have [obsessive-compulsive](https://doi.org/10.3390/bs13100857) tendencies may be vulnerable, especially when the structure of training offers the soothing predictability that they struggle to find elsewhere. Similarly, those who are highly [perfectionistic](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11469-022-00943-6) might be at increased risk due to the structure and rigidity that exercise can provide.

In a world that often feels unstable – emotionally, economically, environmentally – an exercise routine can feel like one of the few aspects of life over which someone has complete control. One group of researchers has [documented](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11469-020-00264-6) and [analysed](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11469-025-01549-4) dozens of publicly reported stories of apparent exercise addiction. These accounts repeatedly describe a pattern where someone uses exercise to regulate emotional turmoil but, in case after case, people eventually find that the pursuit of control controls them. ‘These stories suggest that the condition is not simply about excessive willpower or ambition but rather a pattern of compulsive behaviour driven by psychological dependency, where the intention to change is frequently overwhelmed by the urge to continue,’ Attila Szabo and colleagues write.

In one of the accounts they reviewed, James McWilliams [described](https://psmag.com/social-justice/seduction-addiction-runners-confession-75187/) how his relationship with running shifted from a healthy habit into something more addiction-like. Over time, he felt compelled to run regardless of illness, exhaustion or risk of injury, and needed ‘more and more miles to experience the benefits’.

Part of the trouble with exercise addiction is that, as it takes root, it might not seem like a problem. Other people’s praise and body-focused feedback serve as encouragements. Modern fitness norms often disguise unhealthy patterns with the language of virtue, making it difficult for someone to recognise when they’ve crossed from commitment into compulsion. Online fitness culture and social media trends, such as #NoDaysOff, #NoExcuses and #Gymaholic, likely help perpetuate unhealthy exercise patterns. ‘People find themselves shaming and blaming themselves for not being able to recover sufficiently or white-knuckle their way through a workout,’ Clara Swedlund, a sport and exercise psychologist, told me.  
  
Along with the visible behavioural patterns of exercise addiction, there is a story about identity and meaning. The psychological framework [called](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/1479-5868-9-78) self-determination theory suggests that our healthiest motivations grow from three basic needs: the need for autonomy, for mastery, and for a sense of connection. Physical activity feels deeply satisfying when it is chosen freely, pursued with curiosity, and integrated into a full life. The trouble happens when these motivations collapse into pressure, and it feels as if one no longer chooses the routine but is instead driven to it.

For many dedicated exercisers, working out becomes part of their identity and something they’re known for. In exercise addiction, it becomes a central pillar of self-definition. Someone might question who they are unless they track their reps or miles run. As Swedlund says, for some individuals, self-worth is ‘built upon being the fit friend, being the person who exercises – and letting go of that, and moving away from that, [feels] really risky.’

What does the path back to healthy behaviour look like? For people addicted to exercise, the idea of easing off can feel like a threat to their identity. Yet recovery doesn’t require abandoning exercise altogether or stepping away from sport.

A therapist who is working with a compulsive exerciser might draw on concepts such as psychological flexibility, a key idea in acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT). ACT involves developing the ability to notice [uncomfortable thoughts](https://psyche.co/guides/instead-of-wrestling-with-self-defeating-thoughts-try-this) and feelings without immediately reacting to them. For instance, instead of obeying an inner voice that tells someone they are lazy if they don’t stick to their intense workout schedule, they can learn to pause, acknowledge the feelings of anxiety or guilt, and make a choice that reflects the bigger picture, including their health, relationships or long-term wellbeing. Practising [self-compassion](https://psyche.co/guides/how-to-be-kinder-to-yourself-by-practising-self-compassion) can work alongside this by softening that harsh inner critic. According to Swedlund, for someone who is over-exercising, ‘we need to be able to support them with … identifying who they are,_as well as_ somebody who exercises.’

What is clear, both from clinical accounts and personal stories, is that recovery is possible

To help someone understand the emotional dynamics underlying their exercise routines, a therapist might prompt them to reflect on questions, such as: what is exercise doing for me? What emotions and feelings am I soothing or provoking through movement? These reflections could help loosen the rigid meanings that exercise has accumulated over time.  
  
Therapists may help clients experiment with reducing their exercise in manageable ways. For example, introducing an actual rest day, shortening a session, or resisting the urge to ‘make up’ a missed workout. A therapist can also help someone work on tolerating the discomfort that surfaces when they loosen control – the anxiety, restlessness or guilt that can flare up when a routine is interrupted. This might involve practising alternative ways to manage stress, such as breathing exercises, art therapy, mindfulness, social connection or activities that provide meaning beyond exercising. Ultimately, the process can be seen as less about reducing exercise than about expanding flexibility: learning that wellbeing doesn’t collapse when a workout is skipped, and that identity can stretch beyond the gym.

Research on recovery from exercise addiction is still emerging. There isn’t yet a standard treatment, and much of what we know comes from small clinical studies and related fields such as eating disorders and behavioural addictions. Approaches such as cognitive behavioural therapy, ACT, and interventions that address perfectionism and self-worth hold promise.

What is clear, both from clinical accounts and personal stories, is that recovery is possible. With support, people can learn to rebuild their relationship with exercise. Running can become a source of pleasure again, rather than a punishment; weightlifting a form of self-development rather than an obligation. In time, the return to healthy exercise may show in a missed workout that no longer sparks panic, a rest day that feels restorative instead of threatening, and a life that begins to widen again.
