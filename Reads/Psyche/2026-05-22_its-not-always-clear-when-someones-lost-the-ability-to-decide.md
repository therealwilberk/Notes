# It’s not always clear when someone’s lost the ability to decide

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Jessica M Choplin  
**Date:** 2026-05-22  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/ideas/its-not-always-clear-when-someones-lost-the-ability-to-decide

---

Raymond Jones wasn’t using the door. He was climbing through the windows of his home in Oshkosh, Wisconsin. It was then, according to court documents, that his neighbours called the sheriff. When a deputy arrived, Jones couldn’t name the current president and said the year was 2055. He was placed on an emergency hold, taken into custody for psychiatric evaluation. But the decline had, in fact, been building for months: among other things, he’d been found pounding on a stranger’s door, shouting that his wife (who was long dead) had left him, and he had turned up at a medical centre searching for a non-existent friend.

In the weeks before his evaluation, Jones had also sold some parcels of land. When a buyer offered him $100,000 for 30 acres that were appraised at $155,000, Jones initially refused – then returned days later to accept. Not long before the closing on Jones’s property, a neighbour called social services, concerned about Jones’s confusing recent behaviours. On the day after the closing, Jones drove into traffic on a highway and, again, couldn’t recall the president or year.

The land sales went forward anyway, and his son brought a [lawsuit](https://www.courtlistener.com/opinion/8285884/jones-v-pizon/). The buyer of the land conceded that Jones had dementia symptoms before and after, but not _during_ the sale proceedings. At trial, he put forward witnesses who said they saw no sign of decline. A lifelong friend of Jones, who went with him, said he was ‘excited to make the deal’. Two closing agents said Jones had no trouble understanding the process. How could that be the case? The defence argued that Jones had been experiencing a ‘lucid interval’, or a temporary restoration of mental capacity – a concept long recognised by the law in his state. On that basis, they claimed the sales were valid and enforceable.

When the idea that someone had a lucid interval is invoked in court, it is usually in cases where people with dementia or other cognitive disorders have signed binding agreements that have serious consequences, such as those involving wills, powers of attorney and real-estate sales. The concept appears not only in the United States, but across the English-speaking world, including Canada, the United Kingdom, New Zealand and Australia. The assumption is that the capacity to make decisions can disappear, then return – briefly, but fully – and then disappear again.

Typically, however, clinicians and psychologists do not describe decision-making that way. In the Jones case, a clinical psychologist testified that the man’s condition during the transactions was not what it seemed. Jones, he said, showed ‘apparent lucidity’ but remained incapable of making a decision about his property.

For families caring for ageing relatives – and for anyone thinking about their own future autonomy – the question is whether agreements reached during moments when someone appears lucid should count as fully consensual, or whether those moments merely conceal the loss of decision-making capacity.

Although a flicker of clarity might resemble capacity, research [suggests](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26438805/) that so-called lucid intervals in dementia really are only brief fluctuations in attention or alertness. Lasting for just seconds or minutes, these states seem to be too unstable to support consent. A patient might appear to be alert during these intervals while still remaining unable to grasp options, weigh multiple consequences, or hold a decision in mind.

In one [study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11044778/) that tested people with dementia using a variety of behavioural response-time tasks and physiological measures, performance bounced up and down from one moment to the next. These shifts showed brief sparks of alertness, momentary engagement and the appearance of being ‘with it’, but not sustained focus. That distinction matters for real-world decisions. A contract demands more than surface-level clarity: it requires holding multiple terms and consequences in mind at once, and integrating how a single term can help in one way, harm in another, and create additional consequences. A brief increase in engagement does not equate to the focus needed to make binding decisions.

These intervals are not altogether meaningless. They could be valuable for loved ones

Similarly, another [study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24151106/) found that moments of seeming alertness did not necessarily align with improvements in cognitive ability. Researchers tracked people with dementia using simple tests such as recalling strings of numbers. Here, too, performance jumped up and down unpredictably – five digits remembered one moment, only two the next – regardless of how awake or engaged the person appeared to be. What looked like lucidity did not bring sustained reasoning or memory ability.

In some moments, attention might temporarily focus, and alertness may return. These intervals are not altogether meaningless. They could be valuable for loved ones: a chance to reconnect or to feel the person’s presence in ways they might not usually be able to – as if the person with dementia is momentarily ‘back’. But the research literature indicates that these changes do not amount to a restoration of the person’s ability to make decisions.

The court ultimately found that Jones lacked capacity when he sold his land. But the legal doctrine of lucid intervals had created legal exposure – a hurdle that his son needed to overcome in court – and in other [recent](https://law.justia.com/cases/kentucky/court-of-appeals/2024/2023-ca-0965-mr.html) [cases](https://law.justia.com/cases/alabama/supreme-court/2025/sc-2025-0303.html), the concept has been cited in upholding an individual’s decision. Court is a far from ideal place to untangle decisions made under dementia. The outcome is too uncertain.

What can be done instead? Some families of people with dementia resort to the legal arrangement of guardianship, which hands the person’s decision-making to another. But this option can be even [worse](https://scholarship.law.upenn.edu/jlasc/vol24/iss2/2/) than litigation for the individuals who have lost their decision-making capacity. Guardianship strips away legal personhood. The preservation of a person’s autonomy matters. It is tied to dignity, to identity, to the right to choose for oneself. Older adults with dementia or other cognitive disorders deserve that right as much as anyone.

An alternative pathway for people like Jones and the families who care about them involves [planning](https://www.alz.org/help-support/caregiving/financial-legal-planning/planning-ahead-for-legal-matters) early, while memory and judgment still mostly hold. Rather than taking someone’s decisions away from them, the goal is to [prepare](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10282104/) for moments when the person might sound clear and agreeable on the surface, but no longer fully understands what they are agreeing to.

During early-stage dementia, people often can still notice where thinking feels harder

A person with cognitive decline might, for instance, be able to sign their name and describe what they are doing, yet [no longer](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13607863.2024.2338199) understand the entire transaction. A second set of eyes – what’s sometimes [called](https://files.consumerfinance.gov/f/documents/cfpb_considering-a-financial-caregiver-know-your-options_guide_2021-05.pdf) a financial caregiver – provides a source of protection. A joint account that requires two signatures serves a similar purpose when someone with dementia can no longer determine who is trustworthy and social cues replace careful judgment.  
  
Comparable safeguards might be needed in medical settings. A patient may nod, ask polite questions, and say ‘yes’ while failing to actually retain or weigh the information given. Having a loved one sit in on medical visits could be a valuable safeguard. A [power of attorney](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/ask-cfpb/what-is-a-power-of-attorney-poa-en-1149/) document can be drafted and filed away until the day it is truly needed. For many people in the early stages of cognitive decline, setting up protections such as these is a way to guard their future selves, ensuring that later moments of apparent clarity are not mistaken for informed decisions.

Working out which supports make sense is itself a complex decision, one that needs to be made as [early](https://www.alzheimers.gov/life-with-dementia/planning-after-diagnosis) as possible. Following a diagnosis of mild cognitive impairment, or during early-stage dementia, people often can still [notice](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9169465/) where thinking feels harder, where decisions take more effort, and where a second voice would help. The aim is to ensure that future decisions reflect the person’s values. Having the conversation sooner rather than later allows those priorities to be stated plainly, while the person can recognise their own limits and plan for them.

Supported decision-making agreements, now recognised in [multiple](https://sdmny.org/supported-decision-making-legislation/supported-decision-making-agreement-legislation-in-the-u-s-and-elsewhere/supported-decision-making-agreement-laws-in-the-u-s/) US states, offer another way to establish protection. These allow an older adult to designate trusted supporters to help them weigh options, review consequences, and guard against exploitation. Unlike guardianship, they do not erase a person’s legal autonomy. Jones’s state of Wisconsin recognised supported decision-making agreements in 2017, some two years after the events described above. The statute makes clear that the final choice remains with the impaired adult.

Had this law been in place earlier, Jones could have named his son or another trusted supporter to help him with his affairs. Perhaps then someone could have been there when the buyer made his offer, pointed out the value of the property, asked questions, and reminded him of the consequences. The final decision would have been his, but the sale might have been slowed or stopped, and his son might have been spared the cost and uncertainty of a lawsuit.

The legal concept of a lucid interval treats mental capacity like a switch: off most of the time, briefly on, then off again. That framing made sense when courts relied largely on witness impressions and outward behaviour. But scientific accounts show us something different – a more graded and complex view of cognition, where qualities such as attention can rise and fall, even as the deeper work of decision-making becomes inaccessible.

Once we see capacity as a gradient, not a moment, the practical implications change. Instead of asking whether someone is lucid or ‘with it’ when a decision needs to be made, loved ones can start asking: ‘What parts of this decision-making process can she reliably handle, and where does she need support?’ That shift makes room for safeguards that match the person’s actual strengths and weaknesses, and makes it harder to confuse apparent clarity with informed consent. The law could move in the same direction. Courts already ask whether a person understood a specific act at a specific time. A better fit with psychology is to treat outward coherence as the beginning of the inquiry, not the end, and to demand evidence of the abilities that make consent real.

Tools such as supported decision-making agreements keep adults with cognitive impairments at the centre of their choices, while reducing the risk that poor judgment will cost them everything. These tools can both preserve autonomy for longer and spare families the turmoil of court. To avoid entanglement with legal fictions such as lucid intervals, which are rooted in case law but not science, the time to act is while a person’s mental capacity remains.
