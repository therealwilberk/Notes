# Why raves are such a reliable source of spiritual experience

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Alexander Dillabaugh  
**Date:** 2026-05-26  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/ideas/why-raves-are-such-a-reliable-source-of-spiritual-experience

---

In abandoned warehouses, forests and industrial ruins, vast congregations can be found dancing for hours to high-BPM electronic music. In these spaces, often referred to as raves, phones are covered, professions and everyday identities are left behind, and esoteric accessories mark the entrance into a subversive social order. What began as an underground dance culture has exploded in popularity since the 1990s, amassing a growing global community with frequent pilgrimages to the sacred sites of Berghain in Berlin, Burning Man in Nevada and other techno meccas. Devotees have long described their dancefloor experiences using lofty religious language: comparing DJs to shamans, the dancefloor to church, and their music to gospel.

The DJ, producer and house music legend Frankie Knuckles made perhaps the most memorable of these comparisons when he described the iconic Chicago venue The Warehouse as ‘church for people who have fallen from grace’. This parallel has lingered in the cultural imagination, inspiring both fascination and scepticism, and eventually drawing scholars, such as Graham St John and Robin Sylvan, to the academic exploration of raving and spirituality. Today, neuroscience offers new tools for elucidating how and why so many people choose [communal dance](https://aeon.co/videos/dance-seems-to-be-the-ultimate-frivolity-how-did-it-become-a-human-necessity) as their form of spiritual catharsis.

For these adherents, underground dance culture functions as a kind of _spiritual technology_. That is, a set of environmental and social conditions that reliably produce the kinds of experiences that humans have long called sacred. Viewed through this lens, rave culture appears less like a decadent imitation of religion and more like its post-industrial continuation.

One of the most widely recognised features of spiritual experience is the softening, or temporary dissolution, of the ego – that inner narrator keeping track of who you are, how you are perceived, and where you end and others begin. The cultural anthropologist Victor Turner placed this process within the concept of liminality. Ritual practices, he explained, facilitate the intentional abandonment of ordinary social roles and induce a temporary state characterised by openness, social ambiguity and potential transformation.

Similarly, in his book _The Cognitive Neuroscience of Religious Experience_ (2nd ed, 2022), the neuroscientist Patrick McNamara argues that ego-decentring is not incidental but essential to the induction of spiritual states. Spiritual experience, in his account, depends on a temporary loosening of the brain’s narrative grip. When this grip relaxes – as it might during deep meditative, psychedelic or trance states – individuals can experience themselves from a broader, less ego-bound perspective, creating conditions in which new patterns of meaning and self-understanding can emerge and later be reintegrated into the self-concept.

Conventional hierarchies dissolve as the everyday, social self recedes

Neuroscientifically, this process [corresponds](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1053811919302952) to reduced [activity](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43440-023-00551-8) in core hubs of the default mode network, particularly the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) and the posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) – regions implicated in self-referential thought, autobiographical memory and internal narrative maintenance. However, this pattern of decreased mPFC and PCC activity is not unique to religious or spiritual contexts. Neuroimaging [studies](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12827708/) of intense immersion or ‘flow states’ show that when individuals are fully absorbed in an activity, such as music performance or dance, these same self-referential regions temporarily quiet, reflecting diminished internal dialogue and heightened engagement with what’s perceived in the present.

This convergence helps explain why people describe both traditional spiritual encounters and embodied dancefloor experiences as forms of surrender that leave them fundamentally changed – in both cases, the familiar sense of self softens, placing the ego in a plane of potential transformation.

Rave settings have long facilitated this abandonment of identity. Low lighting, dense crowds and sensory overwhelm obscure social markers and quiet narrative thought. As dancers merge with the crowd, anonymity is paradoxically created and attention is collectively shifted to the sound and movement of the present. Conventional hierarchies dissolve as the everyday, social self recedes.

While ego softening helps explain how dancers let go, synchronised movement helps explain how they come to feel as one. Across disciplines, researchers have [found](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2014.01064/full) [evidence](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/2009-24381-011) that [synchronised](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4985033/) movements promote trust, cooperation and social bonding. Complementary research has demonstrated that complex whole-body [movements](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1547022/full) and prolonged physical [exertion](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40547903/) are [associated](https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/20/3/1992) with low-frequency theta and alpha wave activity in the brain. These patterns of neural activation are similarly observed in [spiritual](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33371283/) and [meditative](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19922249/) states, suggesting a shared neurological signature.

Electronic dance music is particularly well suited for synchronising effects. Its characteristic repetitive structure and gradual modulation promote neural entrainment – the alignment of internal neural rhythms to external sensory patterns. Low-frequency bass, felt as much as heard, physically vibrates bodies and brains into a shared rhythm. In this context, the feeling of ‘being on the same wavelength’ is not just metaphorical; it’s neurophysiological.

Turner called this _communitas_. Émile Durkheim named it collective effervescence. On the dancefloor, it’s simply referred to as the ‘peak’.

As the London-based DJ LO-LOW described it to me: ‘It’s the synchrony with other people that brings me to that peak moment, the feeling of the whole dancefloor being a single, coordinated being. It’s the sensory assault, the lights and sound … that when it hits you, your connection to the people around you reaches this higher plane. Communication moves beyond words and you are able to just be in your body. You’re really in your rawest form.’

Spiritual experiences are inherently emotional – sometimes euphoric, sometimes frightening, and often a mix of both. The German philosopher Rudolf Otto described this as _mysterium tremendum et fascinans_ : the overwhelming sense of encountering something completely other, a presence so powerful and awe-inspiring that it both humbles and draws you in. In electronic dance spaces, many participants seek similar affective states and use psychoactive substances to reliably reach them.

For better or for worse, recreational drugs are a common feature of rave culture’s experiential landscape, and they offer a view into the conscious and emotional states that colour these spaces. For example, [MDMA](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30949077/), also known as ecstasy, has been deeply embedded in dance culture since the 1980s due to its ability to heighten pleasure, induce social bonding and keep dancers moving all night. Its consumption increases the release of serotonin and facilitates dopamine and oxytocin signalling, resulting in effects that closely resemble the emotional intensity and heightened feelings of connection reported in spiritual experiences. Meanwhile, [psychedelics](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36272145/) like LSD and psilocybin induce altered consciousness and perceptual distortions comparable to those commonly reported in spiritual visions by acting on serotonergic receptors and decreasing activity in the brain’s default mode network. Similarly, dissociatives such as ketamine induce altered perception and awareness by quieting the usual narrative ego and facilitating the feeling of ‘letting go’ to the music and crowd. This induced dissociative state is also [linked](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30353262/) to [disruptions](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31532029/) in the default mode network, contributing to shifts in boundary perception and self-other integration that echo the ego-softening processes of spiritual states.

Many people report transformative dancefloor experiences without reliance on substances

Regardless of their source, these neurochemical processes share a common function: they make experiences feel significant. Heightened emotional arousal [activates](https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev.neuro.27.070203.144157) limbic circuitry involved in memory consolidation, deeply embedding such moments in the personal narrative. This may help explain why both traditional spiritual encounters and rave experiences are often described as life-altering moments that incite the reorganisation of meaning, purpose and identity.

Some might argue that using a spiritual framework to describe a culture marked by substance use is merely a sacrilegious attempt at sanctifying hedonistic escapism. Yet many people report similar dancefloor experiences without reliance on substances.

One ravegoer gave me insight into this when they described their experience with going sober. ‘It was a really validating experience for me,’ they said, ‘to go to these spaces and still get so much out of it.’ While the raver acknowledged that their experience is different now – they don’t stay out as late and sometimes struggle to connect – they said: ‘I think that makes it more special … When the music is right, and the crowd is aligned, I can still feel this spiritual release.’

By acknowledging the transformative potential of the dancefloor, we can begin to view it not as inextricably linked to substance use but as an opportunity to practise reaching these states without depending on their artificial induction.

Underground dance spaces have long been dismissed as frivolous, dangerous and socially corrosive. In Europe and North America, anti-rave legislation in the 1990s and early 2000s threatened to dismantle not just these gatherings but the communities they cultivated, yet the fringe community has continuously invented new ways to survive and evolve. This political reaction is hardly novel. It echoes the social and political tension that often arises when an alternative form of collective transcendence emerges outside of institutional control, as seen in early Christianity, Sufi practice, and the countercultural movement of the 1960s.

What distinguishes rave culture is not its novelty but its historical and political context. Emerging from Black, Brown, and queer working-class communities in the shadow of the AIDS crisis, house music and communal dance became an embodied affirmation of life in the face of death. A church for the dispossessed, it offered a way to find ecstasy amid agony, community from abandon, and meaning through resilience. In some spaces, dancing became inseparable from overt political resistance. The New Afrika Shrine in Lagos, for example, fused nightlife with anti-authoritarian activism, transforming the club into a place of cultural and political mobilisation.

Rave and electronic dance spaces continue to play a vital role in political solidarity and mobilisation

Within many dance spaces, collective cooperation is as practical as it is symbolic. With minimal security and limited oversight, dancers look to each other for safety and care. Prolonged physical exertion, intoxication and sensory overload make vulnerability inevitable, turning mutual aid from a moral abstraction into a practical necessity. Cooperation emerges not from enforcement but from a shared ideology of peace, love, unity and respect. Precisely the social alchemy that religions have sought to cultivate for millennia.

Viewed through this lens, it’s not surprising that contemporary rave and electronic dance culture has been profoundly shaped by trans women. Beneath its techno-industrial facade, rave culture is deeply feminine: protective rather than punitive, relational rather than hierarchical, and oriented towards care, imagination and transformation. Today, amid escalating anti-trans legislation, violence and backlash to LGBTQ+ rights, rave and electronic dance spaces continue to play a vital role in political solidarity and mobilisation.

William James argued that the ‘fruits’ of religious experience matter more than its source. From this perspective, it may be unnecessary to debate the extent to which dance culture resembles mainstream spiritual practices. What matters is its ability to reliably produce spaces that normalise emotional catharsis, empathic expansion and the exploration of alternative states of consciousness.

In an age of compounding distraction and quasi-connection, raves demand a radical form of presence. Where phones are away, attention is reclaimed, and bodies rather than screens are the primary medium of connection. To recognise these spaces as legitimate sites of spiritual experience does not require their romanticisation or exemption from critique. Rather, it acknowledges their cultural, political and psychological significance, and extends respect to the communities that have built and upheld them. More broadly, it invites us to reconsider where and how spiritual life can emerge today. In a fractured world hungry for connection and meaning, the dancefloor offers a place to congregate in the euphoric practice of reimagining ourselves, our world and our ability to love one another.
