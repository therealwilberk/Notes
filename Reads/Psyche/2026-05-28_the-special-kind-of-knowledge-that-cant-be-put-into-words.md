# The special kind of knowledge that can’t be put into words

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Matt Duncan  
**Date:** 2026-05-28  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/ideas/the-special-kind-of-knowledge-that-cant-be-put-into-words

---

Imagine waking up with a horrible headache. The pain is absolutely blinding. It’s so bad, in fact, that you go to a hospital. The nurses escort you to a doctor who patiently listens to you describe your headache. Then something weird happens. Instead of giving you pain meds, or further tests, or even a word of empathy, the doctor looks you in the eye and says: ‘I have good news: you’re not in pain. It may feel like you are. But you’re not.’ What would you say? What would you do? What would you think?

I’d think the doctor is crazy. Or cruel – playing some sick joke on me. Or maybe I’d just be confused. I’d wonder whether they _meant_ to say something different, like that there’s nothing physically wrong with my head, that my headache is psychosomatic or something like that. I could get that. I’d also be open to the idea that I misdescribed, or failed to fully express, what I’m feeling. I’m no poet. But what I wouldn’t get is the idea that it could _feel_ like I’m in pain – horrible, blinding pain – when I’m not. Plus, how would they know? I don’t care what fancy titles or diplomas they have. I’m the expert on my own pain.

I’d ditch the doc. I hope you would, too. You _know_ you are in pain. If it feels like you are – if it seems or appears that way – then you are. After all, the feeling of pain – the ‘ouch’, the what-it’s-likeness of the experience – is itself pain. That’s what pain _is_. You may not know what’s causing it, whether there’s anything physically wrong with you, or what words to use when describing your pain, but you have special access to – and knowledge of – what you’re experiencing in a given moment, including your pain.

This special way you know your pain and other experiences is called ‘introspection’. And it really is special. No one has access to your pains – or thoughts or feelings – in the way that you do. This access is also especially _secure_. You couldn’t be wrong about what you are currently experiencing in the way that you could be wrong about other things. Even if you’re dreaming – or in a computer simulation, the Matrix, or some creepy _Black Mirror_ scenario – and don’t know what’s going on around you in the external world, even then your experiences exist, and you know what they are. In these ways, introspection is special.

Sometimes, people use the word ‘introspection’ to mean something more effortful, more meditative – like the ‘looking within’ you do on a yoga retreat, which requires careful attention and is enhanced by practice or training. Sometimes, philosophers and researchers in other fields talk about introspection in similar ways. But you don’t have to meditate to know you have a headache. You don’t have to peer within, search your soul, plumb the depths. Pain is its own testimony. It lets you know. The same goes, at least to some extent, for other experiences. There are many mysteries of the mind, but what you’re experiencing right now is not among them.

Or so it seems. In recent decades, sceptics have emerged, questioning the specialness of introspection. These philosophers and scientists point to a substantial empirical literature showing that people regularly make mistakes about their experiences. Some of this research shows that people make mistakes in identifying the causes or effects of their experiences, or in predicting what they will experience in the future. For example, in one classic [experiment](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1978-00295-001) by Richard Nisbett and Timothy Wilson, people attributed their preferences between products to differences in the qualities of the products they experienced, even though the products were in fact identical. Other research shows that people make mistakes in describing or categorising their experiences. You might misdescribe the frustration you feel in response to your doctor’s denial as some other kind of feeling – sadness, disappointment, anger, or embarrassment. Yet other research reveals further mistakes. Taken together, this literature seems to support a kind of scepticism – or at least pessimism – about introspection, a sense that we aren’t very good at introspecting, or that our knowledge of our own experiences isn’t as special as we may assume it to be.

What are we to make of this? On the one hand, your doctor is nuts – you know your pain exists, and they’re in no position to cast doubt on this. On the other hand, science shows that people often make mistakes about their experiences. You could deny the science, but that seems bad. Or you could nod along to your doctor’s take on what you’re ‘really’ experiencing and sweat out your ‘illusory’ pain as best you can. But that seems even worse. There’s a tension here. And it’s not easy to see how to ease it.

Perhaps some of it can be eased with a little clarification. When you bolt for the hospital door on your way to a saner medical service, it’s not because you think you’re infallible about what _caused_ your pain. After all, you came to the hospital to find that out! You’d probably also admit that you don’t always know what you’ll experience in the future, and, like me, you’d probably grant that your powers of expression are limited – you might misdescribe your experiences, or fail to fully express what they’re like.

This last thing is actually pretty common. Just think about trying to describe how something tastes to someone who hasn’t tasted it before. Or try relaying what your most recent vacation was like to someone who wasn’t there. Or consider your experiences of love or grief, of giving birth or fighting in a war. Try as you might, you’re always going to come up short. You can’t fully put experiences into words. Does that mean you don’t know what they’re like, even when you’re undergoing them? Of course not.

People can and do misremember and misinterpret past or future things. It’s your pain _right now_ that you know so securely

It’s not hubris that’s driving you to the hospital exit. It’s not because you think you’re a wordsmith, oracle or some kind of medical mastermind. Your special knowledge of your experiences isn’t knowledge of their causes, effects or proper descriptions. That’s not what your introspective expertise consists of.

So what, then, does it consist of? For starters, it’s knowledge of your experiences _right now_ , in the present moment. It’s not knowledge of past or future experiences, or about other things related to your experiences that are in the past or future – such as their causes or effects. People can and do misremember, mispredict and misinterpret these things. It’s your pain _right now_ that you know so intimately, so securely.

This still leaves open certain questions, such as why people misreport and misdescribe their current experiences. If we can’t put our experiences fully into words, why think we have special knowledge of them? We still need to know more about what this knowledge consists of. It’s one thing to clarify the claim that introspection is special and excuse the mistakes; it’s another to justify the claim and explain how it’s true.

One explanation that has gained some traction in the recent philosophical literature starts with the idea that the knowledge you have of your own experiences is just a completely different kind of knowledge from what your doctor knows, or could know, about you – or the kind of knowledge you have of your experiences’ causes, effects or future manifestations. It’s not the kind of knowledge that you gain from reading a textbook or listening to a lecture, nor is it the kind of knowledge that subjects report when they try to describe their experiences to others. It can’t be expressed in natural language – at least, not fully.

That’s what it’s not. What it _is_ is experiential knowledge, made up of your conscious awareness of things you experience, like colours, shapes, smells, tastes, feelings and, in your case, pain. It’s not propositional, or the kind of knowledge that is expressed with declarative sentences like ‘The sky is blue’ or ‘Paris is the capital of France.’ It’s just conscious awareness of things, the things you experience throughout your waking life. To put it differently, it’s not knowledge of _truths_ like facts that you could learn from reading a textbook, it’s knowledge of _things_ like the experience of pain.

This terminology comes from [Bertrand Russell](https://aeon.co/videos/a-fanatic-against-fanaticism-and-other-pleasures-of-bertrand-russell-in-his-own-words), who made the same distinction in his book _The_ _Problems of Philosophy_ (1912). Within the ‘knowledge of things’ category, he distinguished between ‘knowledge by description’ and ‘knowledge by acquaintance’. On his view, we know things by description when we are aware of them as satisfying some concept or description, such as ‘the nurse who admitted me’ or ‘this crazy doctor’. Knowledge by acquaintance, in contrast, doesn’t rely on any concepts or descriptions. It’s simply constituted by direct conscious awareness of things – like your pain. Even if you don’t have the right words for it – even if you didn’t have _any_ concept of pain – you could still be acquainted with it and, for Russell, that acquaintance would count as knowledge.

As long as you’re aware of your experiences, you have the knowledge – even if you’re dreaming

In the past few decades, philosophers have largely ignored these other kinds of knowledge. They focused more on ‘book smarts’ – the kind of knowledge that can be read in a book, pronounced in a lecture, or written on a whiteboard. However, there’s recently been a revival of interest in Russell’s idea that some knowledge is different – constituted simply by conscious awareness of things.

If we take this distinction on board, we can explain why your introspective knowledge is special – and your doctor is crazy – without being science-deniers. When you feel pain, you are introspectively aware of your pain, and this awareness, by itself, constitutes a kind of knowledge of your pain. It’s different from other, third-personal ways of knowing about pain, and it cannot be put into words in the way that would be required to fully describe it in a science experiment. Since no one else can be aware of your pain in the way that you are, no one else – including your doctor – can have this knowledge of it. This knowledge is also especially secure. It’s constituted only by direct conscious awareness, so it doesn’t depend on concepts, descriptions, memories or other things that could be mistaken or manipulated, even by powerful sci-fi robots or aliens. As long as you’re aware of your experiences, you have the knowledge – even if you’re dreaming or in the Matrix. In these ways, it’s special.

If all of this is right, then not only do we have a scientifically respectable explanation for why your doctor should stay in their medical lane, we’ve also brought into focus a whole other, potentially expansive domain of knowledge: experiential knowledge. Until recently, it was mostly ignored by philosophers of the past century. And while ‘experiential learning’ is a buzzword in education, its nature or significance hasn’t been explored in adequate depth. But it is important, as the case of your headache illustrates.

There are so many other ways to illustrate it, too. Think of your experiential knowledge while on vacation. Or during childbirth or war. Or the knowledge you gain by experiencing art – music, film, painting, poetry and more – which cannot be fully put into words. Or think of the knowledge constituted by your experiences with your partner, the way they look and sound and smell, the way they love you. This isn’t just classroom stuff. It shapes our lives. Our introspective knowledge really is incredibly special.
