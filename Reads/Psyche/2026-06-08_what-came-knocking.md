# What came knocking

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Michael Jawer  
**Date:** 2026-06-08  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/turning-points/our-dog-died-then-i-started-noticing-the-odd-sounds

---

One spring morning, my wife and I were awakened by a woodpecker tapping outside our bedroom window. Although we’ve lived in this house for 33 years, a woodpecker had come only once before, presumably attracted by a slat of rotting wood near the roof. That bird pecked for days. But this time the woodpecker came for just a few minutes and then was gone.

In those minutes, the bird’s staccato tapping uncannily mimicked the thrumming inside my ears when I’d cried over Daisy, our 15-year-old schnauzer. We’d put her to sleep two days before. She had advanced kidney disease and dementia that, together, marred her last few months with us. Even then, her ebullient spirit would break through and we would get a sense of the ‘old’ Daisy: energetic, quick, fun-loving, and occasionally persnickety.

Our family loved that dog; she was especially dear to our kids, who were elementary-school age when we’d adopted her. Bradley, now 23, took Daisy’s care as his special responsibility. As her appetite ebbed, he would try a variety of foods to entice her to eat, making sure to include her supplements and medicine. He took her on daily walks – and, when she wasn’t up for one, a car ride with the back window rolled down so Daisy could put her head out and enjoy the breeze blowing back her ears and whiskers. Bradley positioned blankets around the house so she wouldn’t be far from a comfortable place to lie down. Years ago, she’d been so fast, there was no chance of catching her racing around the back yard; now, he tenderly played games of ‘feint’ and ‘catch the stick’ with her. Almost every night, Daisy slept in his room.

As her time drew down, Bradley kept a vigil with his dog. He lay beside her and read to her a favourite childhood book. _The Gift of the Tree_ tells the tale of a hundred-year-old oak that stands tall in the forest, providing a home for birds and squirrels, and feeding the latter richly each autumn with a fall of acorns. Eventually, beset by rot and grubs, the tree grows weak, its branches sparse, its leaves few. Even as woodpeckers pepper its trunk with holes, the tree continues as a source of life: the woodpeckers make bigger holes for their babies to nest in. After the tree falls, it remains home to chipmunks and raccoons, while mosses, mushrooms and ferns gather in its shadows. Ultimately, the rich loam**–** dark, crumbly soil made from the remains of what once lived**–** offers a haven for earthworms, slugs and fungi, whose work accelerates the rot and returns the tree to the forest floor.

We dug Daisy a grave in the back yard, next to a young tulip poplar that had grown from the stump of another tulip poplar I’d had to take down. We wrapped her in her favourite blanket. Then we held a short service, reciting the Mourner’s Kaddish, a centuries-old Jewish prayer affirming the sanctity of God, a source of comfort and reassurance in the face of finality.

About an hour later came a knock that sounded odd. Opening the door, I saw no one

In the hours and days after Daisy’s death, a pall fell around us. Having lost pets before, my wife and I knew we would remember the sprightly Daisy, the dog who took endless time inspecting smells and who, in the summer months, would pull herself through the warm grass as if surfing.

The day after she left us, we received a delivery of flowers from friends who’d known Daisy; there was a knock at the front door and a deliveryman to thank. About an hour later came another knock, though this one sounded odd. To my ears, it fell midway between a solid knock and the sound of wax paper crinkling. Opening the door, I saw no one.

What made the situation remarkable was its parallel with a crisp October day in 2001, after the death of my cat, Dalton. I was at the top of our staircase, bent down to explain to my then-toddler daughter that ‘Dalty’ wouldn’t be coming back – he’d been hit by a car. The necessity of broaching the subject of death with my child felt daunting and my voice broke. Just then, there was a knock on the door and my wife went to open it. No one was there.

Perhaps 45 minutes after the flower delivery, I was at my desk trying to divert my sadness by scrolling through emails when I heard a loud crack nearby. The sound came from a sports drink bottle of my son’s; perched on a sloped surface, it had fallen onto the wood floor. A simple enough explanation, except the bottle had been in that same spot for months. As a small test, I put the bottle back in the same sloped position; weeks went by and the bottle did not move.

When the woodpecker woke me up the next morning, I was prompted to think about the symbolism of birds, which often occupy the nexus of life and death in the human imagination: robins, eggs and spring songs on one side; ravens, vultures and ominously hooting owls on the other. Perhaps it is because they appear and vanish so quickly, lifting into an airy realm we long to follow, and with a freedom we can only approximate in mechanical flight.

The religion professor Christopher Moreman [notes](https://brill.com/view/journals/soan/22/5/article-p481_3.xml) that this swiftness and border-crossing quality has long made birds seem like messengers between worlds. Just after Daisy died, I opened a window, as Jewish tradition instructs, for the soul to take flight. I couldn’t help but feel the woodpecker’s visit belonged to that ancient language of passing and return. Moreman suggests that birds may activate a Jungian archetype – primeval material in the collective unconscious that surfaces in moments of heightened emotion. In that sense, the woodpecker’s appearance might be understood as a synchronicity: a meaningful coincidence or a convergence of inner feeling with the outer world.

Perhaps animals live closer to the bone, because they do not traffic in rumination and analysis

In my family’s case, though, I am drawn to something simpler. Perhaps, in sufficiently advanced creatures – those with a discernible personality – there exists an impetus: a concentration of feeling and affiliation capable of outlasting the material brain and body. One could call this _soul_ , not in a religious sense, but as a force that impels contact with those remembered and valued, if only briefly.

Could this help explain accounts we have all heard of family pets travelling long distances to return to their owners, even after those owners have moved? Perhaps animals live closer to the bone, feeling more directly and intensely than we do, because they do not traffic in rumination and analysis. And our pets are, in every practical sense, family members. We love and provide for them; they love and rely upon us; bonds form that feel tenderly enduring.

In _A Wolf Called Romeo_(2014), the Alaskan photographer Nick Jans describes an unusual bond between two men and a wild wolf in Juneau, Alaska. After poachers kill the wolf, both men – independently and without knowledge of the other – dream vividly of the fatal injury. Only years later do they discover the coincidence. Jans does not offer an explanation. The story is notable because it echoes the mystery of my own experience.

Perhaps Romeo’s essence made itself known to those who knew him, just as Daisy’s may have come to us. If such moments occur, they seem to bypass our accustomed frameworks of time and place, cause and effect – leaving us with the stunning experience itself.

In the end, I can only say that Daisy’s ‘visitations’ (if they were that) eased the feelings of sorrow and loss that had engulfed my family. My wife and I felt a sense of completion – as if Daisy had signalled at first ‘Hey’ and then ‘Goodbye, farewell, so long.’

I’ve come to feel a renewed closeness with the totality of nature, what the eco-philosopher David Abram has [called](https://www.davidabram.org/essays/magic-and-machine-65hd4-fj357-hmg7n-r44nk) the ‘commonwealth of breath’. Mind, says Abram, is not merely inside each of us but bears relation to everything outside ourselves in the world. We are animals and part of nature ourselves. We live, we die, and we are connected – by breath, by feeling, and by the simple fact that not one of us willed ourselves into existence. Yet here we are. And when someone we love is no longer here, we are shaken, moved to tears. Just maybe, the movement goes on.

One can’t know what, if anything, awaits after death, but we were comforted with the sense that, whatever phases living beings move through, the journey for our cherished schnauzer had gone the way it should.
