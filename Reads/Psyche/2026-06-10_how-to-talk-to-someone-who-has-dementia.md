# How to talk to someone who has dementia

It’s 7:30 pm, and your mother puts on her coat instead of her pajamas. She pats her pockets, then starts scanning the kitchen counters, tugging open the hallway closet and finally tipping her purse onto the floor, as if the missing item might clatter into view. ‘I need to go home,’ she says, lifting a set of keys from the bowl where you’ve been keeping them lately. ‘My mother will be waiting.’

You feel the familiar jolt: the urge to fix this with facts. _Mom, you are home. Grandma died years ago._ Even as you try to soften your voice, you can hear the edge in it. But the more you insist, the more she looks at you like you’re the one who’s confused. ‘Why are you doing this?’ she demands. ‘Stop telling me what I know.’

Suddenly you’re in a standoff. She’s frightened and determined; you’re exhausted and pleading. Both of you want to return to the same reality you shared earlier that evening, but you don’t know how to do it in the same language.

If you have a loved one with dementia – perhaps a parent, a grandparent, a partner or another relative – the above scenario might seem familiar. It illustrates some of the challenges that can arise when communicating with someone living with dementia, though you have likely faced other kinds of difficult situations as well.

Dementia, as you may know, is an umbrella term for the changes that happen when a brain disease begins to disrupt a person’s thinking. It refers to a decline in cognition that builds over time and interferes with the person’s independence in everyday life. It isn’t one disease, but a consequence of several different possible brain diseases. And it’s more than memory loss: dementia can alter attention, judgment and language, changing how someone finds words, follows a conversation or makes sense of what they hear. That’s one reason communication can start to feel unfamiliar, even between people who have known each other for decades.

There isn’t just one correct way to communicate with someone who has dementia. Based on experiences in the clinic and interviews with caregivers on our podcast Dementia Matters, it’s clear that families arrive at different communication styles that fit their loved one and the stage of disease. Some families are very direct and honest, without being abrasive. They address challenges head on, openly and gently naming the changes they’re noticing and offering support. Some choose to let certain inaccuracies pass in order to protect the person’s dignity. Still others explicitly ground moments of difficulty in the diagnosis, to reduce fear and blame: ‘This is the dementia, it’s not your fault,’ they might say, and then calmly explain how they are going to help. Though navigating these brain changes can be challenging – opening you up to new feelings of anxiety, stress, grief and more – there are strategies available that can lead to calmer, more connected interactions and make the journey a little easier, one conversation at a time.

In this Guide, we’ll look at a number of specific practices to help you stay emotionally connected and keep your relationship strong as the illness progresses. Though the severity or stage of a person’s dementia often indicates when certain challenges in communication and behaviour are more likely to arise, the techniques we’ll be covering can be helpful at any stage, and they can be combined and tailored to meet your and your loved one’s needs. These approaches are designed to support you with everyday tasks (such as getting your loved one ready for an appointment or for taking medication), as well as with the moments that matter most: catching up, sharing a meal and finding ways to feel close when words don’t come easily.

### Learn how their form of dementia affects communication

The diseases that cause dementia symptoms don’t all affect communication in the same way. That’s why one of the most useful things you can do is learn more about how the kind of dementia that your loved one has tends to interfere with the abilities that are relevant to communicating. This can help you make sense of what you’re seeing and choose approaches that are more likely to be effective.

Talking with your loved one’s doctor can help you understand the particular communication difficulties and behaviour changes that your loved one might be experiencing. Here, we’ll briefly cover some of the ways in which dementia-causing diseases relate to communication. (We’ll share more specific guidance on what to do in the next steps.)

  * _Alzheimer’s disease_ or _Alzheimer’s dementia_(AD) often starts with short-term memory problems and can include word-finding difficulty, trouble recalling people’s names and repeating questions or stories. In many people, understanding what’s being said remains stronger, early on, than the ability to remember or retrieve specifics.
  * _Lewy body dementia_ (LBD) can cause slowed thinking and difficulties with attention, reasoning, problem-solving and multitasking, which can make conversations hard to follow. Many people also develop visuospatial problems (such as missing things in front of them) and vivid, well-formed visual hallucinations that are often nonthreatening.
  * _Vascular dementia_ is caused by strokes or by damage to small blood vessels over years, and symptoms depend on where strokes occur or which networks are affected by reduced blood flow. Many people develop slowed processing and inattention, along with difficulties in problem solving, planning and sequencing, and reasoning – changes that can make it harder to track and participate in conversations.
  * _Frontotemporal dementia_(FTD) tends to affect the frontal and temporal lobes, regions involved in language, insight, attention, working memory and executive function. For many people, language is the earliest and most prominent change: they may struggle to find and use words, understand word meanings, read and write. They might substitute incorrect words, have trouble naming common objects and increasingly lose the thread of conversations.



People with dementia often struggle to form and hold on to new memories, yet memories from years or decades ago might remain [surprisingly vivid](https://psyche.co/ideas/earlier-memories-are-relatively-spared-in-dementia-why), especially early in Alzheimer’s disease. Someone may forget the details of a conversation you had an hour ago, or what’s on tomorrow’s agenda, but still describe a childhood home or a first job with striking detail.

That said, in some cases a person may remember recent events better than you’d expect, while having gaps in older memories if an injury affects the circuits that support them. In the moderate and severe stages of dementia, regardless of the cause, both recent and long-ago memories tend to fade, and it can become harder to talk confidently about the past.

No two people experience dementia in the exact same way. Differences in life experience – education, work, hobbies and social routines – can influence which skills stay stronger for longer.

Other factors can matter just as much: hearing loss, vision changes, medications, sleep problems, chronic health conditions, anxiety, apathy or depression. It can help to ask practical questions: do they hear well, or do they need their hearing checked? Do they get overwhelmed in busy environments? Are they unusually withdrawn or disengaged? Many of these issues are addressable, and improving them can make communication noticeably easier. For example, treating hearing loss (eg, with hearing aids from an audiologist) can reduce misunderstanding and help someone feel more confident joining conversations.

The more you notice patterns in your loved one’s thinking and the factors that affect it day to day, the easier it becomes to choose ways of communicating that fit them.

### Avoid responses that backfire

Before we get into more of what helps, it’s useful to cover a few habits that can make connection harder. These might sound obvious on a calm afternoon, but it can be much harder to spot your own patterns when you’re in the thick of it and juggling the stresses of caregiving.

  * **Sharply correcting them.** When you’re speaking with your loved one, they may say things that don’t quite make sense. Perhaps they point out the window at something that isn’t there, or they say they’d like to go home while they’re already at home. In these moments, it might be tempting to try to prove that they’re mistaken. But arguing about facts could cause them distress. In situations like the first one, they might be unsettled by not knowing what’s real or not, while in the latter, they may become frustrated that you’re keeping them from leaving. Sometimes it’s necessary to correct what your loved one has said – for example, if they accuse a caregiver of not caring, or if they claim that their medication isn’t helping when it is. But even in those cases, it’s important to do it with patience and kindness.
  * **Testing their abilities in conversation.** You might feel concerned that you’re ‘enabling’ them if you don’t encourage them to flex their memory and thinking abilities. Though there are ways to help our loved ones exercise their brains, repeatedly challenging them does not help. Pressing your loved one by repeatedly asking ‘What happened yesterday?’ or ‘Tell them what you told me about this’ can cause them to grow agitated or shut down. They may feel ashamed that they can’t remember something they feel they ought to.
  * **Raising your voice.** Talk to someone with dementia the way you would want to be spoken to – with respect. Yelling will not help your loved one understand you better. Even if you are in a bad mood about something, it’s important to talk to your loved one with the respectful tone that you would use to speak with anyone.
  * **Shaming.** Like raising your voice, this won’t help them understand you or communicate better. Your loved one may already be confused and unable to follow what you’re asking. And because dementia carries so much stigma, many people feel embarrassed about their diagnosis and the changes they notice in themselves. Responding to them with sarcasm or blame can make them withdraw or avoid conversations.
  * **Treating them like a child.** Even when your loved one needs support, they are still an adult. Infantilising – through baby talk, exaggerated praise or speaking dramatically slower than you normally would – can feel disrespectful and provoke resistance or shame. You can make accommodations for the person while keeping your tone warm and age-appropriate. (We’ll say more about this below.)



As we mentioned, some of these habits can be hard to notice when you’re tired, stressed or worried. As you talk with your loved one, it can help to do a quick internal check: _Am I trying to reason them into understanding? Am I raising my voice? Am I correcting them in a way that reassures, or in a way that adds pressure or shame?_ If you feel yourself escalating, it’s OK to pause or step away for a moment to collect yourself.

### Reduce noise and other sources of distraction

Loud noises, bright lights and overlapping conversations can be overwhelming for anyone. For a person living with dementia, that sensory overload is often harder to filter. It could leave them distracted or agitated, and make it more challenging for them to talk with you.

So, if you’re finding it difficult to communicate, scan the room and consider the following:

  * **Noise:** is the TV, radio or smart speaker on? Are fans, appliances or alarms creating a constant hum or repetitive beeping? Are multiple conversations happening at once? How loud are the surrounding noises?
  * **Light:** are the lights too bright or harsh? Is there flickering or fast-moving light from a TV or screen?
  * **Temperature:** is it uncomfortably hot or cold?
  * **People:** is the space crowded? Are several people trying to get your loved one’s attention?



Adjust what you’re able to. Turn down the volume or switch off the TV. Move closer or speak in a quieter corner. Dim harsh lights or step away from flickering screens. If the room is too busy, suggest a brief reset in a calmer space: ‘Want to come sit with me for a minute?’ Even small changes could make it easier for your loved one to follow what you’re saying, and to want to keep talking.

This approach is especially useful at family gatherings or larger events. Instead of having your loved one sit in a busy room for hours, caught between multiple group conversations, kids running around and the TV or music playing, try to create a quieter space that they can choose to retreat to when they feel overwhelmed. This space, possibly in a room nearby, might allow them to recharge and recollect themselves. Invite people to visit in small groups, a few at a time, so your loved one can have real connection without having to deal with environmental noise.

### Use less complex language, slow down, and repeat as needed

Imagine that you’re learning a new language and you decide to attend an event to practise speaking. You arrive, and instead of being surrounded by other learners, you find yourself in a room full of fluent speakers. They talk quickly, use unfamiliar slang and bounce between topics without pausing. Add music in the background and side conversations nearby, and it becomes impossible to keep track. Overwhelmed, you stop trying to follow along.

This is how it can feel to live with dementia. The brain is already more vulnerable to overstimulation. When attention and processing speed are affected, even ‘normal’ conversation can come in too fast, with too many moving parts. While we can’t always create a perfect environment for communicating, we can be more thoughtful about how we say what we’re trying to say. The goal is to make the message easier for them to receive.

Try using simpler words and shorter sentences. Keep concepts simpler too, and explain them if your loved one seems confused. Sharing one idea at a time can make it easier for your loved one to truly understand what you’re saying. When needed, slow your pace slightly. You don’t have to overdo this, which can come across as insulting. Just go a bit slower than you usually might, being deliberate and conscious as you speak, trying not to let your words run into each other. Pause between phrases to give them room to process, and watch their face and body language for signs they’re tracking or getting lost. If you need to repeat yourself, try to do it without signalling frustration. Your loved one may be studying your facial expressions and body language, too. It’s helpful to display a calm, comforting and inviting posture. You can also reinforce what you’re saying with a brief summary: ‘So the plan is: we leave at two, and the appointment is at three.’

These practices could be especially important for conversations about health care, palliative care, legal and financial planning, or other complex topics. Take time to explain what’s happening in plain language so your loved one can stay involved in decisions for as long as possible, and repeat it as needed in the future.

### Use props or visuals to help you communicate

Pairing spoken language with something visual can give a person with dementia an additional way to take in information and reinforce what’s being discussed. It can also help improve the likelihood of retention by reducing the pressure on memory and incorporating other aspects of brain function.

For example, let’s say you’ve been asked by a loved one with dementia when an appointment is scheduled for today. There’s nothing wrong with simply saying: ‘The doctor’s appointment is at 3 pm,’ but they might not retain the information and so repeat the question. A different response could be: ‘Let’s go check the calendar together to see.’ In other instances, you might use a small whiteboard to anchor the day (‘Today is Tuesday’; ‘Doctor at 3’; ‘Dinner at 6’) and point to it if a question is repeated. You could place sticky notes where they’ll be seen, such as by the hook where the keys hang, on the bathroom mirror, or by the front door.

If making choices seems to feel overwhelming for your loved one, try ‘show, don’t tell’. For example, instead of just asking what they want to wear, you might hold up two shirts, or place tea and coffee in front of them and let them point to which one they’d like.

Visual support can also make routines go more smoothly. For example: a clearly labelled pillbox or a simple taken/not-taken card for medications, or a page of photos with names, to remind your loved one of frequent visitors before a gathering. Demonstration can sometimes do what language can’t, whether it’s brushing your own teeth alongside them, miming the first step to folding laundry, or starting a task together so they can follow your lead.

### Join their world with open-ended questions and observation

Sometimes the most meaningful conversations happen when you stop trying to pull your loved one back into _your_ reality and instead meet them in theirs. That doesn’t mean agreeing with everything they say or letting them get into risky situations. It means staying curious about what they’re experiencing and choosing connection over correction.

How can you try to bridge the gap? One simple way is to set aside questions that have right-or-wrong answers (eg, ‘Do you remember what we did yesterday?’) and instead use open-ended prompts that invite imagination, perspective and feeling. Anne Basting, a dementia and elder care expert, calls this practice ‘beautiful questions’. These questions offer moments for you and your loved one to express yourselves and connect in a way that doesn’t rely heavily on facts and memories. It can be similar to improv – you ask a question and follow wherever your loved one brings the conversation.

Here are a few examples:

  * If you could go anywhere in the world, where would you go?
  * What’s something that feels comforting to you right now?
  * If today had a colour, what would it be?
  * What are you noticing around us?
  * What does ‘home’ feel like to you?
  * If you could design the perfect day, what would it include?
  * What’s a smell you love?
  * What’s a food that sounds delicious?



You can also join their world by paying attention to what they are already responding to. For example, you might find your loved one examining a picture or painting framed on the wall. Instead of pulling them away from it by asking what they’d like to do today, you could join them in looking at the picture. Point out the features you like and ask what they enjoy about it. Tie in some beautiful questions: ask what they think it would sound like in that scene, or if they’d like to be there. Moments like these create more opportunities to connect and to see how they are experiencing the world around them.

### Be patient with them – and with yourself

As you support and communicate with your loved one, there might be moments when things feel surprisingly easy, and others when you feel spent or discouraged. Sometimes these moments will happen back to back. For many family members and friends of people with dementia, there comes a day when their loved one no longer recognises them, and they wonder how the relationship will carry on. Dementia brings a lot of uncertainty and strong emotions. Try to take it one day, or even one conversation, at a time. Give yourself grace for the emotions you feel along the way.

It may help to remember that you’re not in this alone. Many families find support through caregiver organisations, disease-specific communities and groups for people living with cognitive change and their care partners. ‘Memory Cafés’ are a type of gathering held in multiple countries that offer a relaxing space for those with dementia and their loved ones to engage in conversation and activities (such as art-making). National and regional organisations (for example, in the US, the Alzheimer’s Association, the Alzheimer’s Foundation of America, UsAgainstAlzheimer’s, the Lewy Body Dementia Association, or the Association for Frontotemporal Degeneration) offer helplines, articles, events and other resources. Connecting with groups and other caregivers in your area, or online, gives you a chance to trade practical ideas and build a sense of community with people who have some familiarity with what you’ve experienced.

### Talking through behavioural and psychological changes

For many families, the hardest part of dementia caregiving is not forgetfulness. It is the behavioural and psychological symptoms of dementia (BPSD): agitation, anxiety, irritability, suspiciousness, hallucinations, sleep disruption, apathy and even aggression. These symptoms can feel personal, even when you know they are not. They also lead to some of the moments when communication breaks down most quickly.

A common example is ‘sundowning’, a pattern in which confusion and agitation worsen in the late afternoon or evening. Here’s a scenario to illustrate: it is 6.30 pm, and your dad begins pacing from room to room, checking the front door, peering out the windows, insisting that something is wrong. He cannot settle into the couch the way he did earlier. When you ask what he needs, he snaps but isn’t able to say clearly what’s wrong. His voice is urgent, his body tense. You feel your own heart rate rise, because you have already had a long day and you don’t know where this is coming from.

Let’s say you have done the clinical homework. Your dad has been evaluated by the primary care provider. There is no urinary infection, no medication side-effects, no new pain, dehydration, constipation or clear trigger beyond the dementia itself. That knowledge can help – you’re not going to panic over a sudden medical problem or have to take him to the emergency room – but knowing that the distress is BPSD does not make the moment easy. It can even make you feel more helpless: if there is nothing to ‘fix’, what are you supposed to do?

This is where you can make use of the combined lessons from this Guide. The first move is often to avoid the reflex that escalates distress: correcting, arguing, quizzing or shaming. If you say: ‘There’s nothing wrong, stop it,’ what he hears is dismissal. If you demand an explanation that he cannot provide, he may feel cornered.

Instead, and as long as it’s safe for you both, try meeting him where he is. You can treat his words as an emotional signal. ‘You seem really worried,’ you might say, keeping your voice low and steady. ‘I’m here with you. You’re safe.’ Sometimes this kind of reassurance has to be repeated, because the feeling might persist even after the cause of the distress or subsequent conversation is forgotten.

Look at the environment, too. Late day is when the brain is tired and sensory overload hits harder. If the television is on, turn it off. If the lights are dim and the shadows seem to be confusing, gently brighten the room. Reduce the number of voices talking at once. Sit at eye level. Offer a simple, concrete choice: ‘Want to take a walk to the kitchen and get a drink of water?’ or ‘Shall we sit here together for a minute?’ You are trying to help his body downshift by redirecting him away from the stressor or situation.

Finally, offer something calming that does not rely on short-term memory or reasoning: a familiar song, a warm drink, folding towels together, a short walk, a hand massage with lotion, or simply sitting nearby while he settles. If he keeps saying he needs to ‘go’, you can redirect, without confrontation: ‘We can go after we rest a bit. Tell me what you’re getting ready for.’

These approaches will not always work. Some moments will still unravel. The point is to try your best to calm and regulate the situation while respecting both your needs and your loved one’s needs. Over time, many caregivers find that the most effective communication does not rely on finding the right words to say. Instead, it is a calm presence and a steady willingness to meet your loved one where they’re at when their world grows overwhelming.
