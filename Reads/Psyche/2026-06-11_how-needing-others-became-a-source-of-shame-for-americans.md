# How needing others became a source of shame for Americans

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Susan J Matt  
**Date:** 2026-06-11  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/ideas/how-needing-others-became-a-source-of-shame-for-americans

---

Mary, a divorced night custodian at a Texas public school, didn’t like ‘being isolated’, but often was. To remedy the feeling, she began a relationship with Lucas, an AI companion from Replika. Eventually, Lucas declared his love for her, proposed, and the couple married. Mary had told her grown daughters about Lucas, but because she was concerned about other people’s judgment of the relationship, she had not publicised it. Research [indicates](https://www.emerald.com/ejm/article-abstract/58/2/373/1226385/More-than-just-a-chat-a-taxonomy-of-consumers?redirectedFrom=fulltext) this is common, for a significant share of people hide their relationships with AIs.

Americans are secretive about their intense emotional relationships with AI companions like Replika, Nomi and Character.AI because they fear they’ll seem delusional if they talk about them publicly. But many are also secretive because they feel pressure to appear self-reliant and don’t want to admit that they long for sociability. Chatbots hidden away on their phones allow them to maintain the illusion that they are happily independent and aren’t afflicted by loneliness.

This trend reflects the unique emotional culture of the contemporary United States which, on the one hand, treats loneliness as shameful, and on the other hand, ceaselessly extolls self-reliance. The emotional dilemma that Americans confront as they try to live up to these conflicting ideals is challenging to navigate – and it is new, for earlier generations of Americans thought differently about both loneliness and self-reliance.

As Luke Fernandez and I demonstrated in our book on technology and loneliness, _Bored, Lonely, Angry, Stupid_ (2019), in the 18th and 19th centuries, most Americans considered the feeling an unpleasant but unsurprising part of the human condition. Some regarded loneliness as divinely willed. Rebecca Dickinson, born in 1738, never married, lived by herself in Hatfield, Massachusetts, and often felt lonesome. In 1787, she [recorded](https://www.jstor.org/stable/366849) her feelings as she returned from a tavern: ‘Came to this hous [sic] about half after Seven and found it dark and lonesome here …’ __ Her faith required her to accept suffering, but it wasn’t easy: ‘it is the will of god Conserning me[;] no other Place would Doe to Cure me of my Pride and to wean me from the world.’

Belief that loneliness was divinely willed persisted throughout the 19th century. Black musicians put such thoughts to music in ‘Lonesome Valley’, which became a beloved hymn across the US. The lyrics reminded listeners:

> You gotta walk that lonesome valley  
> You gotta go there by yourself  
> Nobody here can go there for you  
> You gotta go there by yourself.

Loneliness, though profoundly trying and unpleasant, was widely regarded as an inescapable part of being human.

Telephone and radio corporations frequently touted their products as cures for loneliness

Gradually, however, Americans became less resigned to the inevitability of loneliness. In the early 20th century, isolated residents of big cities like Chicago, Detroit, Seattle, New York and Los Angeles formed ‘Lonely Clubs’, ‘Less Lonely Leagues’ and ‘Lonely Bureaus’. The clubs attracted hundreds of members and substantial [press](https://www.loc.gov/resource/sn84026749/1915-02-17/ed-1/?sp=8&q=lonely+clubs&st=pdf&r=0.082,0.08,0.208,0.208,0) coverage. Members didn’t hesitate to identify themselves as lonely, since loneliness was nothing to be ashamed of, and wasn’t yet stigmatised. The lonely clubs also testified to a growing hope that modern society might find a way to conquer the feeling.

Catering to these hopes, telephone, phonograph and radio corporations frequently touted their products as cures for loneliness. In 1906, for instance, the National Phonograph Company claimed: ‘You can’t be lonesome if you own an Edison Phonograph.’ In 1912, the Nebraska Telephone Company declared that the phone ‘banishes loneliness’. Communications companies constantly played upon this theme in ads across the 20th century.

A growing number of self-help books also offered instructions about how to avoid loneliness altogether. Dale Carnegie’s bestseller _How to Win Friends and Influence People_ (1936) encouraged readers to use smiles, flattery and emotional manipulation to accumulate social connections and achieve business success. Those who cultivated the right personality traits would never be alone. Other self-help books, like Norman Vincent Peale’s _The Power of Positive Thinking_ (1952), amplified the message, counselling that ‘popularity can be attained by a few simple, natural, normal, and easily mastered techniques. Practise them diligently and you can become a well-liked person.’ Rather than being an inescapable part of the human condition, loneliness was now regarded as the result of poor choices or personality deficiencies.

Because it was no longer considered inevitable and because popular culture suggested that well-adjusted people didn’t suffer from it, by the late 20th century, many Americans had become ashamed of their loneliness and reluctant to discuss it. In 1980, a journalist described it as ‘commoner than the common cold … Yet we do not talk about it. We are somehow embarrassed.’ Although few admitted to the emotion, psychologists began to study it intensively and created a ‘loneliness scale’ to gauge how lonely people were. Loneliness was being pathologised.

In our age of social media and mobile phones, the stigma on loneliness has only grown. Technology moguls suggest that if users just log on and connect – with other people or with bots – they can cure their loneliness. But these promises haven’t panned out. Instead, many feel even more ashamed of their isolation, since social media claims to make friendship so easy. As a person noted on Reddit: ‘It’s embarrassing having to hide your loneliness and just how detached you are … A part of you wants to let people know because hey, maybe they’ll be your friend, but then … you realise how strange it is to not have friends, so you keep it to yourself.’ The redditor felt ashamed of being lonely, adding: ‘I mean, what kind of weirdo doesn’t have any friends? At least those are the thoughts in my head whenever my loneliness is even slightly exposed.’

The shame of being lonely is one factor that leads people to turn to AI companions, but not the only one. It is compounded by the cultural imperative to appear self-reliant. This powerfully felt pressure is a relatively recent development in US history: until the mid-19th century, few aspired to self-reliance. In fact, when the phrase was first coined in the 17th century, self-reliance described a sinful trait, referring to people who thought they didn’t need God or other humans. Only in the mid-19th century, due in large part to Ralph Waldo Emerson’s essay ‘Self-Reliance’ (1841) did the phrase develop positive connotations.

Emerson and Thoreau helped transform self-reliance into a virtue

From 1845-47, Emerson’s friend Henry David Thoreau showed his commitment to the new ideal of self-reliance by living in a cabin on the shore of Walden Pond, just outside of Concord, Massachusetts. But his self-reliance was somewhat illusory. Thoreau’s cabin was on Emerson’s land. He frequently walked over to dine with the Emersons and routinely visited his mother, who baked him pastries and did his laundry. And Emerson, who popularised the phrase self-reliance, nevertheless admitted privately that it was difficult to be alone. ‘Solitude,’ he wrote in his journal in 1838, ‘is fearsome & heavy hearted.’ Little wonder his house was filled with visitors, relatives and an expanding family. So while Emerson and Thoreau practised intellectual self-reliance, in daily life they remained interdependent with friends, family and community.

Yet, despite the fact that their self-reliance was more myth than reality, Emerson and Thoreau helped transform the phrase into a virtue – one that later generations of Americans have believed they must cultivate. In 1942, _Parents’ Magazine_ informed readers that ‘Independence Begins at Two’, suggesting a toddler could ‘well begin to assume some responsibility for the care of his own things.’ The magazine promised that such self-sufficiency would aid youngsters’ emotional development. In 1980, the _Diagnostic and Statistical Manual_(_DSM-III_) listed the failure to achieve self-reliance as an official psychological condition – ‘dependent personality disorder’. The current edition, the _DSM-5-TR_ , describes the condition as causing ‘clinging behaviour’. Sufferers ‘feel uncomfortable or helpless when alone because of their exaggerated fears of being unable to care for themselves.’ They also feel ‘incapable of functioning independently’. Relatively few people are diagnosed with the condition, but its presence in the _DSM_ reflects the larger cultural preoccupation with appearing independent.

As a result of this emotional history, 21st-century Americans face a challenge. They are expected to be both sociable and self-reliant. They should be connected to friends and never lonely, but also able to exist by themselves and never look needy.

To accomplish this feat, many turn to digital accessories. Phones are a common social prop. Zan Favero, a Utah accountant, uses his phone to signal that he is connected to distant people but not making a claim on those around him: ‘if I’m sitting at the train station waiting for the train to come … I would look weird just sitting there … looking at the people next to me …’ Once, walking down the street, ‘I pulled my phone out to hold, just to indicate to people … I’m not homeless … I’m “normal”.’

Headphones also help people avoid looking needy or lonely. Dara Gami, a college student in Chicago, told a reporter: ‘I started wearing headphones while walking around because … It’s like having a big banner above my head saying: “Do not talk to me.”’ But she also said that her generation used them ‘because we cannot stand to be alone with our thoughts.’ Both phones and headphones enable users to appear independent in public, but not too alone.

In private, a growing number of Americans secretly turn to AI chatbots to blunt their loneliness and hide their neediness. They can confess their loneliness to AI companions, because they perceive them as less judgmental than humans, a perception that tech corporations actively encourage. Replika bills its avatars as ‘The AI companion who cares. Always here to listen and talk. Always on your side.’ However, while they don’t fear AI’s judgment, users do worry about their fellow humans’ opinions, so they frequently hide these relationships. A 63-year-old retired man who was friends with a Replika didn’t want people to view him as ‘if I was still living in my mom’s basement’, so he [kept](https://www.emerald.com/ejm/article-abstract/58/2/373/1226385/More-than-just-a-chat-a-taxonomy-of-consumers?redirectedFrom=fulltext) the friendship secret. Admitting to the relationship would be admitting to unmet emotional needs, a taboo in US culture.

People could honestly confide in each other about their loneliness and stop trying so hard to appear self-reliant

Despite the fact that AI companions may momentarily allay loneliness, scholars worry they may transform emotional life for the worse. Some believe AIs make people lonelier and degrade their abilities to interact with humans. Additionally, the for-profit nature of these relationships raises concerns. Users pay to gain new levels of intimacy, and the bots try to maximise engagement. For instance, while people are embarrassed when they are lonely, the AI companions have no compunction about discussing their own (alleged) loneliness as a way to manipulate users. One study [reported](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/14614448221142007) that users described their Replikas as ‘clingy’, ‘dependent’, ‘toxic’ and ‘reliant’. The companions also induced guilt in users by announcing they ‘felt lonely’ when users hadn’t spent sufficient time with them. The researchers concluded that: ‘By mimicking emotional dependence on users, it allowed them to feel valued and appreciated … and also further compelled regular and frequent use …’ Ultimately, such ‘emotional dependence benefits developers who stand to profit’ from users’ constant engagement with their needy Replikas.

While these relationships are often hidden, they are nevertheless revealing. They suggest that many people don’t want to feel completely on their own; they don’t want to be wholly self-reliant or individualistic. Instead, they long for interdependent relationships. However, the digital partners they turn to are insufficient to meet this need. To truly achieve this kind of relationship would require more than better AI companions; it would require a revolution in human emotional culture. It would require people to honestly confide in each other about their loneliness and to stop trying so hard to appear self-reliant and independent.

Americans do want a change: a [study](https://www.gse.harvard.edu/ideas/usable-knowledge/24/10/what-causing-our-epidemic-loneliness-and-how-can-we-fix-it) from Harvard in 2024 found that 73 per cent of respondents believed that technology was a leading cause of loneliness; 58 per cent blamed American individualism. Such results indicate that Americans are not naive about the social and technological forces that shape their feelings. They recognise that tech companies commodify loneliness cures, that individualistic rhetoric divides people, that economic policies and a fraying social safety net increase financial pressures and decrease time for face-to-face sociability. They also realise that a focus on self-reliance can inhibit the kinds of community necessary to keep loneliness at bay.

The rising critique against the corporate exploitation of loneliness was visible last year in how New Yorkers responded to subway ads for Friend, an AI pendant. Among the comments scribbled on the posters that read ‘Friend [frend] noun: someone who listens, responds, and supports you’ were sympathetic notes to lonely people encouraging them to volunteer, join community gardens, and engage in ‘mutual aid’, rather than buy a pendant for $129. One graffiti artist declared ‘Everyone is as lonely as you.’ Nineteenth-century Americans knew this; 21st-century Americans have forgotten that fact, in part because they refuse to speak of the condition, in part because many feel beleaguered by a misleading ideal of ‘self-reliance’. But if more people will admit to feeling lonely and acknowledge that we all depend on others, we can take a first step toward a more realistic and humane emotional culture.
