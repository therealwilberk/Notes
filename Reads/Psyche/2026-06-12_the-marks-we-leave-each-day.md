# The marks we leave each day

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Hannah Seo  
**Date:** 2026-06-12  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/notes-to-self/what-if-we-could-see-the-marks-we-leave-throughout-the-day

---

When we are hurting, or watching someone we love hurt, most of us reach instinctively for words of comfort. Neat phrases, familiar narratives that suggest things will make sense eventually. I used to think of clichés as a kind of shared wisdom, small hopeful gestures that help fill unbearable silences. But they can also flatten what is in front of us, reducing a particular experience to something prepackaged and resolved.

Reading Yiyun Li’s memoir _Things in Nature Merely Grow_ (2025) made me more aware of this reflex, and of how little it actually offers. Writing after her second son died by suicide, as her first son had also done, Li refuses everything we usually associate with grief: stages, healing, the idea of moving forward. These, she says, are narratives that distract from the harder truth: that loss, once it enters a life, simply becomes a new fact of it.

This might be too bleak for some, but I found her words to be grounding: ‘Facts are the harshest and the hardest part of life,’ she writes, ‘and yet facts, unalterable, bring with them some order of logic.’ Without that anchor, the mind spirals into what-ifs and endless explanations or, worse, it might push us to measure ourselves against [imagined stages](https://psyche.co/ideas/grief-is-not-a-process-with-five-stages-it-is-shattered-glass) we keep failing to reach. Instead, Li borrows a phrase from the psychologist Marsha Linehan and invites those who are hurting to ‘do things that work’ – things that may have nothing to do with thinking about loss, but with the life that continues despite it. For her, it is writing. For others, whatever keeps them tethered to the living world. And for those on the comforting side: we don’t have to offer better words, presence is enough. A nourishing meal, an errand taken care of, the simple act of staying close without asking grief to be anything other than what it is.
