# Why older people are happier and what we can learn from them

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Xianmin Gong & Natalie Wong  
**Date:** 2026-06-15  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/ideas/why-older-people-are-happier-and-what-we-can-learn-from-them

---

After playing mahjong for an entire day, our retired parents came home glowing with satisfaction. They recounted the specific moves of the game and the thrill of well-executed wins. They were tired, but it was a good kind of tiredness, the kind that comes from a well-spent day. What is their secret to being so _happy_?

For many of us, achieving this momentary state of joy and wellbeing (what psychologists call ‘hedonic happiness’) is surprisingly difficult. Often, the deliberate search for happiness can even lead to less of it, an established ironic phenomenon [known](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21517168/) as the happiness paradox. It is like trying to catch a bar of wet soap: the tighter you squeeze, the faster it slips away. Yet, like many other older people, our parents seemed to find happiness effortlessly, apparently making them immune to the paradox.

In fact, [research](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00148-020-00797-z) spanning 145 countries has identified a U-shaped happiness-age curve, whereby self-reported wellbeing tends to dip in mid-adulthood and peak in old age. Despite the functional deterioration (such as health decline, cognitive impairment) and other losses that often accompany ageing, older adults generally report [being happier](https://psyche.co/notes-to-self/the-happiness-chart-that-changed-the-course-of-my-life) than do younger adults.

So, why are older adults happier? And what can we learn from them?

To understand their secret, we [propose](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/10888683251384840) the concept of intergoal compatibility. Successfully achieving happiness depends not just on finding things that make you happy and doing them, but on whether being happy and pursuing happiness fits into your current broader system of life goals.

For younger and middle-aged adults, life goals typically resemble a crowded, competitive landscape. If you are middle-aged or younger, then you are likely in the ‘accumulation phase’ of life, juggling multiple high-stakes objectives: acquiring skills, building a career, raising a family, saving for major expenses, establishing your reputation, and so on. In this phase, goals centred on growth, future security and achievement take priority. These goals are time-sensitive and demanding, requiring immense dedication of your resources (such as time, energy, focus), and they often ask you to delay gratification today for a payoff tomorrow.

As a consequence, when a younger adult engages in a purely hedonic activity (such as playing mahjong, binge-watching a TV series, or taking a long nap), it often triggers a sense of goal conflict and opportunity cost. A day spent seeking happiness can feel like a day stolen from success. You may be physically at the mahjong table, but mentally still at your office desk. This internal friction generates stress and anxiety, dampening the joy the activity was meant to produce.

When older adults imagined a medical breakthrough extending their lives, their goals shifted back toward those of younger people

For older adults, however, their hierarchy of goals has shifted. One might assume this shift is simply a result of wisdom – that older adults have ‘life figured out’ through decades of experience. While life experience certainly plays a role, research suggests a more important driver is our ‘perceived time horizon’: the years we feel we have ahead. As illustrated by what the lifespan psychologist Laura Carstensen [calls](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8599276/) the ‘socioemotional selectivity theory’, when future time feels limited as we grow older, our goals pivot away from long‑term achievement and toward emotional happiness and meaning. As a result, happiness moves to the centre of the goal system, and life is reorganised to be more compatible with happiness goals, making happiness more attainable.

This distinction between wisdom and time horizon is crucial. A landmark [study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9848799/) by Carstensen and the social psychologist [Barbara Fredrickson](https://aeon.co/users/barbara-fredrickson) published in 1998 suggested that this shift isn’t unique to old age, but can be driven by changes in the perceived time horizon. They found that when younger people (specifically, symptomatic HIV patients) faced a shortened time horizon, their goals adjusted to resemble those of older adults, prioritising emotional connection over future achievement. Conversely, when Carstensen and her colleagues in another [study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10632147/) asked healthy older adults to imagine a medical breakthrough that extended their lives by decades, their goals shifted back toward those of younger people.

This shows that the ‘secret’ to older adults’ happiness isn’t necessarily some mystical wisdom gained only through age. Rather, it is an adaptive adjustment in response to a shrinking time horizon. Because older people are more keenly aware than younger people of their time being finite, maintaining social connections and enjoying life aren’t ‘distractions’ from more important work – these activities are their ‘work’ and primary life priorities.

Based on all this, we suggest that the most important lesson we can learn from older adults about happiness is that: 1) the compatibility between happiness pursuits and other life goals matters; and 2) we can deliberately adjust our system of life goals to improve this compatibility. The question then becomes how to make these adjustments.

Given the heavy responsibilities on our plates, achieving the kind of intergoal compatibility that older adults enjoy might seem impossible. You can’t simply act retired when you have a mortgage to pay, children to feed and a career to build. However, this structural challenge is often made worse by unhelpful beliefs about what happiness is and how it works.

Many of us have internalised a zero-sum mentality, viewing happiness and ambition (for growth, achievement, and success) as enemies. This false dichotomy causes us to swing between two extremes.

Many younger adults are stuck between overwork and procrastination, never fully inhabiting either state

On one end lies the ‘grind mindset’. People in this mode prioritise ambition above all else. They view effort and stress as the fuel for success, and relaxation as the rust that corrodes it. In this mindset, we convince ourselves that we must engage in hard, continuous labour, delaying gratification indefinitely to ensure success. This may even lead to the stigmatisation of happiness pursuit as mere hedonism – a moral failing of indulging oneself in pleasure when one should be grinding.

On the other end lies the YOLO (you only live once) trap. Here, people rebel against relentless pressure by treating pleasure as the only thing that matters, neglecting the very real need for long-term achievement and meaning. This approach attempts to force happiness into a life that cannot sustainably support it, often leading to a chaotic existence where pleasure is chased to numb the anxiety of neglected responsibilities.

Both extremes miss the mark. The first leads to burnout: you might succeed in your career but find yourself miserable, depleted and, over time, potentially losing the vitality and motivation that made you successful in the first place. The second leads to a lack of purpose and a fragile foundation for the future. Many younger adults are left stuck in the middle, oscillating between overwork and guilty procrastination, never fully inhabiting either state.

We need to update these beliefs. Happiness is neither a luxury nor the enemy of ambition. A wealth of research [evidence](https://psycnet.apa.org/doiLanding?doi=10.1037%2F0033-2909.131.6.803) shows that happiness and ambition both are [foundations](https://psycnet.apa.org/doiLanding?doi=10.1037%2F0003-066X.56.3.218) of a good life, and they complement each other. Happiness builds resilience, strengthens our fitness, broadens our cognition, and makes us more productive in the long run. Conversely, making progress on our other important personal goals builds the foundation for sustainable happiness.

As well as updating your beliefs about happiness, there are also strategies you can use to resolve the friction between enjoying today and building for tomorrow. A core approach we recommend is to reframe your leisure and social activities. Take an afternoon spent relaxing or catching up with friends – if you can see these sorts of pursuits not as time-wasting but as energy-management that’s essential for your career goals, then you’ll find the guilt vanishes. This compatibility or alignment (using happiness to amplify your other life goals) transforms the pursuit of happiness from a guilty pleasure into part of a well-rounded life.

We want to acknowledge that everyone has their own unique circumstances and priorities. What works for a freelancer might not work for a corporate executive or a stay-at-home parent. Each of us needs to look carefully at our own lives to find the best ways to create this compatibility. Beyond reframing, below are some further suggestions to help you:

  * **Expand your definition of happiness.** Younger adults often equate happiness primarily with high-arousal positive emotions – excitement, thrill and intense euphoria. They may think of a wild night out, a rollercoaster, or an expensive vacation. While these experiences have value, they’re not always available and can be difficult to integrate into daily life. Another lesson we can learn from older adults is to appreciate low-arousal positive emotions such as calm, peace and contentment. These gentler forms of happiness are easier to weave into daily routines and can be just as nourishing. You can enjoy a peaceful cup of coffee while reviewing a document or find satisfaction in a tidy workspace.
  * **Choose activities that serve multiple purposes.** We often compartmentalise our lives: this hour is for work, this hour is for friends, and this hour is for health. This leads to time scarcity and goal conflict. Another effective happiness strategy is choosing activities that hit two or more targets in one go. For example, if you enjoy social interaction, consider prioritising socially oriented activities with colleagues or clients (eg, arranging a regular team dinner or forming a study group). You’re building your social network while enjoying yourself.
  * **Seek happiness that feels meaningful.** Younger adults often separate fun activities (eg, partying, gaming) from meaningful ones (eg, studying, working, volunteering). To build better alignment between happiness and meaning, look for enjoyable activities that also provide a sense of purpose. If you enjoy writing, you might start a blog that helps others, turning a pleasurable activity into something that also contributes value to the world. If you like fitness, occasionally joining a charity run can add a layer of purpose to your exercise routine. You’re not just having fun, but also building something, contributing, growing.



As we write this entry over the weekend, we feel a distinct sense of contentment. This isn’t just because the pursuit of happiness is a subject close to our hearts, but because, for the first time in a while, we don’t feel the familiar weight of burnout.

Yesterday, we took the afternoon off to visit our parents. We sat around the table, the clatter of tiles filling the room as we played mahjong, something we haven’t done in a long while. In the past, a voice in our heads would have whispered that we were wasting time, that we should be home working. But this time, we reframed the moment. We realised that the laughter we shared and the focus we poured into the game were not distractions from our work, they were fuel for it.

By allowing ourselves to be fully immersed in that joy, we gathered the energy and clarity we needed to write these words today. We don’t have to wait until we are retired to learn this lesson. We can build a life where ambition and joy don’t just coexist, but actually elevate one another. The art of being present isn’t an escape from the future. Rather, it is the foundation upon which a successful one is built.
