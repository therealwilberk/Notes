# I cognitively shuffle myself to sleep

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Amy Arthur  
**Date:** 2026-06-16  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/notes-to-self/i-couldnt-sleep-then-i-tried-cognitive-shuffling

---

I’ve never been a great sleeper, but a medication change recently left me lying awake for more than an hour each night, unable to relax. I tried the usual sleep hygiene tricks, like no caffeine after midday and avoiding screens in the 30 minutes before bed, but nothing worked.

I’d read on the sofa and start to feel sleepy, but as soon as I was in bed, my mind would wake up. I’d think about all the things on my to-do list that I would have to do in the morning, on limited sleep no less.

Intrusive thoughts and rumination, according to the cognitive scientist Luc Beaudoin, suggest to our brain’s ‘sleep-onset control system’ that we need to remain awake to solve these problems, and thus [inhibit](https://summit.sfu.ca/item/17237) sleep onset. This theory of insomnia is just one of many, and it has received limited attention from researchers outside of Beaudoin’s team. But, desperate for something, anything to help, I decided to try Beaudoin’s method for combating the mental ‘perturbance’ that was keeping me up at night.

His ‘cognitive shuffling’ [technique](https://summit.sfu.ca/item/16196) is simple: pick a random word (eg, duck) and imagine it (the duck) fully in your mind. Then, choose another random word. Imagine it. Pick another. Imagine it. Repeat until you are miraculously asleep, having signalled to your sleep-onset control system that there is nothing particularly important worth staying awake for. It’s worked for me – for the past two weeks, I’ve fallen asleep after about 15 minutes of shuffling.

Variations of cognitive shuffling involve listing items based on the alphabet – apple, beard, capybara – or choosing a random word and trying to name everything you can think of beginning with the first letter of that word, then the second, and so on. I do this latter version, and tonight I think my word will be ‘Psyche’. Potato, porcupine, pencil…
