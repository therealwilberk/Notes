# Struggling with creative work doesn’t mean you’re failing

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Chris Smith  
**Date:** 2026-06-17  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/ideas/struggling-with-creative-work-doesnt-mean-youre-failing

---

As I’ve been writing – and not writing – my next nonfiction book, I’ve also been training for my first marathon. It’s got me thinking. I’ve run the steep hills and industrial canal towpaths near my Yorkshire home since I first moved here, but last year I decided to get serious: 10Ks first, then half marathons, and now a full one.

When you train towards a race, each week gets progressively harder. The distances lengthen and the pace quickens. The runs become more gruelling. Injuries occur and, some days, your legs can feel like concrete.

Marathon training is effortful, and so is writing a book – but it’s effortful in a very different way. At the moment, the book feels murky, like being lost in the fog. Some parts I know work, others I know don’t. Some parts I have a hunch about, while others, I haven’t a clue. So, I sit with it all. And as I do, doubt creeps in. I question why I’m writing the book in the first place and whether I’m capable of ever writing it.

While training for a long run hurts, I know why it hurts. I also feel it _should_ hurt. Isn’t that the whole point? If training was a cinch, then I’d feel I wasn’t pushing myself. But the writing doesn’t feel like that one bit. Going round in circles on the book – finding it hard, feeling stuck – doesn’t seem challenging in a motivating way. It just feels bad. Like I’m doing something wrong or the project itself is a waste of time. If you’ve ever grappled with a design that won’t come together, a technical problem that resists every solution, or a piece of writing or other creative work that feels directionless, you might know this feeling too. Why does difficulty and discomfort often feel like failure when you’re engaged in work like this?

The answer lies in understanding the kind of work you’re actually doing. The Harvard Business School professor Teresa Amabile [distinguishes](https://psycnet.apa.org/record/1984-06764-001) between two kinds of tasks: algorithmic and heuristic. Algorithmic tasks follow a logical, known sequence: if you do A, B and C, you can expect D to follow. Imagine baking a cake from a recipe. Assuming you don’t drop your eggs, have the right ingredients on hand and the oven works fine, you follow the steps and produce a cake. The process is predictable and repeatable. Following those same steps will mean you produce the same cake every time. You know where you are at any point in the process, too. Following a training plan for a marathon works in much the same way. Step by step.

Heuristic tasks aren’t like that. They have no clear path. This time, let’s imagine your task is to create a _new_ kind of cake. Suddenly, there’s no recipe to follow – and what does ‘new’ mean anyway? The only way forward is [trial and error](https://psyche.co/ideas/our-greatest-invention-was-the-invention-of-invention-itself) – through experimentation. You’ll get some things right and some things wrong. You move forward in fits and starts. Some attempts will work, others will end up in the bin. At any point, you don’t necessarily know whether you’re close to a solution or moving further away from it. You might not even be sure a solution exists at all. This uncertainty is inherent to heuristic work – and it lies at the heart of creativity and innovation.

Both types of tasks can be hard. But heuristic tasks in particular, with their lack of clear feedback about progress, tend to create what psychologists call [disfluency](https://psycnet.apa.org/doiLanding?doi=10.1037%2Fpspa0000030): a discouraging feeling of cognitive effortfulness or difficulty. Training for a marathon is gruelling, but you know why it’s gruelling. Assembling flat-pack furniture might be frustrating, but if you follow the instructions and don’t lose your Allen key, you’ll get the job done. Algorithmic tasks aren’t easier than heuristic tasks, but they have a clarity. There’s a method to them that’s clear and repeatable. Whereas when you create something new, shape an argument or develop an original idea, you can work intensively and still feel lost. The goal might be clear, but the route often isn’t. Heuristic work often lacks reliable signals about whether you’re making progress or just going round in aimless circles.

This creates a problem. When effort isn’t accompanied by feedback (either bad or good), all you have is a void of uncertainty. You might struggle to interpret what the feeling of difficulty means. That’s where the misreading begins. Many of us fill that void by interpreting effortfulness itself as evidence that we’re [not good enough](https://psyche.co/guides/how-to-get-over-never-good-enough-a-practical-guide) or are getting nowhere. We don’t think that the work is simply uncertain, but rather that we’re failing.

I know this all too well. When I experience disfluency in my work, it’s hard not to assume that it’s because my idea is terrible or I’m not up to the job. And yet I also know that these periods of doubt and despair are just part of the process. I’m trying to create something new and, in doing so, I have to experiment – to get things wrong and get things right. It’s only by sticking with this slow and often painful process of iteration that I’ll reach my goal. I might feel as if I’m wasting my precious time on the planet and, no, that feeling’s not at all nice, but it’s also just a feeling. And it commonly shows up in other heuristic work, too.

While her early work had _felt_ like failure, it wasn’t: it was just part of the process

While working on her PhD, the qualitative researcher Anuja Cabraal spent weeks categorising interview transcripts, only to find that parts of the data refused to fit her framework. ‘I felt confused and lost, like I was doing it all wrong,’ she told me. Only later did she realise that this period of confusion – what she now calls ‘the golden time’ – was when she stopped forcing the data into existing categories and started inventing new ones. The mess she experienced for a while wasn’t failure; it was her thinking expanding as she created a framework the data actually needed.

Sometimes, it takes someone else to push you through a difficult stretch. In 2016, the artist Hannah Honeywill spent months hand-crafting intricate gilded frames for a commissioned piece called ‘Tumbleweed’. Despite spending hours in her studio, she felt the piece wasn’t working. She told me that the harder she worked, the more frustrated she became. When a ‘critical friend’ visited and told her bluntly ‘You need to start again,’ Honeywill felt angry and adrift. But in the end, the feedback was the catalyst she needed. She kept going with the project, rebuilding the sculpture using simple extruded plastic frames. While her early work had _felt_ like failure, it wasn’t: it was just part of the process of creating something she was happy with.

A different kind of struggle emerged for Mark Fisher, a business intelligence analyst who volunteered to create a new automated system for collecting monthly data. At the time, he thought it would be ‘a piece of cake’. But as the requirements he was given multiplied, the project’s complexity spiralled. As the deadline got closer, he felt overwhelmed. ‘At one point I thought I’d have to give up and let everyone down,’ he told me. His breakthrough came when he changed his approach and started to break the problem into small, testable chunks and devise his own creative workarounds. This new approach made sense only because of what he’d learned through all that struggle and those moments of despair. The project was completed on time and is now seen as a big success. It was painful but, looking back, he’s certain of the benefits: ‘I’m definitely better at my job because I went through that.’

Across these domains, the pattern repeats: sustained effort without clear signals of progress creates a void. The mind fills that void by treating discomfort as evidence – _if it feels this hard, something must be wrong_. Certain cognitive tendencies, such as the insight bias, help explain why this misreading is common. Research [suggests](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1364661321002369) that people overvalue ideas that arrive with a sense of sudden clarity and undervalue persistence in creative work. When a breakthrough appears fully formed – that ‘Aha!’ moment – it might feel more valid and trustworthy than a solution you’ve laboriously pieced together.

In heuristic, creative work where the path is uncertain and progress is often illegible, this sort of thinking misleads us. One might conclude that ‘This is hard because I’m failing,’ or ‘This is hard because I don’t have the talent for it,’ when the truth is: ‘This is hard because it’s uncertain.’ The feeling of difficulty becomes self-confirming: if this were the right path, wouldn’t it feel easier? Shouldn’t it feel clearer and less confusing? In short, the answer is often ‘No.’

Being stuck isn’t easy, and recognising disfluency for what it is won’t make it disappear. My book isn’t finished yet – far from it. I don’t know if the murky period I’m experiencing now will turn out to be necessary or not. But I do know that the difficulty isn’t proof that I’m failing. Knowing that difficulty isn’t a verdict can change how you relate to it. It feels bad because it’s uncertain. Experiencing difficulty doesn’t mean ‘Stop.’ It doesn’t mean push through blindly either. It means you’re somewhere in your creative pursuit or project that doesn’t offer you clear signals yet.

What changes isn’t the feeling itself, but what the feeling means to you. Once you learn to spot the pattern and recognise disfluency when it arrives, you can reinterpret effort as a sign that you’re doing something that matters to you, rather than doing something wrong. The story you tell yourself about that effort, about what it means, shapes what you do next. Experiencing disfluency will rarely be comfortable, but it can become recognisable. And for anyone making something original, that recognition can be enough to keep you going.
