# Mary McLeod Bethune reminds us: education must be fought for

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Salwa Mbarouk  
**Date:** 2026-06-22  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/ideas/mary-mcleod-bethune-reminds-us-education-must-be-fought-for

---

I came across Mary McLeod Bethune one night while putting my son to bed – or _nilikuwa namlaza mwanangu_. That’s Swahili, my mother tongue. I write exactly the way I talk, so the two languages sometimes just naturally tend to tangle together. Anyway, I was scrolling my phone in the dark.

If you look her up, the history hits you immediately. She was the 15th child in her family, but the very first one to actually be born into freedom. She’s the woman who took a dollar and 50 cents, gathered five young girls, and somehow built what is now Bethune-Cookman University out of thin air. She spent her entire life fighting a country that was dead set on keeping Black children out of classrooms.

When Bethune was 10 years old, a little white girl snatched a book out of Mary’s hands and told her she couldn’t read it because she was Black. My little boy was sleeping just next to me. I looked at him, and I had this heavy feeling in my chest that’s hard to describe, but it came down to: my son will never have a book snatched from his hands because of who he is.

_Kusoma_ – reading. The simple act of holding a book without anyone questioning whether you’re allowed to.

For most of us today, nobody really thinks about literacy – it’s just there, something we assume everyone has. But imagining this little girl picking up a book for the first time in her life, only to have it taken away moments later… _hiyo imenigusa_ – that hurts me. And that’s when I sat up. My whole focus shifted. I wasn’t doomscrolling anymore. I needed to know who she was.

When I imagined this specific moment of the book being snatched out of Bethune’s hands, I kept thinking about how confusing and painful that must have been for a 10-year-old. You’re just a kid, curious, and that very same moment someone tells you you’re not allowed, simply because of who you are. I imagined her hurt. Confused. ‘Why can’t I read it?’

That single insult ignited something inside her: this feeling of _kama unanizuia nisisome_

They tell you that your curiosity itself is forbidden. And the truth is, nothing about who she was was the problem. The problem was what other people brought to it. All of it. From the Jim Crow laws to the heavy racism bleeding into every part of her daily life.

But Bethune didn’t shrink or accept the proscription. She pushed back. She went from ‘Why can’t I?’ to ‘Actually, I will.’

I like to think that single insult ignited something inside her: this feeling of _kama unanizuia nisisome_ – if you’re stopping me from reading, then surely there must be a reason, an important one at that. A reason strong enough to make her want it even more.

The more I read about her early life, the more it became clear just how tightly her world was drawn, how few chances she was given to picture another kind of life.

Bethune was born free in 1875, but she grew up in rural South Carolina, where opportunities were very limited for poor Black people. Around her were lives already boxed in, futures already closed, so _elimu_ – reading and learning – weren’t just skills in that world. They meant something much bigger. They held power.

The power of literacy entered Bethune’s life through a small mission school five miles from her family’s cabin. It wasn’t impressive or well-funded, and it was never built with girls like her in mind as future leaders, but it was still a door. In a world where most doors were shut tight to children like her, this one wasn’t, and that mattered a lot.

She walked the miles every day to get to school while her siblings and family worked the fields. I often wonder, in the quiet of those long walks, what she was thinking. What was going through her mind, being the first in her family to learn – _kusoma_. As a small child, that must’ve felt very heavy. The immense responsibility of being the first. Excitement and eagerness, too.

Maybe Bethune dreamed of writing her own name for the first time. Reading Bible verses out loud. Perhaps she imagined reading to the elderly who couldn’t, or becoming a teacher herself – passing the gift of literacy to other young Black children. Words have power, and at a time when everything was closed to people like her, I like to think her excitement came from sensing that education could be armour. A way to change her world and the worlds of others.

Dry goods boxes and crates were converted into desks and chairs for her students

Bethune had one vision: to teach – _kusomesha_. She saw the power in education, and she wanted to give that power to other Black children in a system that was racially built against them. Jim Crow laws meant education was legally segregated – white-only schools, and almost nothing for Black children.

She arrived in Daytona Beach in September 1904 with only $1.50 and her five-year-old son Albert. Finding shelter must’ve been her first priority with a boy to care for. She found a rundown cottage to rent for $11 a month and used the only money she had for the deposit.

The cottage was _karibu na_ – close to the city dump, a place locals called Hell’s Hole. She needed supplies for the school she wanted to start. With her only money gone on a place to stay, Bethune started frequenting local dumps. It was _uokotezaji_ – scavenging – doing whatever was necessary to keep going. She took old, discarded furniture and chairs from trash piles behind hotels. Dry goods boxes and crates were converted into desks and chairs for her students. Lacking basic supplies, they made use of homemade materials – ink from mashed elderberries and pencils from charred pieces of burnt wood.

On 3 October 1904, the Daytona Literary and Industrial Training School for Negro Girls finally opened. That first day, only five girls between the ages of eight and 12 – the children of Black railroad labourers – arrived. Tuition was 50 cents.

To keep the school running, Bethune started selling sweet potato pies to the local railroad crews and hotel workers. She used the money to keep funding her school. Within two years, the enrolment had reached 250 students.

As the school grew, the threats grew with it. When the Ku Klux Klan threatened to burn her school to the ground, Bethune didn’t run or hide. One night, as dozens of Klansmen gathered outside, she stayed in the building with others and kept vigil until morning. I don’t imagine shouting or confrontation. I imagine stillness. People sitting together, alert, listening for movement, for footsteps, for the sound of fire being lit. Perhaps some prayed quietly. Perhaps others simply watched the door. The act itself was simple but defiant: they did not leave. They did not surrender; they held school through the night.

When morning came, the school was still standing. The men who had threatened it were gone. No speeches. No victory. Just the simple fact that the night had passed and the doors were still there to be opened. Classes resumed. Students returned. And in that ordinariness, a line had been drawn. This was a place that would stay.

That vigil didn’t just feel historical to me – it stirred up memories of my own childhood, and the subtle ways belonging and exclusion were taught without ever being spoken out loud.

Nobody actually sits you down to teach you this. Proximity doesn’t mean equality

Growing up back home in East Africa, we had domestic workers. It was just the norm in our communities.

It wasn’t cruelty inside my own house. My immediate family didn’t do it. But you notice things. Even as a very small kid.

The rules.

You hear the casual comments. _She is not clean. You shouldn’t share utensils with them._ You see the different outside bathrooms. Built entirely separate, just for the maids.

Nobody actually sits you down to teach you this shit. You just absorb it.

Proximity doesn’t mean equality. You learn without a single word being explicitly taught that some people are allowed inside your walls to serve.

Never to belong.

Bethune’s story matters because it reminds us that education has never been neutral or automatic; it has always been something people had to fight for, protect, and sometimes physically stand guard over. Her fight tells us that you don’t have to wait for permission, approval or ideal conditions to build. She kept building until the thing she made became harder to destroy than the forces trying to stop it.

Today, Bethune-Cookman University enrols more than 3,100 students. The school Bethune started with $1.50, five girls and absolute audacity still stands – some 120 years later.

I look at my son sleeping next to me, and I realise he rests easier because women like Mary McLeod Bethune stayed awake.
