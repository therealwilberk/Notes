# Ghosts of summers past

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Lisa Marie Basile  
**Date:** 2026-06-23  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/turning-points/my-teens-in-foster-care-were-a-puzzle-with-missing-pieces

---

It was July when I received my foster care records – hot and bright and wild. I felt a familiar pit in my stomach, a recoiling that had come to signify summer for me. At the time, I didn’t know why.  
  
The documents were sealed behind a digital, password-protected vault. A Pandora’s box, possibly, but I didn’t care. I’d spent nearly three years in foster care as a teenager, but remembered it in fragments. Perhaps paperwork – social worker documents archived by the state of New Jersey – would help where memory failed.  
  
It is strange to see the details of your own life, things a young girl might scribble into her diary, printed as fact on official letterhead. But there they were – scattered traces of a version of myself lost to time and trauma – staring back at me:  
  
I talked to my mother on the phone often during our first year apart. (_I don’t remember this.__****__I only remember feeling that she was gone._)

I wanted to see her more. (_I do remember that feeling._)

She came for Thanksgiving once. (_No recollection whatsoever.)_

I saw my brother then, too. (_I asked my brother – he says he can’t remember either._)

Foster care was like being ripped out of the film reel of my own life, mid-scene. I was 16. My brother wasn’t yet 10. Having grown up in 1990s New Jersey – before social media distractions, when we had to invent games to pass the time – we were close. We stayed in the first foster home together, dropped into a new frame that didn’t belong to us at all. Some of the stills have no discernible image.**** I can’t remember the specific details of any conversation we’d had with our 70-something-year-old hosts; I can only really remember the way it _felt_ there: empty and dark, a house that belonged to another family, another life.

Eventually, my brother and I were separated, our second homes cities apart. For a short time, my brother was in a third foster home.

The world we had known until then fell apart in slow motion. My mother and father both struggled with addiction. My father went to prison for several years. Before foster care, we’d spent years going to AA meetings in the backs of old churches and walking to methadone clinics in the early hours of the morning. My memory is spotty during these times, too. I can remember birds in the rafters at the church, but not if my mother ever spoke at the podium. Without extended family or access to reliable and consistent financial support, life for my mother – and for us – was barely tethered together**** by a series of low-wage jobs, bad boyfriends, too-small apartments, eviction warnings, homeless shelters, and changing schools. Eventually, she lost custody of us.

My husband has caught me staring at an image of my foster home on Google Maps

At times, the recall of my years in foster care is kaleidoscopic, made up of vague or half-memories. Other times, I find myself gazing into a void. But where my mind draws a blank, my body holds sensation. Each year, for instance, when spring sharpened into summer, when it got warm and sticky out, my insides recoiled. Was it fear, sadness, dread? Why did I always feel this way?

When I’d ask my brother to fill in the gaps, he usually said, _I really can’t remember._ As for my mother, she avoided or rerouted the conversation. It brought her too much pain. Still does. Selfishly or not, she’s made this boundary clear.

The frayed edges of my memories made me do strange things: I burrowed into clinical journals, to see if memory loss has affected others like me. (Foster youth, especially older foster youth, _Social Psychiatry and Psychiatric Epidemiology_ tells me, experience higher rates of PTSD – which, I also learned, is considered a memory disorder.) My husband has caught me staring at an image of my foster home on Google Maps, as if I might catch a glimpse of myself in the past, looking back from a window.

He’s seen me zooming in on the trees and doorways. ‘You’re not there anymore,’ he’d remind me. ‘You’re right here.’

Perhaps one reason these gaps bothered me so much is that I am a writer: my work depends on being able to recall and translate memories and feelings. Not just the _what_ of it all, but the _why_ ,__ the _how_ , the _interiority_. Not just the colour, but the hue. Is it blue, or is it greenish-blue? It matters. I felt split in time without all my memories. I’d make lists of burning questions: what did my brother say to me when he was taken to another foster home? Where did all my belongings go before I arrived in foster care with a bag or two? What was high school like, truly, day to day? How could I forget something as memorable and formative as high school?

In psychology, late adolescence and early adulthood is referred to as the ‘reminiscence bump’ – we remember more events from this stage of our lives than any other. But many details from those years were inaccessible to me. It felt like a constant, low-grade ache without imagery, as if the scenes wouldn’t come into focus.

Years ago, I started working on a memoir of my time in foster care, but always put it to bed. Like many writers, I feared hurting my family members. I feared no one would care. I feared being triggered by the old wound. But also, I worried about the gaps in my memory. Sitting down to write meant groping through the haze and having to embrace myself as an unreliable narrator.

Apparently, I worked at a doctor’s office. This is something I don’t remember ___at all_

I reached out to an expert, Estefana Johnson, a trauma therapist with experience working with foster youth; perhaps she could help me understand my own mind. ‘If something terrifying happens to us as children – especially when our survival or emotional security is threatened,’ she told me, ‘the brain might suppress or fragment the memory of the experience to protect us from becoming overwhelmed.’ Foster youth tend to stay in a survival state, wearing down the nervous system, and preventing the brain from consolidating experiences into organised, retrievable memories. ‘Instead, those experiences remain disjointed, like scattered puzzle pieces that don’t quite fit together,’ she explained. ‘Even into adulthood, the body may continue to suppress these memories in service of protecting us from pain.’

Hearing from her substantiated my reality, but I still wanted to know myself. I wanted answers my puzzling Google Maps ritual wasn’t going to give me. That’s when I contacted the New Jersey Department of Children and Families. What I received wasn’t the full record, just a comprehensive summary. Still, it felt like a victory. Generally, such records are strictly confidential.  
  
Here’s what else the state records told me about myself: I received glasses after I started care. (I’d never been able to remember when I started wearing glasses, my own body a sort of mystery to myself.) Apparently, I protested heartily when I was told I needed to repeat 10th grade. I struggled endlessly with math. I skipped gym class a lot. I loved writing long poems (something I don’t generally do, even today). I got into every college I applied to. I won a scholarship.

There was a list of some of my high-school jobs – which I’d mostly forgotten about. I wrote for the local newspaper. I worked at a costume shop. Apparently, I worked at a doctor’s office. This is something I don’t remember _at all_. My social worker noted that my teachers ‘liked you very much and recognised the potential you had.’ (Reading that felt like receiving love from the past, like old starlight.)

Reading that timeline, the unnameable summer beast became a little more legible

When thinking back on my time in foster care, the sorrow is distant but tidal. I mourn for aspects of girlhood I never had. I mourn for my mother’s struggle. I mourn for my brother’s stolen years. I mourn for all foster children and teens who have to grapple with a disintegrated identity. But where I tend to fixate on what was missing or lost, the records remind me of the corporeal nature of the experience, of what _was_. They serve as a witness, a written testament of my resilience, words that let the experience live outside of just my mind. I wasn’t just a girl in the system. I had desires, interests, bad eyesight. I was real.

It is through those records that I learned I’d entered my first foster home in August 2002. During June the following year, I was transferred to my second foster home. By June 2005, after I turned 18, my placement had ended.  
  
Reading that timeline, the unnameable summer beast became a little more legible. I pictured myself standing on those strange porches under a hot, lonely sun, waiting to be brought into a stranger’s home. I thought of what the trauma therapist told me about implicit memory: ‘It simply moves underground, often showing up in other ways: through emotional reactivity, chronic anxiety, or somatic discomfort with no clear origin.’

It’s July as I write this, and I feel the quiet dread even now. Summer will always gnaw at me. I feel it when the sun sets. I feel the violation of high noon shadows, of being taken and given and made to live inside a stranger’s life. Although the state – and its strangers – are no longer my keepers, I often feel that my own story was a sort of fever dream, keeping me unrooted, unknowable, fragmented. I know I will always live with, and write alongside, this veiled archive. Maybe the blanks are doing me a kindness, keeping some specific, unknown pain at bay – but everyone wants to belong to themselves, to their own story.

At least now I understand it better. I understand myself better. There are material facts, however small, that anchor me in the haze. I can write it down. I have details. I have proof. I have a few more pieces of myself. I can look back and see an origin point.
