# How to deal with relationship OCD

**Source:** Psyche  
**Author:** Marisa T Mazza  
**Date:** 2026-06-24  
**Link:** https://psyche.co/guides/how-to-deal-with-relationship-ocd

---

Anyone who has been in a romantic relationship has experienced doubt. It is natural to have doubts about even important parts of our lives from time to time, including whether a relationship is on the right track or there’s a challenge that needs to be addressed. No relationship is doubt-free. But for people who experience what’s known as relationship obsessive-compulsive disorder (ROCD), everyday moments of disconnection can feel alarming – resulting in hours or even days of distress.

For one person with ROCD, this might involve continually questioning whether a partner is truly right for them – despite years of happiness together – and searching for evidence that will extinguish the doubt. For someone else, it could be chronic, guilty fears that they’re insufficiently attracted to their partner, which prompts them to compare the partner with others and feel intense anxiety when the comparison seems at all unfavourable. A third person might worry (despite knowing better) that a long-faithful partner is cheating. They might ‘play detective’, seeking out signs of infidelity, making it difficult to be fully present with their partner. In my therapy practice, I often see clients struggling with these kinds of doubts and behaviours. They open up about the pain they’ve been carrying and how it keeps them from showing up authentically in their relationships.

If you think you might be struggling with this problem yourself, I’ll tell you a bit more about ROCD and its impact on relationships. Then, I’ll share some practical strategies for responding to these doubts and feeling more authentic and connected.

### What is ROCD?

ROCD is a term used by many therapists and patients for a particular form of [obsessive-compulsive disorder](https://psyche.co/heal/ocd) (OCD). Like any form of OCD, it involves two main parts:_obsessions_ , which are unwanted, repetitive thoughts or urges, associated with anxiety or disgust; and _compulsions_ , repetitive physical or mental acts that a person does to try to deal with the obsessions.

OCD has various subtypes – it can involve obsessions related to the fear of contamination, the fear of harming others, the fear of violating religious scruples, etc – and someone with OCD might be focused on one or more of these themes, or move between them. In the relationship subtype, a person has anxiety-inducing obsessive thoughts about their relationship or romantic partner – despite being in a committed and loving relationship – and engages in compulsions to try to decrease their anxiety and doubts.

Relationship-related _obsessions_ might include:

  * Worrying that your relationship or partner isn’t ‘the one’ or isn’t good enough.
  * Fearing that you’re missing out on something better or making a mistake.
  * Constantly questioning the relationship (for example, ‘Do I really love my partner?’, ‘Am I attracted to them enough?’ or ‘Are they cheating?’)



The _compulsions_ that follow may include:

  * Comparing the relationship or partner with others (real or fictional).
  * Scrutinising your own feelings (for example, whether feelings of attraction or being ‘in love’ seem strong enough).
  * Continuously judging or analysing a partner’s character or appearance.
  * Testing a partner’s actions or words to see if they fit some standard.
  * Mentally reviewing past interactions for possible flaws or problems.
  * Repetitively thinking about your partner or relationship in other ways to try to resolve your doubt.
  * Seeking reassurance from a partner or others (such as wanting to be told that the relationship is a good one, or that the worries are unwarranted).
  * Avoiding attractive people or romantic media.
  * Considering a breakup to end the anxiety or uncertainty.



Compulsions such as these often provide some short-term relief from the anxiety. But in the long term, they actually end up feeding the obsessions, making OCD worse and taking up more of one’s focus, time and energy. The doubt returns; compulsions make it feel better, temporarily; the doubt comes back stronger, and so on. This vicious cycle can last a lifetime for some.

Beyond being painful in itself, the obsession-compulsion cycle can disconnect a person from what they truly need and value. It’s easy to lose sight of who you really are and what is important to you when you feel suffocated by intrusive thoughts and anxiety. You might also miss out on valuable moments of connection. Even brief, spontaneous exchanges – a wink, a hand on your back, a smile, or a hug – add up to create a sense of emotional closeness in a relationship. ROCD can get in the way of that.

The good news is that there are tools to help you respond differently to doubts, overcome compulsions, and become more present and connected. The next section will guide you through some of these helpful approaches.

### Distinguish ROCD from real relationship concerns

A question I often hear from clients is: ‘How do I know if this is ROCD or a real relationship problem?’ This can be a challenging distinction to make because ROCD often mimics the doubts that people experience in incompatible relationships. Interestingly, though, if you are currently in a desperate search for a definitive answer, that urgency is often a sign of ROCD.

Here are a few things to consider:

**The energy of the doubt.** Many people with ROCD are familiar with the ‘emergency’ feeling of OCD – a high-anxiety, panicked need to know the answer _immediately_. In contrast, those facing genuine incompatibility in a relationship usually experience a more persistent, heavy sadness that lacks that frantic edge.

**The point of focus.** ROCD often fixates on minor details, such as a physical feature or awkward laugh. Even if you know, logically, that these things are not very important, you may find yourself unable to stop focusing on them. Genuine relationship issues usually centre on more significant matters, such as shared values, long-term goals, or physical and emotional safety.

**The consistency of the feelings.** OCD doubt is volatile. If you feel fairly certain about your relationship one minute and completely uncertain the next, it is likely ROCD. Those who are genuinely dissatisfied tend to have much more consistent feelings over time.

**The presence of compulsions.** Again, those with ROCD often engage in repetitive compulsions, such as seeking reassurance from friends, searching the internet or asking AI for answers, or obsessively ‘checking’ their own feelings. While anyone might ask a friend for advice about a relationship, they don’t typically do so with the repetitive, persistent intensity seen in ROCD.

### See a therapist who is experienced with OCD

Dealing with relationship doubts can be incredibly heavy, and it’s common to feel as if you have to carry that weight all by yourself. If you haven’t considered meeting with an OCD-trained therapist yet, there are a number of benefits to doing so, including:

  * **Clarity.** They can help you determine if you’re dealing with OCD, relationship problems, or both.
  * **Connection.** You don’t have to do this alone. Support makes it easier to face your fears with courage.
  * **Expertise.** If it is ROCD, a specialist will ensure you receive the evidence-based treatment you deserve.



In particular, I’ll highlight two of the evidence-based therapies that can be helpful for people with OCD. Exposure and response prevention (ERP) is focused on building tolerance for anxiety or disgust and for unwanted thoughts or urges, and it provides a formal structure to help individuals face their fears. Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT, said as one word) emphasises embracing internal experiences while building emotional resilience, which for some people results not only in lower anxiety but a more present and intentional life. ERP and ACT can be used on their own, together, or with other approaches.

A key part of ERP, _exposure_ is one of the therapeutic tools used to help people overcome OCD _._ It involves gradually, and with the support of a therapist, learning to face your fears and embrace uncertainty, rather than engaging in compulsions.

For someone with ROCD, this process may include:

  * **Imaginal exposures.** We use scripts to map out imagined worst-case scenarios and the uncertainty associated with them.
  * **Targeted triggers.** This involves noticing triggers – such as the image of an attractive person – and practising an internal script rather than engaging in the usual compulsions.
  * **In vivo (real-life) exposures.** For example, if you have an obsessive doubt about a partner, instead of pulling away and compulsively analysing your relationship, you practise turning toward your partner to ask for whatever you need, such as a hug or some time together.



Through therapeutic exposures, individuals do things that they value and might once have thought were impossible for them. This builds lasting trust in themselves and their capabilities, weakening the power of the OCD.

Finding a supportive therapist who is experienced in treating OCD and uses one of these treatment approaches – or another evidence-supported [approach](https://iocdf.org/about-ocd/ocd-treatment-guide/), such as inference-based cognitive behavioural therapy (I-CBT) – can go a long way toward helping you overcome ROCD. There are additional, supportive strategies you can use as well, and I’ll describe some of these next.

### Practise mindfulness to give yourself more flexibility

Mindfulness involves focusing on one thing at a time, such as your breathing or a particular sensation, and bringing your mind back to this focus when it wanders. Strengthening this ability through repeated practice can help you notice the obsession-compulsion loop sooner, allowing you to interrupt the cycle earlier. That interruption might involve redirecting your attention to what’s happening in the present moment outside of your obsessive thought; making a conscious choice to connect with your partner; or doing an exposure practice that you’ve learned in therapy.

There are many ways to practise mindfulness, including:

**Guided meditation** using apps such as Insight Timer or Calm that guide you through a meditation practice, or going to a local meditation centre.

**Mindful movement.** For example, a slow yoga class or mindful walking, where you pay attention to each step.

**Focus on your senses** with exercises like the 5-4-3-2-1 grounding exercise. In this exercise, you first take a slow, deep breath. Then, you acknowledge:

> _Five things you can SEE:_ look for small details you usually ignore, like a pattern on the wall, a shadow, or a leaf on a tree.

> _Four things you can TOUCH:_ notice the texture of your clothing, the cool surface of a table, or the feeling of your feet on the floor.

> _Three things you can HEAR:_ listen for sounds such as traffic in the distance, the hum of a refrigerator, or the sound of your own breathing.

> _Two things you can SMELL:_ this can be tricky, but try to notice the scent of your coffee, the air in the room, or even the laundry detergent you used to wash your clothing.

> _One thing you can TASTE:_ focus on the lingering taste of a meal, or simply notice the inside of your mouth.

**Throw yourself into the moment** by choosing one or more of the senses above. For example:__ when you brush your teeth, notice what the bristles feel like; when you are walking, notice each time your right heel hits the ground. If you are doing this with your partner, you can include them in the exercise (eg, the sound of their voice, the feel of their hand).

The duration of your mindfulness practice depends on your experience. You could start with 1-5 minutes a day of mindfulness meditation if you’re new, 10-15 minutes if you have some experience, or 20-30 minutes for more profound benefits. Consistency is key, and even a few minutes daily can bring benefits over time.

When the mind starts spiralling into ROCD thoughts, mindfulness makes it easier to redirect your focus to the here and now, letting the brain and body know you are safe and that it’s a false alarm.

### Reconnect with what matters most to you

Values are guiding principles that provide purpose and meaning. Examples include presence, kindness, connection, and courage. When fear takes over, as it often does with OCD, it can cause someone to lose touch with what matters most and focus instead on escaping or controlling the unwanted experience. Reconnecting with your values can provide the focus and motivation needed to lean into discomfort rather than respond with compulsions.

For example, if you often get stuck ‘checking’ the strength of your feelings about your partner, you might find it helpful to pivot toward your values. To illustrate, let’s say your values include _connection_ and _courage_. When you’re having obsessive thoughts and feeling anxious, you might turn to your partner to share ‘I’m feeling anxious’, in the service of connection. You might also remind yourself that being courageous can mean leaning into uncertainty, without checking your feelings, seeking reassurance or confessing your doubts. When you continue to respond in these ways over time, you learn that you _can_ ride out the compulsive urge to check your feelings, seek reassurance or confess while being connected and vulnerable – and, for many, this leads to fewer urges.

Here’s an exercise that can help you uncover what you value, in the context of your relationship:

> Close your eyes and take a few deep breaths. Then, recall a time when you felt truly connected with your partner. Visualise the scene, noticing your surroundings and the connection between you and them. Focus on what was most important to you in that moment. Notice the quality of how you were. After a few moments, try to put that quality into words. You might note, for example, that you were present, supportive or attentive. Consider how you can bring these qualities into future interactions.

### Consider talking with your partner about ROCD

Another question I often hear from clients is: ‘Should I tell my partner about my doubts?’ Explaining what you’re going through to your partner can be scary. You might worry that they’ll judge you, or that it would mean the end of the relationship. While these things _could_ happen, I would encourage you to think about why it might be a risk worth taking. For example, knowing more about OCD could allow your partner to better empathise with what you’re experiencing, and to respond in a more helpful way when you’re feeling anxious.

If you decide to talk to your partner about OCD, here are a few things to keep in mind:

**Educate.** Share what you are learning about OCD. You can explain that it’s sometimes called the ‘doubting disease’; that obsessions tend to focus on what is important to a person, such as their relationship; and that there are many subtypes of OCD, including ROCD.

**Give a personal example.** Describe an example of a specific obsession or compulsion to help them understand your experience. You do _not_ need to share every specific thought, feeling or worst-case scenario that has come to mind. The goal is not to seek reassurance from your partner that everything is fine – which can become a compulsion – but rather to seek connection and to help them understand your experience.

**Describe your treatment goals.** If you are seeing a therapist, discuss the goals of treatment – such as getting better at embracing uncertainty and emotional discomfort – and what sorts of responses your partner can provide that are aligned with what you are learning in treatment. You could also ask your therapist to facilitate a session in which you have a conversation about OCD with your partner.
