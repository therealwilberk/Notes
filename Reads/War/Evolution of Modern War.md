
# The Evolution of Modern War: From Desert Storm to Drone Swarms

*A working map of how warfare changed after the Cold War, why Ukraine and Gaza look nothing like 1991, and what's left of the rules.*

---

## Part I — The Doctrinal Arc: From RMA to the Laboratory Wars

### The promise: Desert Storm, 1991

For about a decade after the Gulf War, Western military thinking ran on one big idea: the **Revolution in Military Affairs (RMA)**. The theory said information technology, precision-guided munitions, and joined-up command-and-control had fundamentally changed warfare — that a smaller, smarter force could now beat a much larger one, fast, with minimal casualties on either side.

The evidence looked overwhelming. Iraq had the fourth-largest army in the world in 1991. The US-led coalition broke it in about six weeks, with roughly a hundred coalition combat deaths. Only 8% of bombs dropped were precision-guided, but they were responsible for the overwhelming majority of effective hits — the rest was still WWII-style area bombing. The optics, though, were what stuck: GPS-guided weapons going through windows on live TV. The Pentagon, and a lot of other militaries watching, concluded that the character of war had permanently shifted toward stand-off precision, sensors, and speed.

This is worth sitting with for a second, because it shaped twenty years of doctrine and budget decisions worldwide — and it was, in important ways, wrong, or at least radically incomplete.

### The correction: Iraq, Afghanistan, and the return of the insurgent

The RMA was a theory of *conventional* war — state armies fighting state armies in open terrain. It said almost nothing about what happens after you win the conventional phase. Iraq in 2003 demonstrated exactly that gap: the US toppled Saddam's government in three weeks using RMA-style maneuver and precision strike, then spent the next eight years fighting an insurgency that high-tech sensors and stand-off weapons were structurally bad at handling. Same story, longer timeline, in Afghanistan.

The lesson that emerged from that period (sometimes called the "counter-revolution in military affairs") was that technological superiority defeats armies, not politics. Insurgents, militias, and irregular forces don't fight you on the terms your sensors are built for. They hide among civilians, use IEDs instead of tank columns, and win by making the war too costly and too long for you to sustain politically — which is exactly what happened to Soviet doctrine in Afghanistan in the 1980s, and to American doctrine there a generation later.

By the early 2010s, "RMA" had basically disappeared from serious military literature. Not because precision and information dominance stopped mattering, but because the wars that actually kept happening — Iraq, Afghanistan, Lebanon, Libya, the fight against ISIS — were asymmetric, irregular, and resistant to the clean victories the RMA crowd had promised.

### The laboratory wars: where the next era was actually invented

While Western doctrine was stuck digesting the COIN years, three conflicts you've probably paid less attention to quietly previewed how war works *now*. None of them involved a Western army as a primary combatant, which is probably why they got under-covered relative to their importance.

**Nagorno-Karabakh, 2020 — the death of the tank column.** In 44 days, Azerbaijan dismantled Armenia's defenses using Turkish Bayraktar TB2 drones and Israeli loitering munitions, integrated with electronic warfare that blinded Armenian air defenses. Tanks, artillery, and S-300 air defense systems were destroyed by remotely piloted aircraft costing a tiny fraction of the assets they killed. This wasn't really a technological revolution so much as an *economic* one: cheap, expendable airpower had become available to a mid-tier state, and a conventional force with old-school air defense had no good answer to it. Analysts at the time called it a preview rather than an anomaly. They were right — you'll see the same dynamic, scaled up enormously, in Ukraine.

**Tigray, Ethiopia, 2020–2022 — the invisibilized war.** This is probably the deadliest conflict of the 21st century by death toll (estimates run as high as 600,000–800,000), and you almost certainly haven't thought about it much, which is itself the point. Ethiopian and allied Eritrean forces used a near-total communications blackout, blocked humanitarian access, and a media narrative that framed Tigrayans as domestic "rebels" rather than the targets of an interstate war, to keep the war off the world's radar while running a systematic campaign of massacres, mass sexual violence, ethnic cleansing, and — critically — **starvation as a deliberate weapon**. A UN-backed commission of inquiry confirmed war crimes by both sides, but accountability mechanisms were dismantled by Ethiopia after the 2022 peace deal, and the perpetrators have faced essentially no consequences. Tigray is the clearest demonstration in recent history that the modern information environment can be made to *work against* atrocity prevention just as easily as it can expose it — it depends entirely on who controls the narrative and the bandwidth.

**Yemen / the Houthis — non-state actors closing a global chokepoint.** Since late 2023, a non-state armed group controlling part of one of the world's poorest countries has used cheap drones, anti-ship ballistic missiles, and naval mines to disrupt Red Sea shipping at a scale that forced a 20-nation US-led naval coalition (Operation Prosperity Guardian) into a fight it has, by most honest assessments, not won. US Navy crews reported sustained hostile fire they hadn't experienced since World War II. The Houthis became the first non-state actor to field anti-ship ballistic missiles. The strategic upshot: a relatively poor, internationally isolated militia can now impose real economic costs on the global system, using off-the-shelf and Iranian-supplied tech, faster than a superpower coalition can suppress them.

### What these three cases have in common — and what they predicted

Pull the thread across Karabakh, Tigray, and Yemen and you get the shape of war in the 2020s before Ukraine made it famous:

1. **Precision got cheap.** You no longer need a stealth jet to kill a tank — you need a few thousand dollars of drone and someone willing to fly it close.
2. **Mass got dangerous again, but differently.** Concentrating armor or troops in the open is now a death sentence almost anywhere, against almost anyone with drones — this reverses decades of assumption that only peer states could contest the skies.
3. **Asymmetry scaled down to non-state actors.** Hezbollah, the Houthis, and various militias now have strike capabilities that, twenty years ago, only a handful of states possessed.
4. **Information control is now itself a weapon.** Tigray showed that a government can weaponize the *absence* of visibility as effectively as Ukraine has weaponized the presence of it (TikTok and Telegram footage shaping Western support).
5. **Sieges and starvation came back.** Despite being explicitly criminalized, denial of food, water, and medical access reappeared as a deliberate tactic in Tigray, and you'll see the same pattern alleged in Gaza.

## Part II — The Three Wars Defining Right Now

### Ukraine–Russia: from maneuver war to algorithmic attrition

Russia's February 2022 invasion was planned as an RMA-style decapitation strike — fast armored columns to Kyiv, regime collapse in days. It failed almost immediately against a smaller but far better-motivated and Western-armed defender, and the war reverted to something that looked, for a while, like WWI: long fixed front lines, artillery duels, trench networks.

What's changed since then is the steady replacement of human soldiers and traditional firepower with drones, at every level of the fight:

- **FPV (first-person-view) drones now account for the large majority of frontline casualties on both sides** — reporting puts Ukrainian drone units at responsibility for around 80% of Russian losses at the front. A $500 drone with a warhead taped to it is now a more reliable infantry-killer than artillery.
- **Armor has effectively been benched.** Tank crews describe their vehicles as unable to move in open ground without being "peppered" by drones within minutes — tanks now function as static, camouflaged artillery rather than maneuver weapons.
- **The "kill zone" has widened.** Both sides have pulled troop concentrations and logistics back 20–40 km from the actual front line because that whole belt is now lethal to anything that moves, day or night, thanks to drone saturation.
- **Ground robots are entering the fight.** Ukraine has been purchasing tens of thousands of unmanned ground vehicles (UGVs) for resupply, casualty evacuation, and direct attack — explicitly to substitute machines for the soldiers it can no longer easily replace, given its much smaller population relative to Russia.
- **Drone production has become a wartime industrial metric**, the way artillery shell counts were in past wars — Russian drone output reportedly jumped from roughly 140,000 units in 2023 to over a million in 2024, with plans for several million in 2025.
- **Electronic warfare is now a constant, parallel battle** — jamming, GPS-spoofing, and drone-on-drone "dogfights" where one operator deliberately crashes their drone into an enemy's to take it down.

Four years in, this is a war being fought less like Iraq 1991 and more like a continuously updated software arms race layered on top of a WWI-style static front. Western military aid drawdowns (especially the reduction in US support through 2025–2026) have pushed Ukraine further toward this drone-and-robotics-heavy model out of necessity, not preference — it's substituting machines for the artillery shells and manpower it no longer has guaranteed access to.

### Israel–Hamas: urban war, hostage diplomacy, and a ceasefire that won't quite hold

The war began on October 7, 2023, when Hamas launched a coordinated land, air, and sea assault into Israel, killing over 1,200 people and taking hostages — the deadliest single day for Jews since the Holocaust, and the trigger for an Israeli campaign into Gaza that has run, with interruptions, for nearly three years.

The shape of the fighting itself is a study in dense urban warfare against an adversary embedded in a sophisticated tunnel network beneath one of the most densely populated strips of land on Earth — a problem that doesn't have a clean precision-strike solution, because the distinction between combatant and civilian infrastructure collapses when tunnels run under hospitals, schools, and apartment blocks. By the most recent counts, Palestinian health authorities report roughly 73,000 confirmed deaths, including over 20,000 children, and more than two-thirds of Gaza's buildings damaged or destroyed.

The war has gone through several ceasefires that collapsed or stalled:

- A brief November 2023 truce focused on a hostage-prisoner exchange.
- A January–March 2025 ceasefire that again exchanged hostages and prisoners but broke down when talks for a "phase two" — a full Israeli withdrawal and permanent end to the war — failed.
- An October 2025 ceasefire, built around a 20-point US (Trump administration) peace plan, which is the one currently, technically, in effect. Under it, Israel has withdrawn from roughly half of Gaza; Hamas controls the rest. Both sides have continued intermittent strikes despite the deal — Al Jazeera's tracking through mid-2026 counted hundreds of Israeli strikes, raids, and demolitions since the ceasefire began, alongside Hamas attacks on Israeli soldiers from fighters still in tunnels on the Israeli-controlled side. As of this past week, AP reported over 1,000 Gazans killed *during* the supposed ceasefire period.
- Humanitarian aid delivery has badly lagged the plan's commitments — roughly a third of the promised truck volume has actually entered Gaza, with Israel restricting certain food categories at crossings.

The strategic core of this war — and why it's dragged on this long — is that neither side has a workable theory of "after." Israel's stated goal (the destruction of Hamas as a governing and military force) and Hamas's continued partial territorial control are not compatible with the ceasefire's stated long-term plan, and nobody has yet forced a resolution of that contradiction.

### Israel / Iran–US: shadow war goes overt, then comes back

This is the conflict you're least likely to have a clean mental model of, because most of it happened in the shadows for decades before going briefly, spectacularly overt.

Israel and Iran have been adversaries since the 1979 revolution, fighting almost entirely through proxies (Hezbollah, Hamas, Iraqi militias, the Houthis) and covert operations (assassinations, cyberattacks, sabotage) for over 40 years. That changed in 2024, when, in the context of the wider regional war kicked off by October 7, Israel and Iran exchanged **direct** strikes for the first time in their history.

That escalation peaked in June 2025 with the **Twelve-Day War**: Israel launched Operation Rising Lion on June 11–13, 2025, a major air campaign against Iran's nuclear facilities, military infrastructure, and senior personnel. Israel couldn't fully destroy the most fortified, deeply buried enrichment sites on its own, so the United States directly struck three key Iranian nuclear facilities on June 21–22. Iran retaliated against a US base in Qatar and against Israel with ballistic missiles before a ceasefire, brokered by Trump, took hold on June 24, 2025. US intelligence assessed the strikes caused severe damage that would take years for Iran to rebuild its program — though International Atomic Energy Agency inspectors have been unable to verify that independently, since they haven't been allowed back in to actually check.

The ceasefire didn't hold cleanly. Conflict reignited in **February 2026**, following the collapse of US-Iran negotiations and domestic Iranian protests against the regime, and escalated into a roughly five-week war involving Iranian strikes on US installations across Gulf states (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, Bahrain, Iraq, Oman, Jordan), attacks on oil infrastructure and shipping in the Strait of Hormuz (through which ~20% of the world's oil passes), and NATO intercepting Iranian drones near a Turkish air base. A new US-Iran ceasefire (including Israel) was reached in early April 2026, but as of this month the situation has shifted into a tense standoff over access through the Strait of Hormuz, with both sides maintaining blockades neither has actually lifted, and Iran linking the broader peace deal to an Israeli withdrawal from Lebanon.

What this conflict illustrates, structurally: **preemption against nuclear latency is now an explicit, executed strategy**, not just a doctrine on paper. Israel and the US treated "prevent Iran from finishing a nuclear weapon" as a goal worth a direct war, on a compressed timeline, accepting enormous regional escalation risk (including to global oil markets) to do it. It's also a case study in how modern "ceasefires" between great-power-adjacent actors function less like clean endings and more like pauses with renegotiable terms — Hormuz access, Lebanon withdrawal, and inspection rights are all still live bargaining chips months after the shooting nominally stopped.

## Part III — War Crimes and the Institutions That Are Supposed to Stop This

### The architecture, in brief

Four bodies of law and institutions are doing (or failing to do) the actual restraining work here:

- **The Geneva Conventions (1949)** and their additional protocols set the basic battlefield rules: distinction between combatants and civilians, proportionality (force used must be proportional to the military advantage gained), and necessity. Starvation of civilians as a method of warfare is explicitly prohibited.
- **The UN Charter** prohibits the use of force between states (Article 2(4)) except in self-defense or with Security Council authorization, and gives the **Security Council** binding enforcement power under Chapter VII — but only if none of the five permanent members (US, UK, France, Russia, China) vetoes the action.
- **The International Criminal Court (ICC)**, established by the 1998 Rome Statute, prosecutes *individuals* — not states — for war crimes, crimes against humanity, and genocide. 124 states are members. The US, Russia, China, and Israel are not.
- **The International Court of Justice (ICJ)**, the UN's main judicial body, settles disputes *between states* and can issue binding rulings, but has no police force of its own to enforce them.

### How this has actually performed

**The Security Council is structurally incapable of restraining its own permanent members or their close allies — by design.** The same five states with veto power are exactly the states most likely to be the parties (or the patrons) in the wars that matter most. Russia is fighting Ukraine and sits on the Council. The US vetoed Gaza ceasefire resolutions repeatedly through 2024, even as the ICC was simultaneously moving against Israeli leadership — the day before the Court issued its November 2024 arrest warrants, the US had vetoed another ceasefire resolution. The Council can still produce real outcomes when there's enough alignment (Resolution 2735 on a Gaza ceasefire path passed 14–0, with Russia abstaining), but it reliably stalls exactly when a permanent member's own interests are at stake.

**The ICC has real teeth, but only against people who can be physically arrested by a cooperating state.** In March 2023, the Court issued an arrest warrant for Vladimir Putin over the forced deportation of Ukrainian children — a charge Russia denies and one it's not a party to the Rome Statute for, so it ignores entirely. It's still constrained his travel: Putin skipped a BRICS summit in South Africa rather than risk a member state's arrest obligation, and Mongolia faced heavy pressure when he visited. In November 2024, the Court issued warrants for Netanyahu and former Israeli defense minister Yoav Gallant for war crimes and crimes against humanity in Gaza, plus one for a Hamas military commander. Israel, like Russia and the US, isn't a Rome Statute member, so it doesn't recognize the Court's jurisdiction — but the 124 member states technically have a binding legal obligation to arrest Netanyahu if he sets foot on their soil. In practice, that's produced diplomatic chaos rather than an arrest: Germany has avoided directly answering whether it would comply, France suggested Netanyahu might have immunity since Israel isn't a member (a position most international lawyers dispute), and the US passed legislation threatening sanctions against any ally that helped enforce the warrant, with senators calling the Court "a dangerous joke." The ICC's own president has publicly described the Court as facing coercion, sabotage, and threats from two permanent Security Council members simultaneously — which is a remarkable sentence for the head of an international court to have to say out loud.

**Tigray shows what happens when there's no media access and no great-power interest in enforcement.** A UN-backed commission of inquiry did confirm war crimes and crimes against humanity by Ethiopian, Eritrean, and Tigrayan forces, including starvation used deliberately as a weapon — but Ethiopia simply dismantled the investigative mechanisms after the 2022 peace deal, and there's been essentially no individual accountability. Unlike Gaza or Ukraine, there was no continuous, viral documentation feeding international pressure — partly because the government engineered exactly that absence through a communications blackout and a framing strategy that called it a domestic "law enforcement operation" against "rebels" rather than what outside observers increasingly characterize as a war bordering on genocide. The case for a structural lesson here: international accountability mechanisms depend heavily on sustained visibility and at least one major power caring enough to keep the pressure on — and when both are absent, the system simply doesn't activate, regardless of the scale of the atrocity.

### The pattern across all three

Strip out the specifics and a consistent shape emerges:

1. **Documentation and moral condemnation happen fast** — NGOs, UN commissions of inquiry, and journalists can establish the facts of an atrocity within months, sometimes in real time (cellphone footage of the Tigray massacre at Mahbere Dego; constant open-source tracking of Gaza strikes; satellite imagery of Ukrainian war crimes sites like Bucha).
2. **Legal accountability happens slowly, selectively, and usually only reaches individuals, not states.** The ICC can indict a head of state. It essentially never gets to actually try one, because the people with warrants out for them are protected by exactly the kind of state power the warrant was meant to check.
3. **Enforcement requires the cooperation of powers that frequently have no incentive to cooperate.** The US shields Israel. Russia shields itself and, by extension, sets precedent China and others can point to. The institutions built after 1945 (UN) and 1998 (ICC) assumed enough great-power buy-in to function as a deterrent. That buy-in has been visibly eroding in real time across exactly the conflicts you named.
4. **The conflicts least likely to get accountability are the ones with the least international visibility** — Tigray over Gaza, non-Western wars over wars involving great-power patrons, civil wars over interstate wars. The system's attention is not evenly distributed, and that unevenness is itself a vulnerability that some governments have learned to actively exploit.

### Where that leaves things

The honest summary: the legal and institutional architecture for restraining war was built for a mid-20th-century world where great powers had enough shared interest in stability to occasionally enforce rules against smaller players, and where individual leaders' personal accountability for war crimes was a genuinely novel idea worth building courts around. It was never well-designed to restrain the great powers themselves, or their closest allies, and that design flaw is exactly what's being tested — and exposed — by Ukraine, Gaza, and the Iran conflicts simultaneously, on the world's biggest stage, at the same time.

Meanwhile, the actual conduct of war has gotten cheaper, faster, more distributed (down to individuals with a few hundred dollars of drone parts), and harder to observe in cases where a government wants it hidden. The technology curve and the accountability curve are moving in opposite directions.
