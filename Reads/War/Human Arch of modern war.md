
# Who Actually Fights: The Human Architecture of Modern War

*A companion piece to "The Evolution of Modern War" — same conflicts, different lens. Not what the weapons do, but who pulls the trigger, who builds the weapon, who tells the story, and who's left holding the bill.*

---

## The four tiers of exposure

War has a chain of custody. At one end are the people who decide it happens. At the other end are people who had no vote in the matter and absorb almost all of the actual damage. Everything I wrote about doctrine and institutions in the first essay sits on top of this chain — and the chain is where the moral weight of all of it actually lives.

Four rough tiers, with the gap between "controls" and "is exposed to" widening as you move down the list:

### 1. Principals — the decision-makers

Netanyahu, Putin, Khamenei, Zelensky, Trump. The people who order strikes, sign ceasefires, and set red lines. They are, almost without exception, the furthest physically removed from the consequences of their own decisions. Netanyahu has an ICC warrant out for his arrest and is still functioning as head of government, traveling (carefully) and governing normally. Putin skipped a BRICS summit rather than risk a member state's arrest obligation — an inconvenience, not a constraint. This is the tier where legal accountability is theoretically aimed and practically almost never lands, for exactly the reasons covered in the institutions essay: state power protects its own.

### 2. Operators and builders — the people who make the war work, and who are now actively hunted for it

This is the tier where targeted killing has become explicit, routine statecraft, and where the line between "combatant" and "assassination target" has gotten genuinely blurry.

**Scientists.** Iran's nuclear program has lost a string of senior physicists and engineers to assassination over the past 15 years — Mohsen Fakhrizadeh in 2020, widely attributed to Israel and carried out by a remotely operated weapon mounted on a vehicle, and reportedly several more scientists killed in strikes during the 2025 Twelve-Day War. The logic is blunt: you can't always bomb knowledge out of someone's head, so you remove the head.

**Commanders.** Qassem Soleimani (US drone strike, Baghdad, January 2020), Hassan Nasrallah and Ibrahim Aqil and Fuad Shukr (Israeli strikes on Hezbollah's command structure, 2024), Ismail Haniyeh and Saleh al-Arouri and Mohammed Deif (Hamas), Igor Kirillov — head of Russia's chemical, biological, and nuclear defense troops, killed by a bomb in Moscow in December 2024, claimed by Ukrainian intelligence. These are "decapitation strikes" in the literal military-doctrine sense: the goal is to disrupt command and control by removing the people who hold institutional knowledge and authority, on the theory that organizations recover slowly from losing their senior leadership.

**The legal status of this is genuinely contested, not settled.** A UN special rapporteur on extrajudicial killings concluded the Soleimani strike's lawfulness was, at minimum, deeply uncertain — it hit a state official away from any battlefield, inside a third country (Iraq) that said it received no warning, which makes the self-defense justification hard to sustain under the UN Charter's actual text. Western governments barely contested the strike politically, which itself signals something: a tacit, two-decade normalization of targeted killing as legitimate "justice" rather than what it would have been called in an earlier era — assassination. The Soleimani precedent is now cited approvingly in US strategy documents as a template, which tells you the legal debate, however unresolved on paper, has been settled in practice by repetition.

**Drone pilots and remote operators** are a strange subcategory here: physically the safest people in any of these wars, and increasingly shown to carry a psychological cost that rivals or exceeds that of troops in direct combat. The mechanism isn't distance from danger — it's *intimacy* with the kill. A drone crew watches a target in high-resolution detail for hours before, during, and after a strike, in a way a soldier in a firefight never does. Studies put the share of remotely piloted aircraft operators reporting meaningful psychological consequences at somewhere around 45-50%, with elevated rates of moral injury, depression, and PTSD-spectrum symptoms compared to crewed aircraft pilots — and standard PTSD treatment, built around confronting fear responses, doesn't map well onto trauma that comes from inflicting violence with zero personal danger. The Pentagon has only just (under a 2026 defense authorization mandate) been forced to formally study this population. It took roughly two decades of large-scale drone warfare for the institution running it to officially ask the question.

### 3. Narrators — journalists, propagandists, and the new combatant category nobody fully agreed on

This is the tier that's genuinely new, or at least newly dangerous, in this set of wars.

**Gaza is now, by a wide margin, the deadliest conflict for journalists ever recorded since CPJ started tracking in 1992** — deadlier than Iraq, Vietnam, or either World War on a per-conflict basis. At least 263 journalists and media workers have been killed since October 2023, the overwhelming majority of them Palestinian. The Committee to Protect Journalists states plainly that Israel has now killed more journalists than any government it has ever tracked. Some of these deaths are almost certainly incidental to area bombing; CPJ's own investigations have found at least 10 cases they assess as deliberate targeting, with another 20 under review, and human rights groups go further. **Not one of these killings has resulted in accountability** — which connects directly back to the impunity pattern from the institutions essay: documentation happens fast, prosecution essentially never happens.

Why this matters structurally, beyond the raw death toll: international journalists have been barred from entering Gaza independently for almost the entire war except on tightly controlled military-escorted trips. That means **the only people producing first-hand documentation of the war are exactly the people being killed at the highest rate of any occupational group in the conflict** — CPJ's president put the dependency bluntly: outside reporting on Gaza is "entirely reliant" on local Palestinian journalists risking their lives to provide it. If you wanted to design a system that simultaneously maximizes the importance of a profession and the lethality of practicing it, this is close to what it would look like.

There's also a live legal argument underneath this that doesn't have a clean answer yet: some states increasingly treat propagandists and information operatives — not just frontline reporters — as legitimate military targets, on the theory that incitement or operational support for an armed group strips someone of civilian protection. Anwar al-Awlaki (a US-born al-Qaeda propagandist killed by US drone strike) is the clearest American precedent for this logic. Where the actual line sits between "journalist," "propagandist," and "combatant" is one of the genuinely unresolved questions of this era of war, and different belligerents are drawing it in self-serving and incompatible places.

### 4. Civilians — maximum exposure, zero agency

This is the tier every institution discussed in the previous essay nominally exists to protect, and the tier that consistently absorbs the largest share of the actual damage, with the least input into any decision that produced it.

Some scale, just from these specific wars: Gaza's Health Ministry counts roughly 73,000 confirmed deaths as of mid-2026, more than 20,000 of them children, against more than two-thirds of all buildings in the territory damaged or destroyed. Tigray's death toll runs as high as 600,000–800,000 by some estimates — overwhelmingly civilian, driven substantially by engineered starvation rather than direct combat. None of these people chose the war. Most of them couldn't leave it even if they wanted to — Tigray was under active siege; Gaza's population has nowhere to go, and most border crossings have stayed effectively closed throughout.

And one more wrinkle worth naming explicitly, since it's a slightly different mechanism than "civilian caught in crossfire": **the elite-discipline category.** Russia's pattern of mysterious deaths among oligarchs, bank executives, and officials since 2022 — dozens of cases, many officially ruled suicide, several involving falls from windows, several involving entire families found dead together — isn't enemy targeting in the Soleimani/Nasrallah sense. The pattern, as Ukrainian security analysts have described it, looks more like a state using fear to enforce loyalty and silence among the people who could otherwise leak information, divert capital abroad, or publicly break ranks over the war. It's an old Soviet-era tool (purges, internal terror) running through a modern lens of "tragic accidents" and a 24-hour news cycle that notices the pattern but can't pin it down. This is a fifth category that doesn't fit neatly into "controls / executes / narrates / suffers" — it's a state managing its *own* elite as a wartime control mechanism, separate from anything happening on an actual front line.

## What ties this together

Lay the tiers side by side and a few things become hard to avoid:

1. **The closer someone is to actual decision-making power, the further they are, physically, from the war's consequences.** This isn't new to history, but the gap has widened: a head of state today can run a war from total physical safety in a way that was much less true even a generation ago, while the people executing or covering that war face individually targeted lethal risk that's gone up, not down.

2. **Killing the "operator/builder" tier has become normalized faster than the law around it has been settled.** Scientists, commanders, and propagandists are now explicit targets in a way that would have generated much more sustained legal and diplomatic crisis twenty years ago. The practice outran the doctrine, and the doctrine outran the law.

3. **The "narrator" tier is where this era's war is genuinely doing something new** — making documentation itself a battlefield, where the people producing the only available record of a war are dying at rates that should be journalistically alarming on their own, with zero accountability attached, which loops directly back into the institutions-essay problem: if the people gathering the evidence are dead and nobody is prosecuted for it, the evidentiary base for any future accountability process is also being destroyed in real time.

4. **Civilians remain the largest cost center and the smallest decision-maker in every single one of these wars**, which is exactly the gap the Geneva Conventions were written to address and exactly the gap that, across Ukraine, Gaza, Tigray, and Yemen, keeps reopening anyway.

The throughline from the first essay holds here too: technology and tactics have moved fast. The people bearing the consequences — at every tier except the very top — have absorbed nearly all of that acceleration. The people at the top have not.
