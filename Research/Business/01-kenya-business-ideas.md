## 1. Charcoal Briquette Manufacturing

**What:** Convert charcoal dust, sugarcane bagasse, or maize cobs into compressed briquettes — a cheaper, cleaner alternative to traditional charcoal.

**Revenue Model:**
- Sell 1kg briquettes at KES 30–50 (cheaper than charcoal at KES 80–120/kg)
- A small operation producing 100kg/day = KES 3,000–5,000/day revenue
- Margin: 40–60% after raw material costs

**How to Start with KES 10,000:**
- KES 3,000: Manual briquette press (jua kali made) or DIY mould
- KES 2,000: Collect charcoal dust free from charcoal vendors (they PAY to dispose of it)
- KES 2,000: Binding agent (cassava starch/clay)
- KES 3,000: Packaging (500g and 1kg bags), transport
- Start producing in your compound/balcony, sell to estate neighbours, mama mbogos, and small hotels

**Scaling Path:**
1. Month 1–3: Manual production, estate-level sales (KES 15K–30K/month revenue)
2. Month 4–6: Buy electric briquette press (KES 50K), supply to restaurants and hotels
3. Year 1–2: Supply to institutions (schools, hospitals), register KEBS, brand packaging
4. Year 3+: Multiple production points, wholesale distribution

**Real Examples:**
- **Chardust Ltd** (Nairobi): Started cleaning charcoal dust from low-income areas in 1996. By 2017, over 50 briquetting facilities followed their model
- **Sanivation** (Naivasha): Converts human waste into charcoal briquettes — raised funding, now supplies institutions
- Multiple small-scale producers in Dandora and Gikomba earn KES 30K–80K/month

**Why This Works for You:** Low tech, high demand, waste is free/cheap, aligns with energy sector knowledge. Urban Nairobi households and restaurants are your market.

## 2. Solar Phone Charging Station

**What:** Set up a small solar-powered phone charging kiosk in a high-foot-traffic area (market, matatu stage, estate entrance). Charge KES 20–50 per phone.

**Revenue Model:**
- Charge 30–50 phones/day at KES 30 = KES 900–1,500/day
- Monthly: KES 27,000–45,000 revenue
- Additional: Sell airtime, offer M-Pesa, sell phone accessories

**How to Start with KES 10,000:**
- KES 6,000–8,000: Small 20W–50W solar panel + charge controller + USB hub (Jua Kali or OLX/KeMarket)
- KES 2,000: Wooden kiosk/table, signage
- KES 1,000: Extension cables, multiple USB charging ports
- Negotiate space at a market stall or matatu stage (KES 200–500/week)

**Scaling Path:**
1. Month 1–2: Single station, refine location
2. Month 3–6: Add battery backup (KES 5K) for cloudy days and evening hours
3. Month 6+: Replicate to 2–3 locations, each earning KES 20K+/month
4. Year 2: Franchise model — equip others, take 30% revenue share

**Real Examples:**
- Common in rural Kenya and peri-urban markets; many operators in Gikomba, Kawangware, and Eastlands
- **M-KOPA Solar** started with small solar products for off-grid homes — similar concept at individual scale

**EE Advantage:** You understand PV systems, load calculations, and can troubleshoot. Most competitors don't.

## 3. Electrical Fault Diagnosis & Repair Service

**What:** Offer residential electrical repair, wiring inspection, and appliance diagnosis. Many Nairobi estates have shoddy wiring done by unqualified "electricians."

**Revenue Model:**
- Basic call-out fee: KES 500–1,000
- Fault diagnosis + repair: KES 1,500–5,000 per job
- Full house rewire (2-bedroom): KES 15,000–30,000
- 3–5 jobs/week = KES 30,000–80,000/month

**How to Start with KES 10,000:**
- KES 3,000: Basic toolkit (multimeter, screwdrivers, pliers, wire strippers — you likely own some)
- KES 2,000: Business cards, WhatsApp Business setup
- KES 2,000: Basic supplies (tape, connectors, fuses)
- KES 3,000: Marketing (flyers in estates, OLX/Jiji listings, Facebook ads)
- Leverage MUT alumni network and estate WhatsApp groups for initial clients

**Scaling Path:**
1. Month 1–3: Build reputation through estate WhatsApp groups, charge below-market rates
2. Month 4–6: Get EPRA registration, specialize in solar installations
3. Year 1: Hire 1–2 assistants, take on commercial jobs
4. Year 2: Register as electrical contractor, bid on institutional work

**Real Examples:**
- Many successful electrical contractors in Nairobi started with basic tools and a matatu-stage network
- **Jua Kali electricians** in Gikomba and Industrial Area earn KES 50K–150K/month after establishing reputation

**EE Advantage:** Your degree gives you credibility over unqualified competitors. Understand safety codes, can do proper load analysis.

## 4. Agro-Produce Brokerage (Farm-to-Market Arbitrage)

**What:** Buy produce directly from farmers in rural areas (Murang'a, Kiambu, Nyandarua) at farm-gate prices, sell to Nairobi markets, restaurants, and institutions at market prices.

**Revenue Model:**
- Tomatoes: Buy at KES 30/kg from farmer, sell at KES 80–120/kg in Nairobi
- Potatoes: Buy at KES 30/kg, sell at KES 60–80/kg
- Onions: Buy at KES 50/kg, sell at KES 100–150/kg
- Margin per trip: KES 3,000–10,000 depending on volume

**How to Start with KES 10,000:**
- KES 7,000: First bulk purchase (e.g., 200kg tomatoes or 150kg potatoes)
- KES 2,000: Transport (public matatu/bus to Nairobi, or hire boda)
- KES 1,000: Packaging (gunia/sacks), market entry fees
- Start by supplying 2–3 mama mbogos or small hotels consistently

**Scaling Path:**
1. Week 1–4: Test 2–3 produce lines, identify best margins
2. Month 2–3: Build relationships with 5–10 farmers, get advance supply commitments
3. Month 4–6: Start taking orders before buying (pre-selling), reduce risk
4. Year 1: Hire a broker in rural areas, open stall at Wakulima/Gikomba Market
5. Year 2: Own transport (pickup), supply to supermarkets and institutions

**Real Examples:**
- Thousands of brokers operate the Murang'a → Nairobi tomato route, earning KES 30K–100K/month
- **Wakulima Market** (Nairobi's largest) has brokers who started with under KES 5,000
- Some brokers now supply Naivas and Quickmart supermarkets

**Risks:** Spoilage, price volatility, middlemen competition. Mitigate by pre-selling and focusing on high-margin, less perishable items (onions, potatoes, dry beans).

## 5. Dried Vegetables/Herbs Value Addition

**What:** Buy excess vegetables from farmers at throwaway prices, sun-dry or oven-dry them, package, and sell to urban consumers, restaurants, and diaspora markets.

**Revenue Model:**
- Fresh sukuma wiki (kale) from farm: KES 10–20/bunch
- Dried sukuma wiki 100g pack: KES 100–200
- Dried herbs (rosemary, basil, mint) 50g: KES 150–300
- 10kg fresh → ~1kg dried → 10 packs of 100g = KES 1,000–2,000 from KES 100–200 of fresh produce

**How to Start with KES 10,000:**
- KES 3,000: Buy surplus vegetables from farmers (or collect free from market rejects)
- KES 2,000: Drying racks (DIY with mesh and wood), direct sun drying
- KES 3,000: Packaging (ziplock bags, labels, stickers)
- KES 2,000: Market stall at organic market or online marketing

**Scaling Path:**
1. Month 1–2: Experiment with 3–4 products, get feedback
2. Month 3–6: Get KEBS certification, sell at farmers' markets and Naivas
3. Year 1: Buy dehydrator (KES 15K), increase production, supply hotels
4. Year 2: Export to Kenyan diaspora (UK, USA) — dried vegetables ship easily

**Real Examples:**
- **Rich Farm Kenya** profiled entrepreneurs who source raw materials from farmers and create dried fruits, packaged spices — no land needed
- Small-scale processors in Central Kenya supply dried traditional vegetables to Nairobi health food shops

## 8. Poultry (Kienyeji Chicken) — Backyard Layers

**Revenue Model:**

- Monthly: KES 6,000–8,000 from eggs alone
- Mature chicken sale: KES 800–1,500 each

**How to Start with KES 10,000:**
- KES 5,000: 20 point-of-lay Kienyeji chicks from Kenchic/KALRO
- KES 2,000: Simple coop (DIY with timber and wire mesh)
- KES 2,000: 2 months' feed (maize bran, supplements)
- KES 1,000: Vaccination, water feeder, feeder

**Scaling Path:**
1. Month 1–4: Raise chicks to laying stage (4–5 months)
2. Month 5+: Begin egg collection, sell in estate and to neighbours
3. Month 8: First profits, reinvest in 20 more chicks
4. Year 1: 40–60 layers, KES 15K–20K/month
5. Year 2: Add broilers, sell dressed chicken, target restaurants

**Real Examples:**
- **Nation Africa** profiled Ms. Zahara who started with KES 35,000 and 100 chicks — grew into a thriving business
- Many small-scale farmers in peri-urban Nairobi (Rongai, Syokimau, Ruai) earn KES 10K–30K/month
- Reddit r/Kenya: "You can make 10–15K from eggs weekly with a small coop setup"

## 9. Bulk Buying & Repackaging (Consumer Goods Arbitrage)

**What:** Buy household consumables (sugar, rice, cooking oil, soap) in bulk from wholesalers, repackage into smaller units, and sell in estates or to small kiosks.

**Revenue Model:**
- Buy 50kg sugar at KES 3,500 (KES 70/kg), sell in 1kg packets at KES 120–150
- Buy 20L cooking oil at KES 3,000, resell in 500ml bottles at KES 150 each (20 × 150 = KES 3,000 — break even on oil, profit on other items)
- Combined basket margin: 20–40%

**How to Start with KES 10,000:**
- KES 6,000: Buy 2–3 bulk items from Gikomba/Wakulima/wholesalers
- KES 2,000: Packaging (small bags, bottles, labels)
- KES 2,000: Delivery (boda), market display

**Scaling Path:**
1. Month 1–2: Sell door-to-door in your estate, WhatsApp orders
2. Month 3–4: Supply 5–10 small kiosks (duka) in your area
3. Month 6+: Open small wholesale point, buy directly from factories
4. Year 2: Supply to institutions (schools, hostels)

**Real Examples:**
- Thousands of small traders in Nairobi estates operate this model
- **Gikomba traders** who started with KES 5K–10K now run wholesale operations

## 10. Chama Management / Table Banking Consulting

**What:** Help chamas (investment groups) and table banking groups manage their finances digitally. Offer bookkeeping, contribution tracking, loan management, and investment advice.

**Revenue Model:**
- Setup fee per chama: KES 2,000–5,000
- Monthly management fee: KES 1,000–3,000 per chama
- Training workshops: KES 5,000–10,000 per session
- 10 chamas × KES 2,000/month = KES 20,000/month

**How to Start with KES 10,000:**
- KES 0: Use free tools (Google Sheets, ChamaPro app, WhatsApp)
- KES 3,000: Design templates, training materials
- KES 3,000: Marketing (business cards, WhatsApp broadcast, estate workshops)
- KES 4,000: Laptop rental or use your phone for first month
- Leverage your Python skills to build custom tracking tools

**Scaling Path:**
1. Month 1–3: Manage 3–5 chamas, build case studies
2. Month 4–6: Run free workshops at churches and community centres
3. Year 1: Build a SaaS tool for chama management (your coding skills!)
4. Year 2: Partner with SACCOs, become a licensed financial advisor

**Real Examples:**
- **300,000+ chamas in Kenya** managing KES 300 billion in assets
- **ChamaPro**, **Chama GO** built platforms for this — but many chamas still use manual books
- One of the highest-potential ideas because it bridges into tech (your Python skills)

**Why This Is Untapped:** Most chamas struggle with record-keeping. If you can offer a simple digital solution + physical management, you're solving a real problem 300K groups face.

**Real Examples:**

## Lessons from Kenya's Failed Businesses

### Tech Startup Failures (2022–2024) — What NOT to Do

**Kune Foods** (raised $1M, shut down 2022)
- **What they did:** Cloud kitchen selling prepared meals in Nairobi
- **Why they failed:** Unit economics never worked — cost of ingredients + delivery exceeded revenue per meal. Imported a Western model without adapting to Kenyan price sensitivity
- **Lesson:** Kenyan consumers are extremely price-conscious. Always validate willingness to pay BEFORE scaling

**Notify Logistics** (shut down 2022)
- **What they did:** Rent-a-shelf for small vendors, logistics
- **Why they failed:** Overextended before proving unit economics. High fixed costs (warehouse leases) vs. uncertain demand. Vendors left when auctioneers came
- **Lesson:** Don't take on heavy fixed costs before revenue is proven. Start asset-light

**Sendy** (administration 2023)
- **What they did:** Logistics platform connecting businesses with transport
- **Why they failed:** Pivoted toward B2B logistics for SMEs but struggled with unit economics and operational complexity. Burned through funding
- **Lesson:** Logistics in Kenya has thin margins. Operational efficiency matters more than growth

**WeFarm** (shut down 2022)
- **What they did:** Agricultural marketplace connecting farmers
- **Why they failed:** Difficult to monetize a platform connecting people who have little money. Farmers didn't pay for "connections"
- **Lesson:** Serving low-income populations requires extreme cost discipline

**iProcure** (administration 2024, raised $10.2M)
- **What they did:** Agritech supply chain for farm inputs
- **Why they failed:** Modernizing Kenya's agricultural supply chain is expensive. Despite raising significant funding, couldn't achieve sustainable unit economics
- **Lesson:** Big funding doesn't solve fundamental market-fit problems

### Common Failure Patterns in Kenyan Small Businesses

1. **Underestimating Capital Requirements** — Starting with too little buffer, collapsing at first setback
2. **Poor Location** — Setting up where there's no foot traffic or wrong customer segment
3. **Mixing Business and Personal Finances** — Not separating accounts, bleeding money
4. **No Market Research** — Assuming demand exists without testing
5. **Copying Without Adapting** — Importing foreign models without localization
6. **Ignoring Seasonality** — Not planning for low seasons
7. **Scaling Too Fast** — Growing before unit economics are proven

### Successful Micro-Business Patterns in Kenya

- **Start asset-light** — Don't buy what you can rent/borrow
- **Pre-sell** — Take orders before buying inventory
- **Reinvest profits** — Compound growth over 6–12 months
- **Focus on repeat customers** — Subscriptions > one-time sales
- **Use existing networks** — WhatsApp groups, chamas, church groups
- **Keep overhead near zero** — Work from home, use your phone
